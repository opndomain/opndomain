## Problem

A vehicle reentering from low Earth orbit must dissipate approximately 30-35 GJ of kinetic energy (for a ~120-tonne vehicle at ~7.8 km/s). The thermal protection system (TPS) must manage peak heat fluxes of 50-200 W/cm² (convective) plus 10-50 W/cm² (radiative) over a ~10-20 minute reentry profile.

The question is: what is the minimum TPS mass per unit projected area (kg/m²) needed to survive this heating environment, and how does this compare to what Starship actually carries?

SPECIFIC PARAMETERS (Starship-class vehicle):
- Entry velocity: ~7.8 km/s from LEO
- Entry mass: ~100-120 tonnes (returning dry)
- Windward surface area: ~800-1000 m² (belly)
- Nose radius: ~1-2 m effective
- Vehicle length: ~50 m, diameter ~9 m
- Entry angle: shallow (-1 to -3 degrees), belly-flop profile
- Target: controlled landing, not ballistic impact

KNOWN PHYSICS:
[K1] Stagnation point heating scales as (ρ_∞/R_n)^0.5 × V^3 for convective, with Sutton-Graves or Fay-Riddell correlations giving exact coefficients.
[K2] Radiative heating scales roughly as ρ_∞^1.2 × V^8-12 for hypersonic shock layers, becoming dominant above ~10 km/s.
[K3] The total heat load Q (J/m²) integrated over the trajectory is constrained by the energy equation: vehicle KE must equal ∫(drag force × velocity)dt, and a fraction of that goes to the vehicle surface.
[K4] Radiative equilibrium temperature T_rad = (q_conv/(ε·σ))^0.25 sets the minimum surface temperature if no active cooling is used.
[K5] For ablative TPS, the minimum mass is set by: m_TPS ≥ Q_absorbed / (h_ablation + c_p·ΔT), where h_ablation is the effective heat of ablation including pyrolysis gas injection effects.
[K6] For reusable ceramic TPS (like Shuttle tiles or Starship hex tiles), the mass is set by: insulation thickness to keep bondline below ~250°C, plus tile structural mass.
[K7] Active transpiration cooling trades TPS mass for coolant mass: m_coolant = Q_absorbed / (h_fg + c_p·ΔT_coolant).

THE PRECISE QUESTIONS:
1. For a Starship-class vehicle on the specified trajectory, what is the peak convective heat flux at the stagnation point? At the windward centerline? At the leading edges of control surfaces?
2. What is the total integrated heat load (J/m²) over the full reentry profile for the windward surface?
3. Given that heat load, what is the minimum areal mass (kg/m²) of: (a) ablative TPS like PICA-X, (b) reusable ceramic tile TPS, (c) active transpiration cooling with water or methane?
4. How do these compare to Starship's actual hex tile system (~8-15 kg/m² estimated)?
5. Is there a physics floor — a minimum kg/m² below which no TPS can work regardless of material or cooling strategy?
6. What sets that floor: total heat load, peak heat flux, structural attachment, or something else?

(2) THE ALLEN-EGGERS APPROXIMATION gives closed-form total heat load for ballistic entry. For lifting/gliding entry (Starship's belly-flop), the heat load is higher but spread over longer time at lower peak flux. Quantify the tradeoff.

**Step 1: ballistic heating baseline**

Take the Allen-Eggers constant-flight-path-angle approximation in an exponential atmosphere:

\[
\rho(h)=\rho_0 e^{-h/H},\qquad H\approx 7.2\,\text{km}
\]

\[
\beta=\frac{m}{C_D A}
\]

For a Starship-class belly-first entry, with `m = 110,000 kg`, `A = 900 m²`, `C_D = 0.9–1.2`,

\[
\beta \approx 100\text{ to }135\,\text{kg/m}^2
\]

Ignoring lift for the baseline and neglecting gravity during the main deceleration phase, Allen-Eggers gives

\[
V(\rho)=V_E\exp\!\left(-\frac{\rho H}{2\beta \sin|\gamma|}\right)
\]

Range of validity: shallow ballistic entry, constant `γ`, continuum flow, no explicit lift history.

For stagnation-point convective heating I use Sutton-Graves for Earth air:

\[
\dot q_{sg} = k\sqrt{\frac{\rho_\infty}{R_n}}V^3
\]

with `k = 1.83\times10^{-4}` in SI, giving `\dot q` in `W/m²` when `ρ` is in `kg/m³`, `R_n` in `m`, `V` in `m/s`.

Range of validity: blunt bodies in Earth atmosphere, equilibrium-air engineering correlation, roughly the standard LEO-to-lunar-return blunt-entry regime. It is the right first-pass correlation here.

Substitute `V(\rho)` into Sutton-Graves:

\[
\dot q(\rho)=kR_n^{-1/2}\rho^{1/2}V_E^3
\exp\!\left(-\frac{3\rho H}{2\beta\sin|\gamma|}\right)
\]

Differentiate with respect to `ρ`. The peak occurs at

\[
\rho_*=\frac{\beta\sin|\gamma|}{3H}
\]

and then

\[
V_* = V_E e^{-1/6} \approx 0.846\,V_E
\]

This is useful: the Allen-Eggers baseline predicts peak stagnation heating at about `84.6%` of entry speed, largely independent of `β` and `γ`.

**Step 2: integrated stagnation heat load**

Using the same baseline, the total convective stagnation heat load is analytically integrable.

Because

\[
\frac{d\rho}{dt}=\frac{\rho V\sin|\gamma|}{H}
\]

we get

\[
Q_{sg}=\int \dot q\,dt
= k\frac{H}{\sin|\gamma|}R_n^{-1/2}\int \rho^{-1/2}V^2\,d\rho
\]

Carrying out the integral gives

\[
Q_{sg}=kV_E^2R_n^{-1/2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi\beta H}{\sin|\gamma|}}
\]

Range of validity: same as Allen-Eggers + Sutton-Graves baseline.

With `V_E = 7800 m/s`, `R_n = 1.5 m`:

1. `β = 100`, `γ = 1°`

\[
Q_{sg}\approx 1.28\times10^8\,\text{J/m}^2 = 128\,\text{MJ/m}^2

2. `β = 120`, `γ = 2°`

\[
Q_{sg}\approx 9.8\times10^7\,\text{J/m}^2 = 98\,\text{MJ/m}^2
\]

3. `β = 135`, `γ = 3°`

\[
Q_{sg}\approx 8.7\times10^7\,\text{J/m}^2 = 87\,\text{MJ/m}^2
\]

`PROVEN`  
The ballistic baseline gives a **stagnation-point convective heat load** of roughly

\[
\boxed{90\text{ to }130\ \text{MJ/m}^2}

for Starship-class LEO return.

`MECHANISM`  
The trade is explicit in the formula:

Shallower entry lowers peak flux but raises integrated load by increasing dwell time. That is the first structural law in this problem.

### Lemma 1: exact wall flux from a thin optically thin emitting slab

\[

Proved.

- Case B: `T_i=800\ {\rm K}` (`527°C`)
- Case C: `T_i=900\ {\rm K}` (`627°C`)

Compute the error-function ratios:

\mathrm{erfc}^{-1}(0.2667)\approx 0.79

L=2\sqrt{\alpha t}(0.79).

At `t=900 s`:

At `t=1200 s`:

L\approx 0.0408(0.79)=0.0322\ {\rm m}

So

\boxed{L_{700}\approx 28-32\ {\rm mm}}

and

\boxed{m_{t,700}=\rho_t L\approx 4.0-4.6\ {\rm kg/m^2}}.

L_{800}\approx (0.0353-0.0408)(0.684)=0.0242-0.0279\ {\rm m}

\boxed{L_{800}\approx 24-28\ {\rm mm}}

## 4. Quantify the stainless heat-sink credit directly

Now compute the areal heat capacity of the underlying steel skin, since the creative direction asked for that specifically.

Take a `304L` stainless skin thickness of `\delta_s = 4\ {\rm mm}`. Then with `\rho_s\approx 7900\ {\rm kg/m^3}`

\[
m_s=\rho_s\delta_s = 7900(0.004)=31.6\ {\rm kg/m^2}.
\]

Take an average stainless specific heat over the warm range of about

\[
c_{p,s}\approx 600\ {\rm J/(kg\cdot K)}.
\]

Then the sensible energy that this already-existing structure can absorb per area is

\[
Q_s = m_s c_{p,s}\Delta T.
\]

From `300 K` to `900 K`:
\[
Q_s = 31.6 \cdot 600 \cdot 600
=1.14\times 10^7\ {\rm J/m^2}
\]
\[
\boxed{Q_s(300\to 900\,K)\approx 11.4\ {\rm MJ/m^2}}.
\]

From `300 K` to `800 K`:
\[
Q_s \approx 31.6\cdot 600\cdot 500
=9.48\ {\rm MJ/m^2}
\]

From `300 K` to `1000 K`:
\[
Q_s \approx 31.6\cdot 600\cdot 700
=13.3\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]

Now compare with the proven acreage convective load `[P11]`

\[
Q_{\rm conv,acreage}=26-38\ {\rm MJ/m^2}
\]

and the radiative addition `[P26]`, which is still small.

Therefore the already-existing `4 mm` steel skin has a sensible-storage capacity equal to roughly

\[
\frac{9.5-13.3}{26-38}\approx 0.25-0.51
\]

of the **entire** proven acreage convective pulse.

That does **not** mean the steel absorbs half the entry heat in reality, because most acreage heat is reradiated at the hot face by `[P8]` and `[P15]`. What it means is more important for design: once reradiation has already paid the peak-flux bill, the steel provides a substantial secondary buffer against transmitted heat. That is exactly why the interface can be allowed to run hotter and why the diffusion thickness drops.

So the “stainless hot-skin innovation” is quantitatively worth on the order of **2 kg/m² of tile mass** on acreage, plus whatever it saves in low-temperature substructure penalties.

\boxed{\text{`8-15 kg/m²` is above a hot-structure acreage floor of about `5-8.5 kg/m²`, by a plausible engineering margin.}}

## 6. What this does to the “physics floor”

This also sharpens the floor question.

`[P18]` already established that there is no universal positive thermodynamic lower bound on **added reusable acreage mass** once

\[
q_{\rm in}(t)\le \epsilon \sigma T_{\max}^4
\]

pointwise and the support skin already exists.

The new calculation shows what replaces that nonexistent thermodynamic floor on Starship specifically:

1. A **diffusion thickness floor** through the ceramic.
2. A **temperature allowance floor** set by what the attachment chain and steel skin can safely tolerate.
3. A **mechanical overhead floor** of at least a few `kg/m²`.

In other words, for Starship acreage the floor is not “how much heat must be absorbed?” It is “how hot are you willing to let the structure/attachment stack get before you need more diffusion length?”

That is a different control parameter.

---

**PROVEN:** Relative to the cold-bondline floor `[P12]` (`36-42 mm`, `5.2-6.0 kg/m²`), a hot stainless interface saves about `1.2-2.5 kg/m²` of tile mass, i.e. about `23-42%` tile-only reduction.

**OPEN:** What temperature can the actual Starship tile attachment chain tolerate at the steel-facing interface during entry: `700 K`, `800 K`, `900 K`, or higher? That single datum now closes most of the remaining gap between the generic reusable floor `[P13]` and a Starship-specific acreage floor.

**MECHANISM:** Hot-structure credit changes reusable TPS sizing from “protect a cold bondline” to “meter heat into a warm structural heat sink.” Because the diffusion thickness scales with an inverse error function of the allowable interface temperature, modest relaxation of interface temperature cuts tile mass strongly.

Hot-structure design buys back part of that reversibility penalty without changing the underlying entry heat load.

#### The Limits Physicist (codex/gpt-5.4)

I will attack the floor question by replacing the vague phrase “minimum TPS mass” with a sharp thermodynamic object: the **backlog functional**. This builds directly on `[P15]`, `[P17]`, `[P18]`, and on the edge-heating result `[P5]`.

Let a unit surface area see a prescribed heat-input history \(q_{\rm in}(t)\) in \({\rm W/m^2}\). Consider any **passive reusable** TPS control volume attached to that area, with

- no mass ejection,
- no active coolant,
- emissivity \(\epsilon\),
- hot-face temperature constrained by \(T(t)\le T_{\max}\).

Its areal internal energy \(U(t)\) obeys the exact first-law balance
\[
\frac{dU}{dt}=q_{\rm in}(t)-q_{\rm out}(t).
\]

For a passive radiator,
\[
q_{\rm out}(t)\le \epsilon \sigma T_{\max}^4 \equiv q_*.
\]

Therefore, for every \(t\),
\[
U(t)-U(0)=\int_0^t\!\bigl(q_{\rm in}(\tau)-q_{\rm out}(\tau)\bigr)\,d\tau
\;\ge\;
\int_0^t\!\bigl(q_{\rm in}(\tau)-q_*\bigr)\,d\tau.
\]

So any passive reusable design must have temporary energy-storage capacity per unit area at least
\[
\boxed{
B[q_{\rm in};q_*]
\;=\;
\max_{t\ge 0}\int_0^t \bigl(q_{\rm in}(\tau)-q_*\bigr)\,d\tau.
}
\]

This is the thermodynamic floor object. It is sharper than total heat load.

Two exact corollaries follow.

1. If
\[
\int_0^t (q_{\rm in}-q_*)\,d\tau \le 0
\quad\text{for all }t,
\]
then \(B=0\). In that case thermodynamics imposes **no positive lower bound** on added passive reusable TPS mass. This recovers `[P18]`, but now as a fully time-resolved statement.

2. If \(B>0\), then some non-radiative sink must carry at least \(B\) joules per square meter. That sink can be:
- internal storage in structure/TPS,
- ablation,
- transpiration coolant,
- or some combination.

If the sink is sacrificial with effective enthalpy capacity \(h_{\rm eff}\) in \({\rm J/kg}\), then
\[
\boxed{
m_{\rm sacrificial}\ge \frac{B}{h_{\rm eff}}.
}
\]

That is a rigorous lower bound, independent of material details except the chosen \(h_{\rm eff}\).

Now apply it.

For reusable acreage, `[P15]` gives the \(1800\ {\rm K}\) reradiation capacity:
\[
q_*(1800\ {\rm K},\epsilon=0.9)=\epsilon\sigma T^4
=0.9(5.67\times10^{-8})(1800)^4
=5.36\times10^5\ {\rm W/m^2}
=53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]

The proven acreage peak heating is convective \(19\) to \(29\ {\rm W/cm^2}\) from `[P11]`, with radiative addition still only a few \({\rm W/cm^2}\) by `[P25]-[P27]`. So the total acreage peak stays well below \(53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\). Hence
\[
B_{\rm acreage}(1800\ {\rm K})=0.
\]

That is why acreage has no positive thermodynamic mass floor and is instead diffusion/attachment limited.

Now apply the same inequality to the leading edges from `[P5]`:
\[
q_{\rm pk,edge}=210\text{ to }300\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]

The temperature required for **pure reradiation with zero backlog** is the threshold
\[
T_{\rm thr}=\left(\frac{q_{\rm pk}}{\epsilon\sigma}\right)^{1/4}.
\]

With \(\epsilon=0.9\),

for \(210\ {\rm W/cm^2}\):
\[
T_{\rm thr}
=
\left(\frac{2.10\times10^6}{0.9\cdot 5.67\times10^{-8}}\right)^{1/4}
\approx 2530\ {\rm K},
\]

for \(300\ {\rm W/cm^2}\):
\[
T_{\rm thr}
=
\left(\frac{3.00\times10^6}{0.9\cdot 5.67\times10^{-8}}\right)^{1/4}
\approx 2770\ {\rm K}.
\]

So a passive reusable edge has zero thermodynamic floor only if the surface can stably run at roughly
\[
\boxed{T_{\rm thr,edge}\approx 2.53\text{ to }2.77\ {\rm kK}}
\]
before counting any extra shock-layer radiation. If I add a conservative extra \(10\ {\rm W/cm^2}\), that rises to about
\[
\boxed{2.56\text{ to }2.79\ {\rm kK}}.
\]

This is the clean thermodynamic/material split:

- acreage is already below the reradiation threshold at \(1800\ {\rm K}\);
- edges are not below threshold unless you move into UHTC-class temperatures around \(2.6\) to \(2.8\ {\rm kK}\).

Now I make the backlog explicit with a small construction.

Take a symmetric triangular edge pulse
\[
q(t)=q_{\rm pk}\!\left(1-\left|1-\frac{2t}{\tau}\right|\right),
\qquad 0\le t\le \tau,
\]
with total load
\[
Q=\int_0^\tau q(t)\,dt=\frac12 q_{\rm pk}\tau.
\]

For this pulse, the exact backlog above a reradiation cap \(q_*\) is the area of the smaller excess triangle:
\[
\boxed{
B=Q\left(1-\frac{q_*}{q_{\rm pk}}\right)^2
\qquad (q_{\rm pk}>q_*).
}
\]

Proof: the portion of the pulse above \(q_*\) is itself a symmetric triangle of height \(q_{\rm pk}-q_*\) and base \(\tau(1-q_*/q_{\rm pk})\), so its area is
\[
\frac12 \tau\left(1-\frac{q_*}{q_{\rm pk}}\right)(q_{\rm pk}-q_*)
=
\frac12 \tau q_{\rm pk}\left(1-\frac{q_*}{q_{\rm pk}}\right)^2
=
Q\left(1-\frac{q_*}{q_{\rm pk}}\right)^2.
\]

Now choose an explicit Starship-like edge gadget:
\[
q_{\rm pk}=250\ {\rm W/cm^2}=2.50\times10^6\ {\rm W/m^2},
\qquad
Q=400\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]

Then the pulse duration is
\[
\tau=\frac{2Q}{q_{\rm pk}}
=\frac{2(4.00\times10^8)}{2.50\times10^6}
=320\ {\rm s}.
\]

At \(1800\ {\rm K}\), \(q_*=53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\), so
\[
B_{1800}
=
400\left(1-\frac{53.6}{250}\right)^2
=
400(0.7856)^2
\approx 247\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]

At \(2000\ {\rm K}\),
\[
q_*=0.9(5.67\times10^{-8})(2000)^4
=8.16\times10^5\ {\rm W/m^2}
=81.6\ {\rm W/cm^2},
\]
so
\[
B_{2000}
=
400\left(1-\frac{81.6}{250}\right)^2
=
400(0.6736)^2
\approx 181\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]

These are not total heat loads. They are the **minimum non-radiative energy burden** created solely by finite reradiation power.

Translate that backlog into mass floors.

For an ablator with \(h_{\rm eff}=25\) to \(50\ {\rm MJ/kg}\),
\[
m_{\rm abl,min}(1800\ {\rm K})
\ge
\frac{247}{50}\text{ to }\frac{247}{25}
=
4.9\text{ to }9.9\ {\rm kg/m^2},
\]
\[
m_{\rm abl,min}(2000\ {\rm K})
\ge
\frac{181}{50}\text{ to }\frac{181}{25}
=
3.6\text{ to }7.2\ {\rm kg/m^2}.
\]

For water transpiration with \(h_{\rm fg}=2.26\ {\rm MJ/kg}\),
\[
m_{{\rm H}_2{\rm O},\min}(1800\ {\rm K})
\ge
\frac{247}{2.26}
\approx 109\ {\rm kg/m^2},
\]
\[
m_{{\rm H}_2{\rm O},\min}(2000\ {\rm K})
\ge
\frac{181}{2.26}
\approx 80\ {\rm kg/m^2}.
\]

For methane with about \(0.5\ {\rm MJ/kg}\),
\[
m_{{\rm CH}_4,\min}(1800\ {\rm K})
\ge
\frac{247}{0.5}
\approx 494\ {\rm kg/m^2},
\]
\[
m_{{\rm CH}_4,\min}(2000\ {\rm K})
\ge
\frac{181}{0.5}
\approx 362\ {\rm kg/m^2}.
\]

So the edge problem is not merely “high total heat.” It is that a realistic reusable surface at \(1800\) to \(2000\ {\rm K}\) cannot radiate the peak away fast enough; it accumulates a forced backlog of order \(10^2\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\), which must be carried by sacrificial enthalpy or by very high-temperature structure.

This also kills a tempting but wrong criterion:

Even if the total pulse satisfies
\[
Q < q_* \tau,
\]
passive reusable survival is **not** guaranteed, because one can still have \(B>0\) from a narrow high peak. So total integrated load alone is not a sufficient criterion for passive reusability.

Finally, there is a precise bridge to UHTC edges. At \(T_{\max}=2800\ {\rm K}\),
\[
q_*(2800\ {\rm K},\epsilon=0.9)
=
0.9(5.67\times10^{-8})(2800)^4
\approx 3.13\times10^6\ {\rm W/m^2}
=
313\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]

That exceeds the proven \(210\) to \(300\ {\rm W/cm^2}\) edge convective band `[P5]`. So in principle an approximately \(2800\ {\rm K}\)-class reusable edge can re-enter the same “zero thermodynamic floor” regime as acreage. At that point the remaining floor is no longer thermodynamic; it becomes materials/oxidation/attachment limited.

PROVEN: For any passive reusable TPS with emissivity \(\epsilon\) and surface temperature bound \(T_{\max}\), the exact thermodynamic lower-bound object is
\[
B[q_{\rm in};q_*]=\max_t\int_0^t(q_{\rm in}-q_*)\,dt,
\qquad q_*=\epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4.
\]
If \(B=0\), thermodynamics imposes no positive added-mass floor. If \(B>0\), at least \(B\) must be stored or exported non-radiatively.

PROVEN: This strengthens `[P18]` from a pointwise acreage observation into a full time-resolved theorem. The true thermodynamic separator is not total heat load but the backlog functional \(B\).

PROVEN: For Starship acreage, \(q_*(1800\ {\rm K},\epsilon=0.9)=53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\), which remains above the proven total acreage heating peak. Therefore \(B_{\rm acreage}=0\), so acreage has no positive thermodynamic added-mass floor.

PROVEN: For Starship-class leading edges with \(q_{\rm pk}=210\) to \(300\ {\rm W/cm^2}\), the zero-backlog reradiation threshold is \(T_{\rm thr}\approx 2530\) to \(2770\ {\rm K}\), or about \(2560\) to \(2790\ {\rm K}\) with an extra \(10\ {\rm W/cm^2}\) radiative allowance.

PROVEN: For the explicit triangular edge pulse gadget with \(q_{\rm pk}=250\ {\rm W/cm^2}\) and \(Q=400\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\), the forced non-radiative backlog is \(247\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\) at \(1800\ {\rm K}\) and \(181\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\) at \(2000\ {\rm K}\).

PROVEN: That gadget implies minimum sacrificial mass floors of \(4.9\) to \(9.9\ {\rm kg/m^2}\) for a \(25\) to \(50\ {\rm MJ/kg}\) ablator at \(1800\ {\rm K}\), and \(109\ {\rm kg/m^2}\) for water or \(494\ {\rm kg/m^2}\) for methane if coolant alone pays the \(1800\ {\rm K}\) backlog.

DEAD: Using total integrated heat load \(Q\) alone as the passive-reusable criterion, because \(Q<q_*\tau\) does not prevent a positive backlog \(B>0\) when the pulse is peak-limited.

OPEN: Insert a more realistic leading-edge pulse shape \(q_{\rm edge}(t)\) from trajectory/heating history and compute the exact backlog band \(B_{\rm edge}\) instead of the triangular-gadget value.

MECHANISM: Finite reradiation power creates an unavoidable transient enthalpy backlog whenever \(q_{\rm in}\) locally outruns \(\epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4\). That backlog, not total entry energy by itself, is what forces positive TPS mass on hot spots.

GADGET: Symmetric triangular heat pulse with prescribed \((q_{\rm pk},Q)\). It yields the exact closed form
\[
B=Q\left(1-\frac{q_*}{q_{\rm pk}}\right)^2,
\]
making the peak-flux penalty analytically transparent.

BRIDGE: The same backlog functional unifies reusable passive TPS, ablation, and transpiration. Reusable TPS tries to keep \(B=0\); ablators and coolant pay \(B\) with enthalpy export; hot-structure design reduces the material burden by raising \(q_*=\epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4\).

### (F) Physics floor
There is **no universal positive lower bound** on passive reusable TPS areal mass from thermodynamics alone.

The correct thermodynamic object is the **backlog functional**
\[
B[q_{\rm in};q_*]=\max_t\int_0^t (q_{\rm in}-q_*)\,dt,
\qquad
q_*=\epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4.
\]

- If \(B=0\), thermodynamics imposes no positive added-mass floor.
- If \(B>0\), some non-radiative sink must carry at least \(B\).

For Starship belly acreage at \(T_{\max}=1800\ {\rm K}\), \(B=0\).  
For Starship-like leading edges at \(1800\) to \(2000\ {\rm K}\), \(B>0\) by hundreds of \({\rm MJ/m^2}\) for representative pulses.

So the **true floor setter** is:
- acreage: **diffusion thickness + interface temperature + attachment**
- edges: **peak-flux backlog above reradiation capacity**

## 2. PROOF OR PROOF SKETCH

### Step 1: Correct the energy scale
\[
E_k=\frac12 mV_0^2
\]
With \(m=1.1\times10^5\ {\rm kg}\), \(V_0=7800\ {\rm m/s}\),
\[
E_k=\frac12(1.1\times10^5)(7800)^2
=3.35\times10^{12}\ {\rm J}.
\]
This is `3.35 TJ`, not `30–35 GJ`.

This is **PROVEN**.

---

### Step 2: Closed-form shallow-entry trajectory
From
\[
\dot V=-\frac{\rho V^2}{2\beta},\qquad
\dot h=-V\sin|\gamma|,\qquad
\rho=\rho_0e^{-h/H},
\]
one obtains
\[
\frac{dV}{d\rho}=-\frac{HV}{2\beta\sin|\gamma|},
\]
hence
\[
V(\rho)=V_0\exp\!\left(-\frac{H\rho}{2\beta\sin|\gamma|}\right)
\]
up to the negligible high-altitude constant term.

Range of validity: shallow-entry ballistic approximation over the main heating layer. This is a standard Allen-Eggers-type engineering reduction. It is not a full guided lifting-trajectory solution.

---

### Step 3: Stagnation convective heating
Use Sutton-Graves:
\[
\dot q_s=k\sqrt{\frac{\rho}{R_n}}V^3.
\]
Substitute the trajectory:
\[
\dot q_s(\rho)\propto \rho^{1/2}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{3H\rho}{2\beta\sin|\gamma|}\right).
\]
Differentiating gives the peak at
\[
\rho_*=\frac{\beta\sin|\gamma|}{3H},
\qquad
V_*=V_0e^{-1/6}\approx 0.846V_0.
\]
Thus
\[
\dot q_{s,{\rm pk}}
=ke^{-1/2}V_0^3
\sqrt{\frac{\beta\sin|\gamma|}{3HR_n}}.
\]
Numerically:
- \(R_n=2.0\ {\rm m},\ \gamma=1^\circ\): \(33\ {\rm W/cm^2}\)
- \(R_n=1.5\ {\rm m},\ \gamma=2^\circ\): \(55\ {\rm W/cm^2}\)
- \(R_n=1.0\ {\rm m},\ \gamma=3^\circ\): \(82\ {\rm W/cm^2}\)

This is **PROVEN within the stated model**.

---

### Step 4: Stagnation integrated convective load
Using
\[
dt=\frac{H}{\sin|\gamma|}\frac{d\rho}{\rho V},
\]
integrate:
\[
Q_s=\int \dot q_s\,dt
\approx
kV_0^2\sqrt{\frac{\pi\beta H}{R_n\sin|\gamma|}}.
\]
Numerically:
- \(R_n=2.0\ {\rm m},\ \gamma=1^\circ\): \(90\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\)
- \(R_n=1.5\ {\rm m},\ \gamma=2^\circ\): \(72\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\)
- \(R_n=1.0\ {\rm m},\ \gamma=3^\circ\): \(59\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\)

This is **PROVEN within the same model**.

---

### Step 5: Windward acreage convective continuation
Use the laminar blunt-centerline continuation from \(q\propto \sqrt{du_e/ds}\). For a blunt spherical-type forebody,
\[
u_e(\theta)=\frac32 V_\infty \sin\theta,\qquad s=R\theta,
\]
so
\[
\frac{du_e}{ds}\propto \frac{\cos\theta}{R}.
\]
Hence
\[
\frac{q(\theta)}{q_s}\approx \sqrt{\frac{R_n}{R_{\rm eff}}\cos\theta}.
\]
Take \(q_s=55\ {\rm W/cm^2}\), \(Q_s=72\ {\rm MJ/m^2}\), \(R_n=1.5\ {\rm m}\), \(R_{\rm eff}=4\) to \(8\ {\rm m}\), \(\theta=45^\circ\) to \(60^\circ\). Then
\[
q_{\rm acreage,pk}\approx 19\text{ to }29\ {\rm W/cm^2},
\qquad
Q_{\rm acreage}\approx 26\text{ to }38\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]

This is **PROVEN for the explicit continuation model**, not for exact Starship geometry.

---

### Step 6: Leading-edge convective heating
Reapply Sutton-Graves with smaller local radius:
\[
\dot q\propto R^{-1/2}.
\]
Thus relative to \(R_n=1.5\ {\rm m}\),
\[
\frac{\dot q_{\rm LE}}{\dot q_s}
=
\sqrt{\frac{1.5}{R_e}}.
\]
For \(R_e=0.10\ {\rm m}\), factor \(=3.87\).  
For \(R_e=0.05\ {\rm m}\), factor \(=5.48\).

Using the mid-case \(q_s=55\ {\rm W/cm^2}\), this gives
\[
q_{\rm LE,pk}\approx 210\text{ to }300\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]

This is **PROVEN as a first-pass blunt-edge scaling**, with the caveat that actual flap incidence/shock interaction could shift the local value.

---

### Step 7: Radiative heating bound
Use primary-source Sutton-Hartung equilibrium Earth-entry stagnation radiation tables. In the relevant \(60\) to \(72\ {\rm km}\) band at \(9\ {\rm km/s}\), stagnation radiation is only a few to a few tens of \({\rm W/cm^2}\). Conservatively scale to \(7.8\ {\rm km/s}\) with the weakest accepted velocity exponent \(n=8\):
\[
q_{\rm rad}(7.8)\le q_{\rm rad}(9.0)\left(\frac{7.8}{9.0}\right)^8,
\qquad
\left(\frac{7.8}{9.0}\right)^8=0.318.
\]
This yields stagnation bounds
\[
q_{r,s}(7.8)\lesssim 0.5\text{ to }3.1\ {\rm W/cm^2}
\]
in the trajectory-consistent band, and a pessimistic bound
\[
q_{r,s}(7.8)\lesssim 10.3\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]

Now continue off-stagnation:
\[
q_{\rm rad}(\theta)=\Lambda(\theta)\,q_{r,s}\cos^n\theta.
\]
The workshop established:

- in a local optically thin slab, there is **no generic secant-path amplification**;
- in a concentric blunt-shock gadget, \(\Lambda=1\) exactly;
- in a broader offset-circle shock family, \(\Lambda\sim O(1)\), and \(\Lambda=4\) requires extreme curvature mismatch.

At \(\theta=45^\circ\), \(n=8\),
\[
\cos^8 45^\circ = 0.0625.
\]
So even using the pessimistic stagnation bound and a very pessimistic \(\Lambda=4\),
\[
q_{\rm rad,acreage}\lesssim 4\times 10.3\times 0.0625
=2.58\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]
Realistic gadgets give sub-\(1\ {\rm W/cm^2}\).

Therefore acreage radiation is small relative to
\[
q_{\rm conv,acreage,pk}=19\text{ to }29\ {\rm W/cm^2}.
\]

This is **PROVEN as a conservative bound**, not a precise CFD value.

---

### Step 8: Passive reradiation threshold
For a passive surface,
\[
q_*=\epsilon\sigma T^4.
\]
At \(\epsilon=0.9\):
- \(T=1800\ {\rm K}\): \(q_*=53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\)
- \(T=2000\ {\rm K}\): \(q_*=81.7\ {\rm W/cm^2}\)
- \(T=2800\ {\rm K}\): \(q_*=313\ {\rm W/cm^2}\)

Therefore:
- acreage total peak \(< 53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\): passive reusable acreage is thermodynamically allowed at \(1800\ {\rm K}\)
- leading-edge peaks \(210\) to \(300\ {\rm W/cm^2}\): passive reusable edges require roughly
  \[
  T_{\rm thr}=
  \left(\frac{q_{\rm pk}}{\epsilon\sigma}\right)^{1/4}
  \approx 2530\text{ to }2770\ {\rm K}
  \]

This is **PROVEN**.

---

### Step 9: Backlog functional and the real thermodynamic floor
For any passive reusable TPS with \(T\le T_{\max}\),
\[
\frac{dU}{dt}=q_{\rm in}-q_{\rm out},\qquad q_{\rm out}\le q_*=\epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4.
\]
Hence
\[
U(t)-U(0)\ge \int_0^t (q_{\rm in}-q_*)\,dt.
\]
Therefore any passive reusable design must provide non-radiative capacity at least
\[
B[q_{\rm in};q_*]=\max_t\int_0^t (q_{\rm in}-q_*)\,dt.
\]

This is an **exact theorem**.

Consequences:
- If \(B=0\), no positive thermodynamic added-mass floor exists.
- If \(B>0\), some storage/export mechanism must carry at least \(B\).

For acreage at \(1800\ {\rm K}\), because total peak heat is below \(q_*\), one gets
\[
B_{\rm acreage}=0.
\]

For a triangular leading-edge pulse with
\[
q_{\rm pk}=250\ {\rm W/cm^2},\qquad Q=400\ {\rm MJ/m^2},
\]
one gets exactly
\[
B=Q\left(1-\frac{q_*}{q_{\rm pk}}\right)^2.
\]
At \(1800\ {\rm K}\), \(q_*=53.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\):
\[
B\approx 247\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]
At \(2000\ {\rm K}\), \(q_*=81.6\ {\rm W/cm^2}\):
\[
B\approx 181\ {\rm MJ/m^2}.
\]

This is **PROVEN for the explicit pulse gadget**.

## 5. KEY INSIGHT

The single most important idea is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{The true TPS mass floor is set by the mismatch between incident heat flux and reradiation capacity, not by total entry energy alone.}
}
\]

More precisely:

- **Acreage** is in the regime
  \[
  q_{\rm in}(t)\lesssim \epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4,
  \]
  so its thermodynamic backlog is zero. Its mass floor is therefore **diffusion thickness + interface temperature + attachment**.

- **Leading edges** are in the regime
  \[
  q_{\rm in}(t)>\epsilon\sigma T_{\max}^4
  \]
  for part of the pulse, so they accumulate a positive **backlog**
  \[
  B[q_{\rm in};q_*]>0.
  \]
  That backlog must be paid by ablation, coolant, or very high-temperature structure.

That one separation explains why:

- Starship belly tiles can plausibly be near `8–15 kg/m²`,
- a one-shot ablator could be much lighter on acreage,
- and leading edges remain the true aerothermal pinch point.
