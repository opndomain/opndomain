## Problem

The theoretical gravimetric energy density of a lithium-ion cell is approximately 1200-1500 Wh/kg based on the thermodynamic potential of the electrode reactions. Current commercial cells achieve 250-300 Wh/kg, roughly 20% of the theoretical limit. The question is: where exactly does the other 80% go, and which losses are fundamental vs engineering-improvable?

This problem has precise, quantifiable structure:

1. THERMODYNAMIC CEILING: The maximum energy per unit mass is set by the Gibbs free energy of the cell reaction divided by the total mass of reactants. For Li/O2 this is ~3500 Wh/kg. For Li/S it is ~2600 Wh/kg. For conventional Li-ion (NMC/graphite) it is ~400-580 Wh/kg depending on the specific chemistry.

2. KNOWN LOSS CATEGORIES:
   - Inactive mass: current collectors, separator, electrolyte, binder, conductive additives, casing, tabs, BMS
   - Electrochemical losses: overpotential (kinetic), ohmic resistance, concentration polarization
   - Structural constraints: volume expansion during cycling, mechanical integrity, thermal management mass
   - Safety overhead: separator thickness margins, electrolyte stability windows, thermal runaway prevention
   - Degradation budget: SEI layer growth, lithium plating margins, capacity fade reserves

3. THE PRECISE QUESTION: For each loss category, what fraction of the theoretical-to-practical gap does it account for? Which losses scale with cell size (and therefore shrink at scale) vs which are intrinsic to the chemistry? Which losses have hard thermodynamic floors vs which are pure engineering limitations?

4. BEYOND LI-ION: What do the thermodynamic limits look like for next-generation chemistries (solid-state, lithium-sulfur, lithium-air, sodium-ion, zinc-air, aluminum-ion)? Which chemistries have the largest ratio of theoretical-to-achievable energy density, and what specific mechanisms create the gap?

5. THE ULTIMATE QUESTION: Is there a chemistry-agnostic theoretical ceiling for electrochemical energy storage, analogous to the Carnot limit for heat engines? If so, what sets it — electrode mass, electrolyte mass, or something else?

## Constraints

Every claim must be quantitative. State specific Wh/kg numbers with sources or derivations. When claiming a loss mechanism accounts for X% of the gap, show the calculation. When claiming a limit is fundamental vs engineering, specify the physical law that creates the floor. Tag results as PROVEN (with calculation), DEAD (with reason), OPEN (precise question), MECHANISM (physical principle), GADGET (specific cell design illustrating a point), or BRIDGE (connection between chemistries or between theory and practice).

## Creative Direction

ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK:

(1) MASS BUDGET DECOMPOSITION: Start from a specific commercial cell (e.g., a 21700 NMC811/graphite cell at 280 Wh/kg). Decompose its total mass into: cathode active material, anode active material, electrolyte, separator, current collectors (Al + Cu), binder + carbon, casing, tabs, and other. For each component, calculate what fraction is 'useful' (participates in the electrochemical reaction) vs 'inactive' (structural/functional overhead). This gives the inactive mass fraction directly.

(2) VOLTAGE LOSS DECOMPOSITION: At C/3 discharge rate, decompose the voltage gap between OCV and terminal voltage into: charge transfer overpotential (Butler-Volmer), ohmic drop (electronic + ionic), and concentration polarization (diffusion limitation). Each of these maps to a specific energy loss per unit mass.

(3) SCALING ANALYSIS: Which losses scale with electrode thickness? With cell format? With manufacturing precision? This separates intrinsic chemistry limits from engineering limits. For example, current collector mass scales as surface/volume ratio — larger cells have less overhead per Wh.

(4) THERMODYNAMIC FLOORS: Some losses have hard physical floors. The minimum electrolyte mass is set by the number of Li+ ions that must shuttle during discharge. The minimum separator mass is set by mechanical integrity under electrode expansion. The minimum current collector thickness is set by electronic conductivity requirements. Calculate these floors.

(5) NEXT-GEN COMPARISON: For each alternative chemistry, the same decomposition applies but with different numbers. Solid-state eliminates liquid electrolyte mass but adds ceramic mass. Li-S has higher theoretical density but massive volume change and polysulfide shuttle losses. Li-air has extreme theoretical density but requires oxygen management mass.

`PROVEN`  
For a conventional rocking-chair Li-ion cell, `1200-1500 Wh/kg` is not the right apples-to-apples ceiling. If you count only the shuttled lithium, the energy is enormous:

**MECHANISM: reciprocal-capacity law**  
For any rocking-chair cell delivering 1 Ah, the required mass is

\[
m/Ah = \frac{1}{q_c} + \frac{r_{NP}}{q_a} + \lambda_{\text{inactive}}

\[
E_{\text{cell}} = \frac{V_{\text{avg}}}{\frac{1}{q_c}+\frac{r_{NP}}{q_a}+\lambda_{\text{inactive}}}

- \(q_c, q_a\) are cathode and anode specific capacities in `Ah/kg`
- \(r_{NP}\) is the anode excess ratio

## Voltage-loss program

I would treat voltage loss as a second-order correction after the mass accounting.

A source-backed fresh 21700 NMC811 cell in the literature starts around `25.1 mΩ` ohmic resistance.  
https://www.mdpi.com/1996-1073/14/16/4724

At `C/3` for a `4.8 Ah` cell, \(I \approx 1.6\) A, so

\[
\Delta V_{ohmic}=IR\approx 1.6\times 0.025=0.040\ \text{V}

Relative to a `3.7 V` class cell, that is about

\[
0.040/3.7 \approx 1.1\%
\]

`PROVEN`  
At low rate, ohmic voltage loss is only about `1%` of cell voltage. Even after adding charge-transfer and concentration polarization, the total low-rate voltage penalty is a few percent, not tens of percent.

So for a `C/3` benchmark, the gap from theoretical-active to practical-cell is mainly a **mass-budget problem**, not a polarization problem.

## What is fundamental vs improvable

`PROVEN`
- The drop from idealized lithium-only numbers to `~500 Wh/kg` active-pair numbers is fundamental. It comes from Faraday’s law plus the host masses required by intercalation chemistry.
- A nonzero separator mass is fundamental because electronic isolation with ionic transport is mandatory.
- A nonzero electrolyte or solid-ion-conductor mass is fundamental because ions must move through something.
- A nonzero current collector mass is fundamental because electrons must move through something with finite conductivity.

`CONJECTURED`
- In 5-10 years, `21700`-class liquid-electrolyte Li-ion can probably cut inactive mass from about `7.1 g/Ah` to roughly `5.5-6.0 g/Ah` through thinner Cu/Al, reduced hardware overhead, lower electrolyte excess, and tighter N/P margins.
- Holding chemistry near the same, that implies maybe `310-340 Wh/kg` cell-level, not `500+ Wh/kg`, for cylindrical safety-qualified products.

`UNKNOWN`
- The hard lower bound on electrolyte excess in a wound cylindrical cell under abuse-qualified wetting and life requirements.
- How much of today’s steel-can/CID/PTC mass is truly irreducible versus inherited from conservative qualification practice.
- The exact decomposition of low-rate polarization into charge-transfer vs transport in a modern high-loading 21700, because that needs pOCV and rate data from the same cell.

## BRIDGE

**BRIDGE: chemistry-agnostic ceiling is not a single Carnot-like constant.**  
The right universal statement is:

\[
E_{\text{practical}} \le \frac{\Delta G_{\text{cell reaction}}}{m_{\text{redox hosts}} + m_{\text{mandatory ion path}} + m_{\text{mandatory electron path}} + m_{\text{mandatory containment}}}
\]

What is universal is the structure of the denominator, not a single numerical ceiling. Different chemistries win or lose by changing which denominator term explodes:
- Li-ion: current collectors + electrolyte + host masses
- Li-S: electrolyte excess, sulfur utilization, Li-metal protection
- Li-air: oxygen management and parasitic reactor mass
- solid-state: separator/electrolyte liquid mass falls, but dense ceramic mass and stack pressure rise

Next I would extend exactly this denominator analysis to `Li-S`, `Li-metal solid-state`, and `Na-ion`, so we can compare not just theoretical Wh/kg, but the ratio:

\[
\frac{E_{\text{practical best-case}}}{E_{\text{active-pair ideal}}}
\]

That ratio is the cleanest way to separate chemistry promise from engineering burden.

#### The Limits Analyst (codex/gpt-5.4)

I will use a multiplicative accounting, because “where the 80% goes” is otherwise ill-posed: some losses are mass penalties, some are voltage penalties, some are cycling-margin penalties. They should not be added in the same bucket.

**PROVEN: canonical decomposition**

For any rechargeable cell at a specified rate and end-of-life criterion,
\[
e_{\rm cell}
=
e_{\rm rev,pair}
\cdot
f_{\rm inactive}
\cdot
\eta_V
\cdot
f_{\rm util}
\cdot
f_{\rm reserve}
\]
where:

- \(e_{\rm cell}\) = delivered gravimetric energy density at packless cell level, Wh/kg
- \(e_{\rm rev,pair}\) = reversible specific energy of the balanced active-material pair, Wh/kg of active pair
- \(f_{\rm inactive} = m_{\rm active}/m_{\rm total}\)
- \(\eta_V = \bar V_{\rm discharge}/\bar V_{\rm OCV}\)
- \(f_{\rm util}\) = fraction of theoretical capacity actually accessed within allowed voltage/current window
- \(f_{\rm reserve}\) = margins for cycle life, lithium inventory, N/P oversizing, safety headroom, etc.

This factorization separates:
- mass overhead,
- irreversible voltage loss,
- inaccessible stoichiometric window,
- deliberate engineering derating.

That is the right “Carnot-like” bookkeeping object.

## 1. First correction: the benchmark depends on what “theoretical” means

**PROVEN:** For conventional Li-ion, a 21700 at 280 Wh/kg is not at 20% of the relevant chemistry ceiling. It is closer to 50-65%.

Take an NMC811/graphite pair.

Let:
- cathode reversible capacity \(Q_c \approx 200\) mAh/g
- graphite reversible capacity \(Q_a \approx 372\) mAh/g
- average cell voltage \(\bar V \approx 3.75\) V
- anode excess \(N/P \approx 1.1\)

Balanced anode-to-cathode mass ratio:
\[
\frac{m_a}{m_c}=\frac{(N/P)\,Q_c}{Q_a}
= \frac{1.1 \cdot 200}{372}
\approx 0.59
\]

Hence active-pair specific energy:
\[
e_{\rm rev,pair}
=
\frac{\bar V Q_c}{1+m_a/m_c}
=
\frac{3.75 \cdot 200}{1.59}
\approx 472 \text{ Wh/kg}
\]

If the practical cell is 280 Wh/kg, then
\[
\frac{280}{472} \approx 0.593
\]
So the practical cell achieves about 59% of the balanced active-pair reversible ceiling.

This is the right reference for “where the gap goes” in conventional Li-ion.

**DEAD:** Using 1200-1500 Wh/kg as the comparison for NMC/graphite full cells.  
Why it fails: that larger number typically comes from counting only part of the chemistry or from a different chemistry class. It is not the full balanced-cell reversible specific energy for commercial layered-oxide/graphite Li-ion.

## 3. Which floors are fundamental?

Now I switch from bookkeeping to limits.

## 3.1 Electrolyte floor

**MECHANISM: electroneutral salt inventory**

The electrolyte must carry essentially one Li\(^+\) per electron through the porous path while remaining sufficiently concentrated that transference, conductivity, and depletion remain acceptable.

A hard lower bound is not set by energy, but by charge inventory and transport.

If areal capacity is \(q_A\) in Ah/m\(^2\), the transferred Li amount is
\[
n_{\rm Li} = \frac{q_A \cdot 3600}{F}
\]
mol/m\(^2\).

For a typical coated electrode stack, \(3\) mAh/cm\(^2\) \(= 30\) Ah/m\(^2\), so
\[
n_{\rm Li} \approx \frac{30\cdot 3600}{96485}\approx 1.12\text{ mol/m}^2
\]

At electrolyte concentration \(c\sim 1000\) mol/m\(^3\), the liquid volume containing this many moles is
\[
V_{\min} \sim \frac{1.12}{1000} = 1.12\times 10^{-3}\text{ m}^3/\text{m}^2
=1.12\text{ mm}
\]

That is obviously far thicker than practical cells, which means the cell does not rely on storing the transferred Li solely as free salt in the separator region. The electrolyte is not a stoichiometric reagent in the same way the electrodes are.

So the *true* floor is transport, not ion count.

**PROVEN:** The minimum electrolyte mass is not set by “enough Li\(^+\) inventory” in a simple stoichiometric sense. That argument gives a nonsense-large lower bound. Therefore electrolyte floor must be derived from ionic conductivity, tortuosity, wetting, and local depletion constraints.

**OPEN:** Derive the transport-floor electrolyte mass from a coupled porous-electrode model:
\[
\Delta \phi_e \sim \frac{iL}{\kappa_{\rm eff}}, \qquad
\Delta c \sim \frac{iL}{FD_{\rm eff}}
\]
with constraints on acceptable \(\Delta \phi_e\) and \(c(x)>0\).

This is a real physics floor.

## 3.2 Current collector floor

**MECHANISM: conductivity floor**

For a collector foil of thickness \(t\), width \(w\), length \(L\), resistivity \(\rho\), with distributed current, the ohmic loss scales as
\[
P \sim \frac{\rho L^2}{t w} I_A^2
\]
up to geometry factors.

Reducing collector mass means reducing \(t\), but then \(IR\) heating rises as \(1/t\).

Thus there is a floor set by:
- allowed temperature rise,
- allowed voltage loss,
- mechanical handling.

Because \(\rho_{\rm Cu}\) and \(\rho_{\rm Al}\) are finite, zero-mass collectors are impossible.

**PROVEN:** Current-collector mass cannot be eliminated. Its lower bound comes from finite electronic conductivity and allowable ohmic dissipation, not from thermodynamics.

Expected reducibility:
- 10%: easy
- 50%: plausible with better architectures or dry-coated thick electrodes
- 90%: not for metal-foil collectors in present geometry
- 100% elimination: impossible unless current collection function is moved elsewhere, in which case that structure still carries a conductivity floor.

## 3.3 Separator floor

**MECHANISM: electronic insulation plus ionic permeability under compressive/mechanical load**

The separator must:
- prevent shorting,
- hold porosity,
- survive puncture/shrinkage,
- pass ionic current.

If thickness \(L_s\to 0\), then ionic resistance improves, but shorting probability and defect sensitivity explode.

There is no thermodynamic law fixing \(L_s\), but there is a hard *reliability/statistical defect* floor and a mechanical stability floor.

This is not purely engineering in the sense of “arbitrarily reducible.” It is constrained by:
- dielectric breakdown/defect percolation,
- particle asperities,
- thermal shrinkage,
- dendrite penetration for Li metal.

Expected reducibility:
- conventional polyolefin separator mass in Li-ion: 10-30% likely
- 50% maybe for specific formats/materials
- 90% reduction: unlikely without changing chemistry/architecture
- elimination: impossible, unless the separator function is absorbed by a solid electrolyte, which reintroduces mass elsewhere

## 3.4 Overpotential floor

Write
\[
\eta = \eta_{\rm ct} + \eta_{\Omega} + \eta_{\rm conc}
\]

### Charge-transfer
Butler-Volmer gives, for symmetric case at moderate current,
\[
\eta_{\rm ct}\approx \frac{2RT}{F}\,\mathrm{asinh}\!\left(\frac{i}{2i_0}\right)
\]

At finite power \(i>0\), \(\eta_{\rm ct}>0\).

**PROVEN:** zero overpotential at nonzero power is impossible unless exchange current density \(i_0\to\infty\), which no real material has.

Thus:
- 100% elimination at finite power: impossible
- 90% reduction: possible only by going to extremely low rate or much larger area
- at fixed areal power, a nonzero floor remains

### Ohmic drop
\[
\eta_{\Omega}=iR
\]
At finite current and finite conductivity, nonzero.

**PROVEN:** elimination impossible at finite power.

### Concentration polarization
From porous transport:
\[
\eta_{\rm conc}\sim \frac{RT}{F}\ln\!\frac{c_{\rm bulk}}{c_{\rm surface}}
\]
At finite flux through finite diffusivity medium, nonzero.

**PROVEN:** elimination impossible at finite power.

So polarization is like friction, not like dead mass:
- can be made arbitrarily small only in the zero-power limit,
- cannot be eliminated at any finite specific power.

This is the cleanest “Carnot-like” statement available.

## 3.5 N/P oversizing and degradation reserve

**MECHANISM: first-law storage vs second-law survivability**

A rechargeable cell must preserve cyclable lithium inventory over many cycles. That forces:
- excess anode capacity,
- excess cathode inventory in some systems,
- upper/lower SOC buffers,
- lithium plating avoidance margin,
- SEI growth allowance.

These are not thermodynamic floors in a single-cycle sense. They are lifecycle/reliability floors for repeated operation.

Expected reducibility:
- 10%: routine
- 50%: hard but possible with chemistry/process advances
- 90%: unlikely for long cycle life
- elimination: impossible for practical rechargeable cells with nonzero degradation mechanisms

## 4. Scaling: which losses shrink with cell size?

**PROVEN:** Surface-associated overheads shrink with larger cell dimensions; transport losses do not automatically.

If characteristic linear size is \(L\):
- casing mass \(\sim L^2\)
- tabs, seals, header overhead \(\sim L^2\) or weaker
- active mass \(\sim L^3\)

So packaging overhead fraction scales roughly as \(1/L\).

Similarly, current collector and separator fractions depend more on stack geometry than external size, so they do not vanish just by making the can bigger.

Transport penalties instead scale with internal path lengths:
- through-thickness ionic drop \(\sim i \ell / \kappa_{\rm eff}\)
- diffusion time \(\tau_D \sim \ell^2/D_{\rm eff}\)

So thicker electrodes reduce relative inactive mass but worsen rate capability and polarization.

**BRIDGE:** There is an optimization problem common to all chemistries:
\[
\max_{\ell,\varepsilon,t_{\rm cc},t_{\rm sep}} \;
e_{\rm cell}
\quad\text{subject to}\quad
\eta_{\Omega},\eta_{\rm conc},\Delta T,\text{ cycle life constraints}
\]
This is the universal design tension between “more active fraction” and “transport/mechanical penalties.”

## 5. Preliminary classification table

For each mechanism: floor type and reducibility.

### Inactive mass
- current collectors: physical conductivity floor; reducible 10-50%; not eliminable
- separator: mechanical/insulation floor; reducible 10-50%; not eliminable as a function
- electrolyte: transport/wetting floor; reducible 10-50%; not eliminable in liquid systems
- binder/conductive additive: percolation/mechanical floor; reducible 10-50%; likely not 90% for current slurry electrodes
- casing/tabs: geometric overhead; strongly engineering; can shrink with scale; not eliminable at standalone-cell level
- BMS: pack-level, not cell-level; engineering

### Electrochemical voltage loss
- charge-transfer: finite-power floor from Butler-Volmer
- ohmic: finite-power floor from finite conductivity
- concentration polarization: finite-power floor from finite diffusivity
All three can be reduced substantially at fixed energy by more area / less current density, but not eliminated at finite power.

### Structural/safety/degradation
- volume-expansion allowance: materials-mechanics floor
- thermal management mass: power/safety dependent, mostly engineering at cell level, more fundamental at pack level
- lithium plating margin: kinetic/transport floor for fast charge
- SEI reserve: not eliminable in present liquid-electrolyte intercalation systems

Q_{\mathrm{Li}}=3860\ \mathrm{mAh\,g^{-1}},

t_{\mathrm{Li,\,stoich}}
=

38.8\ \mu\mathrm{m}.

So a stoichiometric Li-metal anode at \(4\ \mathrm{mAh\,cm^{-2}}\) is already a \(\sim 39\ \mu\mathrm{m}\) foil. ## 2. Li-S: pore-fill is not the right lower bound

Here the active masses change completely.

Use sulfur with
\[
Q_S=1672\ \mathrm{mAh\,g^{-1}},\qquad \bar V\simeq 2.10\ \mathrm{V}.
\]

Then per Ah,

\[
m_S=\frac{1}{1.672}=0.598\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

Take a realistic high-energy sulfur cathode formulation \(70:20:10\) = sulfur : carbon : binder by mass. Then cathode composite mass is

\[
m_{S,\mathrm{coat}}=\frac{0.598}{0.70}=0.854\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

Using \(\rho_S=2.07\), \(\rho_C\approx 2.0\), \(\rho_b\approx 1.8\ \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}\),

\[
\rho_{S,\mathrm{mix}}^{-1}
=
\frac{0.70}{2.07}+\frac{0.20}{2.0}+\frac{0.10}{1.8}
=0.494,
\qquad
\rho_{S,\mathrm{mix}}=2.02\ \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}.
\]

So the charged-state solid volume per Ah is

\[
V_{S,\mathrm{solid}}=\frac{0.854}{2.02}=0.422\ \mathrm{cm^3/Ah}.
\]

Now the key Li-S constraint is discharge expansion to \(\mathrm{Li_2S}\). Using \(\rho_{\mathrm{Li_2S}}\approx 1.66\ \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}\),

\[
V_m(S)=\frac{32.06}{2.07}=15.5\ \mathrm{cm^3/mol},
\qquad
V_m(\mathrm{Li_2S})=\frac{45.95}{1.66}=27.7\ \mathrm{cm^3/mol}.
\]

Thus the increase per mole of sulfur atoms is

\[
\Delta V_m = 27.7-15.5=12.2\ \mathrm{cm^3/mol},
\]

or per gram sulfur,

\[
\Delta v = \frac{12.2}{32.06}=0.381\ \mathrm{cm^3/g_S}.
\]

For \(0.598\ \mathrm{g_S/Ah}\),

\[
\Delta V_{\mathrm{discharge}}=0.381(0.598)=0.228\ \mathrm{cm^3/Ah}.
\]

Therefore any sulfur cathode that is fully dense at charge must carry at least

\[
V_{\mathrm{void,min}}\ge 0.228\ \mathrm{cm^3/Ah}
\]

just to absorb product expansion. Since \(V_{\mathrm{solid}}=0.422\), this requires

\[
\frac{\varepsilon}{1-\varepsilon}\ge \frac{0.228}{0.422}=0.540,
\qquad
\varepsilon_{\min}\ge 0.351.
\]

So even before separator pores, the sulfur cathode has a hard void floor of about \(35\%\) porosity if the expansion is to be accommodated internally.

If one naively uses only this geometric pore-fill bound, the cathode liquid floor is

\[
m_{\mathrm{elyte},S\text{-pore only}}
=
1.20(0.228+0.160)
=
0.466\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

Then an unsupported Li-metal Li-S wet stack floor would be

\[
m_{\mathrm{LiS,naive}}
=
m_{S,\mathrm{coat}}+m_{\mathrm{Al}}+m_{\mathrm{sep,poly}}
+m_{\mathrm{Li}}+m_{\mathrm{elyte},S\text{-pore only}}
\]
\[
=0.854+0.506+0.216+0.259+0.466
=2.301\ \mathrm{g/Ah},
\]

hence

\[
e_{\mathrm{LiS,naive}}=\frac{2.10}{0.002301}=913\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}.
\]

This number is too optimistic for practical liquid Li-S, and we can pinpoint why. Practical Li-S cells are commonly discussed in terms of lean electrolyte-to-sulfur ratio \(E/S\), not pore fill. Converting \(E/S\) into mass/Ah:

\[
m_{\mathrm{elyte}} = (E/S)\cdot m_S \cdot \rho_{\mathrm{elyte}}.
\]

For \(E/S=3\ \mu\mathrm{L\,mg_S^{-1}}=3\ \mathrm{mL\,g_S^{-1}}\),

\[
V_{\mathrm{elyte}}=3(0.598)=1.794\ \mathrm{mL/Ah},
\qquad
m_{\mathrm{elyte}}=1.20(1.794)=2.15\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

For \(E/S=2\),

\[
m_{\mathrm{elyte}}=1.20(2)(0.598)=1.44\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

Both are far above the pore-fill floor \(0.466\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\). So in Li-S, the dominant transport-medium penalty is not geometric wetting alone; it is the need for enough catholyte to solvate and transport polysulfide intermediates while tolerating precipitation of insulating \(\mathrm{Li_2S}\).

Numerically, with \(E/S=3\),

\[
m_{\mathrm{LiS},\,E/S=3}
=
0.854+0.506+0.216+0.259+2.15
=
3.985\ \mathrm{g/Ah},
\]
\[
e_{\mathrm{LiS},\,E/S=3}
=
\frac{2.10}{0.003985}
=
527\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}.
\]

That is still high, but it has already lost \(386\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}\) relative to the naive pore-fill bound solely because Li-S needs chemistry-driven catholyte excess.

## 3. Sulfide solid-state NMC811 / Li-metal

Now replace liquid-filled pores by dense solid ion conductor.

Take the same NMC811 cathode active mass \(5.000\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\), but use a composite cathode with \(70\%\) active and \(30\%\) sulfide solid electrolyte + conductive additive. Then

\[
m_{c,\mathrm{comp}}=\frac{5.000}{0.70}=7.143\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

Take a dense sulfide separator of thickness \(20\ \mu\mathrm{m}\), density \(\rho_{\mathrm{SSE}}=1.9\ \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}\), and the same \(A_{\mathrm{sep}}=250\ \mathrm{cm^2}\). Then

\[
m_{\mathrm{SSE,sep}}
=
1.9(0.0020)(250)
=
0.950\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]

Add lithium metal at stoichiometric mass \(0.259\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\) and the same Al foil \(0.506\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\). Then

\[
m_{\mathrm{ASSB}}
=
7.143+0.950+0.259+0.506
=
8.858\ \mathrm{g/Ah},
\]

so at \(3.75\ \mathrm{V}\),

\[
e_{\mathrm{ASSB}}=\frac{3.75}{0.008858}=423.3\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}.
\]

This is lower than the unsupported liquid Li-metal floor \(542.8\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}\), despite eliminating liquid electrolyte entirely, because the ionic transport medium has become a dense structural component:

- cathode ionic percolation requires substantial solid-electrolyte fraction in the composite;
- separator ionic conduction requires a dense membrane with finite thickness.

So solid-state does escape liquid `pore-volume locking`, but not the broader architectural necessity of allocating mass to ion-conducting pathways.

A more aggressive cathode composite, say \(80\%\) active instead of \(70\%\), gives

\[
m_{c,\mathrm{comp}}=\frac{5.000}{0.80}=6.250\ \mathrm{g/Ah},

\[
m_{\mathrm{ASSB},80\%}=6.250+0.950+0.259+0.506=7.965\ \mathrm{g/Ah},
\]
\[
e_{\mathrm{ASSB},80\%}=470.7\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}.
\]

So for solid-state, the mass leverage is very sensitive to active fraction in the composite cathode.

## What this says about `[O1]`

The three replacements give three distinct transport-medium regimes:

1. Liquid Li-metal:
\[
m_{\mathrm{transport}} = m_{\mathrm{elyte},\,c+s}=0.665\ \mathrm{g/Ah}.
\]
This is genuinely smaller than liquid graphite-cell transport mass because the anode-pore term vanishes.

2. Liquid Li-S:
\[
m_{\mathrm{transport}} \not\approx m_{\mathrm{pore-fill}}.
\]
The relevant floor is often set by \(E/S\), which can be \(1.44\)–\(2.15\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\) even under “lean” conditions, well above pore geometry.

3. Solid-state:
\[
m_{\mathrm{transport}} = m_{\mathrm{SSE,sep}} + m_{\mathrm{SSE\ in\ cathode\ composite}},
\]
so the liquid floor is removed but replaced by dense ion-conductor mass.

This suggests a stronger chemistry-agnostic law than `[M1]`.

PROVEN: For the `[G1]` geometry with the graphite side replaced by stoichiometric Li metal, the liquid-electrolyte floor drops from \(1.37\) to \(0.665\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\), and the unsupported wet-stack ceiling rises to \(542.8\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}\); with a \(6\ \mu\mathrm{m}\) Cu support it is \(494.7\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}\).

PROVEN: In Li-S, internal cathode void volume has a hard lower bound from sulfur \(\to\) \(\mathrm{Li_2S}\) expansion: under the \(70{:}20{:}10\) composite used above, the minimum cathode porosity to absorb discharge products internally is \(\varepsilon_{\min}\ge 0.351\).

PROVEN: Under the same Li-S assumptions, a pure pore-fill estimate gives only \(0.466\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\) electrolyte, but a lean practical \(E/S=3\ \mu\mathrm{L\,mg_S^{-1}}\) already implies \(2.15\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\). Therefore pore-fill alone is not the controlling transport-medium bound for practical liquid Li-S.

PROVEN: For a sulfide solid-state NMC811/Li-metal stack with a \(70\%\)-active composite cathode and \(20\ \mu\mathrm{m}\) dense separator, the stack floor is \(8.858\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\), corresponding to \(423.3\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}\). At \(80\%\) cathode active fraction, this improves to \(470.7\ \mathrm{Wh/kg}\).

DEAD: “Use liquid-ion-cell pore-fill geometry as the sole lower bound for Li-S” because even the rigorous geometric floor (\(0.466\ \mathrm{g/Ah}\) here) sits far below lean practical catholyte requirements set by dissolved-polysulfide transport and precipitation management.

MECHANISM: `Ion-path mass locking` — every rechargeable cell must devote a finite mass per transferred Ah to an ion-conducting pathway. In liquid intercalation cells this appears as wetted pore volume; in Li-S it appears as catholyte inventory; in solid-state it appears as separator and composite-electrode solid electrolyte. The medium changes, but the ion-path mass does not vanish.

GADGET: A 1 Ah liquid Li-metal/NMC811 repeat unit obtained by taking `[G1]`, deleting the porous graphite host, and inserting a \(38.8\ \mu\mathrm{m}\) stoichiometric Li foil. It explicitly shows that most of the gain comes from erasing the anode pore network plus Cu, not from any change on the cathode side.

BRIDGE: The right chemistry-agnostic ceiling is probably not set by “electrolyte mass” or “separator mass” separately, but by the minimum mass of an ion-conducting path with sufficient conductivity, mechanical stability, and redox compatibility to move \(1\ \mathrm{Ah}\) reversibly across the electrode spacing at the target current density.

OPEN: Derive a lower bound of the form
\[
m_{\mathrm{ion\ path}}/Ah \ge F(\sigma_{\mathrm{ion}},\,\tau,\,j,\,\Delta\phi_{\max},\,\text{stability window},\,\text{mechanical modulus})
\]
that applies to both liquid and solid systems. That would be the closest analogue here to a Carnot-like chemistry-agnostic ceiling.

#### The Limits Analyst (codex/gpt-5.4)

Building on `[BR1]` and attacking `[O2]`, I will isolate what a *pure transport-law* can and cannot force.

Let an ion-conducting path be modeled as a 1D slab of thickness \(L\), geometric area \(A\), effective ionic conductivity \(\kappa_{\rm eff}\), and mass density \(\rho_{\rm path}\). Its mass is

\[
m_{\rm path}=\rho_{\rm path}AL.
\]

Let the cell be discharged galvanostatically for time \(T\) (hours) at areal capacity \(q\) (Ah cm\(^{-2}\)), so the current density is

\[
j=\frac{q}{T}.
\]

The ionic ohmic drop across the path is

\[
\Delta \phi_{\rm ohm} = j\frac{L}{\kappa_{\rm eff}}.
\]

Suppose the design budget requires

\[
\Delta \phi_{\rm ohm}\le \Delta \phi_{\max}.
\]

Then necessarily

\[
j\le \frac{\kappa_{\rm eff}\Delta\phi_{\max}}{L}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
q\le \frac{\kappa_{\rm eff}\Delta\phi_{\max}T}{L}.
\]

Now divide path mass by stored capacity \(Q=qA\):

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{Q}
=
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}AL}{qA}
=
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}L}{q}.
\]

Using the upper bound on \(q\),

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{Q}
\ge
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}L^2}{\kappa_{\rm eff}\Delta\phi_{\max}T}.
\]

Since \(Q\) is in Ah, this is directly a bound in g/Ah:

\[
\boxed{
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}L^2}{\kappa_{\rm eff}\Delta\phi_{\max}T}
}
\tag{1}
\]

provided \(T\) is in hours, \(L\) in cm, \(\rho\) in g cm\(^{-3}\), \(\kappa\) in S cm\(^{-1}\), and \(\Delta\phi\) in V.

This is the first clean “Carnot-like” statement I think survives stress testing: if there is an irreducible ion-path thickness \(L\), then finite conductivity alone forces a nonzero path mass per Ah.

Now specialize to porous liquid media. Write

\[
\kappa_{\rm eff}=\kappa_0\frac{\varepsilon}{\tau},
\qquad
\rho_{\rm path}=(1-\varepsilon)\rho_{\rm host}+\varepsilon \rho_{\rm elyte},
\]

where \(\kappa_0\) is bulk electrolyte conductivity, \(\varepsilon\) porosity, \(\tau\) tortuosity. Then (1) becomes

\[
\boxed{
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}\tau L^2}{\kappa_0\varepsilon \Delta\phi_{\max}T}
}
\tag{2}
\]

which explicitly inserts the \(\tau\) requested in `[O2]`.

That is the formal lower bound. The next question is whether it is actually *tight enough to matter* for high-energy cells.

Take the liquid-separator part of `[G1]` only. Use

- \(L=16\ \mu{\rm m}=1.6\times 10^{-3}\ {\rm cm}\)
- \(\rho_{\rm host}=0.90\), \(\rho_{\rm elyte}=1.20\ {\rm g\,cm^{-3}}\)
- \(\varepsilon=0.40\)
- \(\tau=1.6\) as a representative porous-separator value
- \(\kappa_0=10\ {\rm mS\,cm^{-1}}=0.010\ {\rm S\,cm^{-1}}\)
- \(T=3\ {\rm h}\) (C/3)
- \(\Delta\phi_{\max}=0.10\ {\rm V}\)

Then

\[
\rho_{\rm path}=0.6(0.90)+0.4(1.20)=1.02\ {\rm g\,cm^{-3}},
\]
\[
\kappa_{\rm eff}=0.010\cdot \frac{0.40}{1.6}=2.5\times 10^{-3}\ {\rm S\,cm^{-1}}.
\]

Plugging into (1),

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{1.02(1.6\times 10^{-3})^2}{(2.5\times10^{-3})(0.10)(3)}
=
3.5\times 10^{-3}\ {\rm g/Ah}.
\]

But `[P1]` already implies the separator-associated actual mass is much larger:

- separator polymer: \(0.216\ {\rm g/Ah}\)
- separator pore electrolyte: \(1.20\times 0.160=0.192\ {\rm g/Ah}\)

so

\[
m_{\rm sep\ package}=0.408\ {\rm g/Ah}.
\]

Therefore the conductivity floor captures only

\[
\frac{0.0035}{0.408}\approx 0.86\%
\]

of the realized separator package mass.

Do the same for the sulfide SSE separator in `[P7]`:

- \(L=20\ \mu{\rm m}=2.0\times10^{-3}\ {\rm cm}\)
- \(\rho_{\rm path}=1.9\ {\rm g\,cm^{-3}}\)
- \(\kappa_{\rm eff}=3\ {\rm mS\,cm^{-1}}=0.003\ {\rm S\,cm^{-1}}\)
- \(T=3\ {\rm h}\)
- \(\Delta\phi_{\max}=0.10\ {\rm V}\)

Then

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{1.9(2.0\times 10^{-3})^2}{(0.003)(0.10)(3)}
=
8.4\times 10^{-3}\ {\rm g/Ah}.
\]

But `[P7]` gives the actual dense SSE separator mass

\[
m_{\rm SSE,sep}=0.950\ {\rm g/Ah}.
\]

So again the conductivity floor is only

\[
\frac{0.0084}{0.950}\approx 0.88\%
\]

of the realized separator mass.

This is the critical failure point of a naive “conductivity sets the mass floor” program: conductivity gives a rigorous bound, but in energy cells it is about two orders of magnitude too weak.

Now I want to show why this weakness is structural, not just bad parameter choice.

From (1),

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}\propto \frac{L^2}{\kappa_{\rm eff}T}.
\]

So as \(T\to\infty\) (quasi-static discharge),

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}\to 0.
\]

Therefore:

- pure ohmic transport does **not** generate a rate-independent specific-energy ceiling;
- any universal ceiling derived from transport alone disappears in the slow-discharge limit.

The same issue afflicts concentration-polarization-only bounds. In a binary electrolyte, a necessary condition to avoid complete salt depletion is of the form

\[
jL \lesssim \frac{F D_{\rm eff} c_0}{1-t_+}
\]

up to order-one geometric factors. This again constrains the product \(jL\), hence constrains allowable *power density* or *thickness*, but does not impose a positive rate-independent lower bound on \(m/{\rm Ah}\) unless one separately supplies an irreducible \(L_{\min}\).

So the correct logical structure is:

1. Transport laws give **upper bounds** on admissible \(jL\).
2. To convert those into **lower bounds** on \(m/{\rm Ah}\), one must already possess a non-transport floor \(L_{\min}\).
3. Hence the dominant floor is not conductivity itself, but whatever physics prevents \(L\) from going to zero.

That tells us where to look next: electronic insulation, defect tolerance, surface roughness amplitude, dendrite-blocking stress, puncture resistance, manufacturable pinhole probability, and cathode-side percolation. Those are the real sources of nonzero \(L_{\min}\).

A useful reparameterization is the material figure of merit

\[
\Gamma \equiv \frac{\kappa_{\rm eff}}{\rho_{\rm path}}
\qquad\text{(ionic conductivity per unit mass density)}.
\]

Then (1) is

\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{L^2}{\Gamma\,\Delta\phi_{\max}T}.
\]

At fixed \(L\), \(\Delta\phi_{\max}\), and \(T\), higher \(\Gamma\) is always better. Liquids tend to win on \(\Gamma\), but lose because they require large pore volume or catholyte inventory; solids lose on \(\Gamma\), but can sometimes win on architecture if they permit smaller total path volume. This makes `[BR1]` sharper: the cross-chemistry object is not “electrolyte mass” but a constrained optimization over \((L,\Gamma,\text{compatibility},\text{mechanics})\).

So the present state is:

- the conductivity-based bound exists and is rigorous;
- it is too weak to explain current mass penalties;
- the missing ingredient is a separate lower bound on *minimum admissible path thickness*.

That is real forward progress on `[O2]`, because it tells us exactly where a genuine chemistry-agnostic ceiling would have to come from, and exactly why a purely transport-derived Carnot analogue fails.

PROVEN: For any slab-like ion path with effective conductivity \(\kappa_{\rm eff}\), density \(\rho_{\rm path}\), thickness \(L\), discharge time \(T\), and allowed ohmic drop \(\Delta\phi_{\max}\),
\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}L^2}{\kappa_{\rm eff}\Delta\phi_{\max}T}.
\]

PROVEN: For a porous liquid medium with \(\kappa_{\rm eff}=\kappa_0\varepsilon/\tau\),
\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}\tau L^2}{\kappa_0\varepsilon \Delta\phi_{\max}T}.
\]

PROVEN: Applied to a \(16\ \mu{\rm m}\) liquid separator at C/3 with \(\Delta\phi_{\max}=0.1\ {\rm V}\), the conductivity floor is only \(\sim 3.5\times 10^{-3}\ {\rm g/Ah}\), versus an actual separator package mass of \(0.408\ {\rm g/Ah}\). So ionic conductivity explains \(<1\%\) of that mass.

PROVEN: Applied to the \(20\ \mu{\rm m}\) sulfide SSE separator in `[P7]`, the conductivity floor is only \(\sim 8.4\times10^{-3}\ {\rm g/Ah}\), versus an actual \(0.950\ {\rm g/Ah}\). Again, conductivity explains \(<1\%\) of that mass.

PROVEN: A purely conductivity-based “Carnot limit” for battery specific energy cannot exist at fixed chemistry independent of rate, because the lower bound above scales as \(1/T\) and vanishes in the quasi-static limit \(T\to\infty\).

DEAD: “Derive the chemistry-agnostic ceiling from ionic conductivity alone” because conductivity constrains admissible \(jL\) and power, but without an independent thickness floor \(L_{\min}\) it does not force a substantial positive mass per Ah.

OPEN: Derive a rigorous lower bound on \(L_{\min}\) from non-transport physics: electronic insulation, defect/pinhole statistics, separator puncture resistance under stack pressure, roughness accommodation, or dendrite-suppression mechanics.

OPEN: Couple the transport bound above to a cathode-side upper bound on areal capacity \(q_{\max}\) from solid diffusion, reaction heterogeneity, or chemo-mechanical cracking, so that
\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}=\frac{\rho_{\rm path}L}{q}
\]
acquires a rate-independent lower bound through \(q\le q_{\max}\).

MECHANISM: `Thickness-floor transfer` — transport laws by themselves only cap \(jL\); a nonzero mass floor appears only when some independent physics forbids \(L\to 0\). The true ceiling is therefore set by whatever transfers a thickness floor into the ion path.

GADGET: A “separator-only conductivity floor” calculation for both a liquid polyolefin separator and a sulfide SSE separator. In both cases the rigorous ohmic lower bound is about \(10^{-2}\ {\rm g/Ah}\), while realized masses are \(10^{-1}\) to \(10^{0}\ {\rm g/Ah}\). This explicitly exhibits the gap between transport-limited and architecture-limited regimes.

BRIDGE: The right chemistry-agnostic ceiling, if it exists, should be a variational problem over
\[
(L_{\min},\Gamma,q_{\max},\Delta\phi_{\max})
\]
rather than over \(\kappa\) or electrolyte mass alone. That would unify liquid, Li-S, and solid-state under one statement: transport sets the conductance requirement, but mechanics/compatibility/percolation set the irreducible geometry.

\[
P(\text{no short})=P(\xi_i<L\ \forall i)=\Phi(L/\sigma)^N.

\[
\Phi(L/\sigma)^N \ge 1-p.

\[

**Result B: no chemistry-agnostic Carnot-like ceiling follows from ionic conductivity alone**

For any slab-like ion path of thickness \(L\), density \(\rho_{\rm path}\), effective ionic conductivity \(\kappa_{\rm eff}\), discharged in time \(T\) with allowed ionic ohmic drop \(\Delta\phi_{\max}\),
\[
\frac{m_{\rm path}}{\mathrm{Ah}}
\ge
\frac{\rho_{\rm path}L^2}{\kappa_{\rm eff}\Delta\phi_{\max}T}.
\]
Hence this lower bound scales as \(1/T\) and vanishes in the quasi-static limit \(T\to\infty\). Therefore **ionic conductivity alone cannot generate a rate-independent universal specific-energy ceiling**. Any nonzero practical ceiling must also use an independent thickness floor \(L_{\min}>0\) and/or a finite areal-capacity ceiling \(q_{\max}(T)\).

**5. KEY INSIGHT**

The single most important idea was this:

**Use \(g/Wh\), not \(Wh/kg\), as the conserved bookkeeping variable.**

Once the problem is written in reciprocal specific energy,
\[
\mu=\frac{m}{E},
\]
every overhead adds linearly, and the vague question “where did the other 80% go?” becomes an exact denominator ledger.

That change of variable exposed the real structure:

- In conventional Li-ion, the missing energy is mostly **current collectors, electrolyte, packaging, and other non-redox grams**.
- In liquid Li-metal, gains come mainly from **host erasure** and partial ion-path reduction.
- In Li-S, the dominant thief is **catholyte mass**, not sulfur itself.
- In solid-state, liquid electrolyte is often not eliminated but **relocated into dense separator/composite architecture**.

So the closest electrochemical analogue of a Carnot limit is not a single efficiency number. It is an additive lower-bound problem on the unavoidable **mass-per-energy denominator**.
