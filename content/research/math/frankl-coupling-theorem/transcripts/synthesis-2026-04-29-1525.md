## Problem

The fiber decomposition technique proves max p_x >= 0.40 for union-closed families with minimal sets of size <= 4, strictly beating the entropy barrier of 0.382. For k=5 (all minimal sets of size >= 5), the technique stalls.

EXHAUSTED RESULTS (do not re-derive):

[DEAD-1] The uncoupled chamber-marginal LP has exact optimum (185-32B)/425, giving 0.3878 < 0.39 at B=75/119. No linear refinement of this LP can close the gap.
[DEAD-2] Every S_5-equivariant linear transfer from edge masses to vertex loads collapses to degree + total mass (dim Hom_{S_5}(E,V) = 2). The 5-dimensional irreducible of the Petersen graph is invisible to all such maps.
[DEAD-3] The isotropic point b_e = B/10 is the exact LP adversary. It is the barycenter of the 12 Hamilton 5-cycles of K_5.
[DEAD-4] Chamber-local LP refinements collapse because a single full-cube chamber state certifies all three opposite-pair demands simultaneously.
[DEAD-5] The triangle stable-set atom {12,13,23} kills every disjoint-pair-only bonus because its support is Petersen-stable.
[DEAD-6] Quadratic chamber energy q_c = sum b_e b_f over opposite pairs also vanishes on triangle atoms.
[DEAD-7] Adjacent-pair triple forcing collapses onto common 1234-good blocks via Lemma: two 3-cubes with 2-point overlap force the 4-cube.

PROVEN TOOLS AVAILABLE:
[P1] Fiber decomposition: F = disjoint union of {T union S : S in L_T}, each L_T union-closed with M in L_T.
[P2] Three-type rigidity: good fibers are untagged (floor 1/17), singly tagged (9/17 on support, 1/17 off), or full (1/2 everywhere).
[P3] Cross-fiber forcing: any two bad fibers with labels e,f force L_{T cup U} to contain 2^{e cup f} cup {M}.
[P4] A/B decomposition: if {x} in L then p_x(L) >= 1/2 via injection.
[P5] Augmentation defect identity: p_x = 1/2 + (|R_x| - |M_x|)/(2|F|) where M_x = bad augmentations, R_x = bad removals.

THE EXACT GAP: The LP adversary places b_e = B/10 uniformly and g_v = (1-B)/5 uniformly across chambers, making every vertex identical. No linear observable distinguishes this from the true fiber geometry. The proof MUST use a nonlinear or structural coupling between where bad mass sits and where good-fiber support localizes.

TARGET: Prove a coupling theorem of the form: for every union-closed family with all minimal sets of size >= 5, there exists a vertex x such that the localized good-fiber load through x satisfies lambda_x >= Phi_x(b) where Phi_x is strictly positive on the isotropic point. Combined with the proven vertex-frequency formula and [P1]-[P5], this would close max p_x >= 0.39.

## Constraints

You MUST prove a NONLINEAR coupling theorem. Linear inequalities on edge masses, chamber marginals, or pattern masses are DEAD and may NOT be used as the primary mechanism. Acceptable proof techniques: (a) dependent couplings between X and Y that are NOT independent, (b) transport inequalities (Marton, Talagrand, or novel), (c) SDP/sum-of-squares certificates, (d) Shearer's inequality or submodularity on the projection poset, (e) quadratic or higher-order structural invariants that see the Petersen 5-irreducible, (f) any genuinely new mechanism that is positive on the isotropic adversary point. Tag all results as PROVEN/DEAD/OPEN.

## Creative Direction

THREE VIABLE ATTACK VECTORS:

(1) DEPENDENT COUPLING: Instead of X,Y iid uniform on F, construct Y = f(X) where f uses union-closure. Sawin's dependent coupling improved entropy by ~0.001 bits/coordinate. You need ~0.002 bits at the boundary. The fiber decomposition gives you structural information Sawin didn't have: you know the exact three-type good-fiber rigidity and the A/B decomposition. Can you build a coupling that exploits fiber structure to get the extra 0.001?

(2) AUGMENTATION DEFECT BOUND: From [P5], p_x = 1/2 + (|R_x|-|M_x|)/(2|F|). The problem reduces to: find x with net augmentation defect |M_x|-|R_x| <= 0.22|F|. On the isotropic point, every x has the same defect. But in a REAL union-closed family, the augmentation map A -> A cup {x} has structure from union-closure that the LP ignores. Can you prove that union-closure forces SOME x to have small defect by a pigeonhole/counting argument on the augmentation failures?

(3) PROJECTION POSET SUBMODULARITY: The good-fiber tag set Z(T) = {z : L_T is A_z-good} has structure from union-closure: if T1 cup T2 = V and both L_{T1}, L_{T2} are tagged, then L_V might be forced to have higher overlap. The projection poset P inherits union-closure. Can you prove a submodularity or Shearer-type inequality on the tag distribution over P that forces concentration of tags around specific vertices?

## Iteration 1

### EXPLORE

#### The Coupling Builder (codex/gpt-5.4)

**Approach**

I would attack this with a **dependent bad-pair to good-union coupling**, but not the iid coupling. The correct nonlinear object is:

1. Couple two bad fibers `T,U` with a deliberately **non-independent label coupling** that sees the Hamilton 5-cycles from [DEAD-3].
2. Push the pair to the union fiber `W = T ∪ U`.
3. Use [P3] and [P4] to convert label information on `(ℓ(T),ℓ(U))` into a certified lower bound on `p_x(L_W)`.
4. The remaining gap is then a **transport/compression estimate**: compare the pushforward law of `W` under the coupling to the actual good-fiber mass law.

The main point is that step 3 is already a quadratic, explicit, positive-on-isotropic mechanism. The unsolved part is step 4.

---

**PROVEN 1: Any bad-pair coupling gives a quadratic singleton-forcing bonus**

Let `β` be the bad-fiber mass distribution:
\[
\beta(T):=\frac{|L_T|}{|F_{\mathrm{bad}}|},
\]
and let `ℓ(T)∈E(K_5)` be the bad label of fiber `T`.

For any coupling `ν` of `β` with itself, define
\[
W := T\cup U,\qquad \mu := U_{\#}\nu.
\]
For each vertex `x∈[5]`, define the hit event
\[
H_x(T,U):=\mathbf 1\{x\in \ell(T)\cup \ell(U)\}.
\]

**Lemma.**
If `H_x(T,U)=1`, then the union fiber `L_{T\cup U}` satisfies
\[
p_x(L_{T\cup U})\ge \frac12.
\]

**Proof.**
If `x∈\ell(T)\cup\ell(U)`, then by [P3] the fiber `L_{T\cup U}` contains
\[
2^{\,\ell(T)\cup\ell(U)}\cup\{M\}.
\]
Since `x∈\ell(T)\cup\ell(U)`, the singleton `{x}` belongs to that cube, hence `{x}\in L_{T\cup U}`. By [P4], any union-closed fiber containing `{x}` has
\[
p_x(L_{T\cup U})\ge \frac12.
\]
Done.

So for every coupling `ν`,
\[
\int p_x(L_W)\,d\mu(W)\ \ge\ \frac12\,\nu(H_x=1). \tag{1}
\]

This is already the nonlinear mechanism we need: the RHS is quadratic in the bad-fiber law.

---

**PROVEN 2: The iid version gives an explicit positive bonus at the isotropic point**

Let
\[
d_x := \sum_{e\ni x} b_e,\qquad \sum_e b_e = B.
\]
If we take the **independent** label coupling, then
\[
\nu(H_x=1)
= 1-\Bigl(1-\frac{d_x}{B}\Bigr)^2
= \frac{2d_x}{B}-\frac{d_x^2}{B^2}. \tag{2}
\]

Hence from (1),
\[
\int p_x(L_W)\,d\mu(W)\ \ge\ \frac12\Bigl(\frac{2d_x}{B}-\frac{d_x^2}{B^2}\Bigr). \tag{3}
\]

At the isotropic point, `d_x=2B/5`, so
\[
\nu(H_x=1)=\frac{16}{25},\qquad
\int p_x(L_W)\,d\mu(W)\ge \frac{8}{25}=0.32. \tag{4}
\]

So the pair-to-union mechanism is **strictly positive at the isotropic LP adversary**.

This is important: the obstruction is not that the quadratic forcing is too weak. The obstruction is elsewhere.

---

**DEAD 1: Why the naive iid coupling does not solve the problem**

The failure is **not** at the label level. It is at the geometric pushforward level.

The map
\[
(T,U)\mapsto T\cup U
\]
can have huge collisions: many bad pairs can collapse onto the same good fiber. Equation (3) is an estimate under the pushforward law `μ`, not yet under the actual good-fiber mass law `g`.

So the exact missing structural statement is:

> we need a coupling `ν` for which the union pushforward `μ=U_{\#}\nu` is quantitatively controlled by the true good-fiber mass distribution.

The iid coupling ignores external-coordinate overlap, so it does nothing to prevent collision.

That is the precise feature it misses.

---

**Hamilton-cycle dependent coupling**

Now I want to build the label coupling so that it explicitly sees [DEAD-3].

At the isotropic point, the uniform edge law on `K_5` is the barycenter of the `12` Hamilton 5-cycles. So define a label coupling by:

1. Sample a Hamilton cycle `C` uniformly from the 12 cycles.
2. Sample an ordered pair `(e,f)` of edges from `C` using a kernel `K_C`.
3. Then lift `(e,f)` to an actual coupling of bad fibers with labels `(e,f)`.

This is genuinely nonlinear and uses the Hamilton decomposition directly; it is invisible to all equivariant linear edge-to-vertex maps from [DEAD-2].

---

**PROVEN 3: Exact isotropic hit probabilities for the cycle kernels**

Fix a cycle `C` and a vertex `x`.

If `K_C` chooses an ordered **adjacent** edge pair on `C` uniformly, then
\[
\Pr(x\in e\cup f)=\frac35.
\]

If `K_C` chooses an ordered **disjoint** edge pair on `C` uniformly, then
\[
\Pr(x\in e\cup f)=\frac45.
\]

**Check.**
In a 5-cycle, the only disjoint pair avoiding `x` is the unique pair entirely on the opposite 4-path, so 1 of 5 undirected disjoint pairs avoids `x`; hence hit probability `4/5`. For adjacent pairs, 2 of 5 undirected adjacent pairs avoid `x`; hence hit probability `3/5`.

Thus for the mixed cycle kernel
\[
K_C^{(\alpha)} := (1-\alpha)\,K_C^{\mathrm{adj}}+\alpha\,K_C^{\mathrm{dis}},
\]
we get
\[
\Pr(x\in e\cup f)=\frac35+\frac{\alpha}{5}. \tag{5}
\]

This is positive at isotropic for every `α∈[0,1]`, and it improves as disjoint mass increases.

---

**DEAD 2: Pure disjoint and pure adjacent both fail for known reasons**

This is where the workshop’s dead results fit exactly.

- `α=1` (pure disjoint) is blocked by [DEAD-5]: triangle stable-set atoms kill every disjoint-pair-only bonus.
- `α=0` (pure adjacent) is blocked by [DEAD-7]: adjacent-pair triple forcing collapses onto common 1234-good blocks.

So a successful coupling cannot live in either pure mode.

**Diagnosis.**
A working nonlinear theorem must use a coupling that mixes adjacent and disjoint structure in a correlated way, or else uses a second geometric ingredient on the actual fibers that pure chamber-pattern information cannot see.

That is very concrete guidance.

---

**Concrete lift to actual bad fibers**

For each ordered label pair `(e,f)`, let `β_e,β_f` be the bad-fiber laws conditioned on labels `e,f`.

I would define the fiber-level lift by **overlap-minimizing optimal transport**:

\[
\nu_{e,f} \in \arg\min_{\pi\in\Gamma(\beta_e,\beta_f)} \mathbb E_\pi |T\cap U|. \tag{6}
\]

Then define the full coupling
\[
\nu := \mathbb E_{C}\, \mathbb E_{(e,f)\sim K_C^{(\alpha)}} \nu_{e,f}. \tag{7}
\]

This is explicit and dependent.

Why this objective? Because
\[
|T\cup U| = |T|+|U|-|T\cap U|,
\]
so minimizing overlap maximizes average union rank. That is exactly the correct direction if we want to suppress collision of the union map and spread mass across more good fibers.

So this is not an arbitrary transport choice; it is the one aligned with the union operation itself.

---

**CONJECTURED theorem**

There should exist `α∈(0,1)` and a lift of the form (7) such that for some absolute `C<\infty`,
\[
\mu_x := U_{\#}\bigl(\nu\!\restriction\! H_x\bigr)
\le C\, g_x \qquad \text{for each }x, \tag{8}
\]
where `g_x` is the actual `x`-good fiber mass distribution.

Then combining (1), (5), and (8) gives
\[
\lambda_x \ge \frac{1}{2C}\,\nu(H_x=1). \tag{9}
\]

At isotropic,
\[
\nu(H_x=1)=\frac35+\frac{\alpha}{5}>0, \tag{10}
\]
so
\[
\lambda_x \ge \frac{1}{2C}\Bigl(\frac35+\frac{\alpha}{5}\Bigr), \tag{11}
\]
which is strictly positive at the isotropic point.

This is the exact shape of the nonlinear coupling theorem I would try to prove.

---

**What is actually proved now**

- **PROVEN:** Pair-to-union forcing converts any bad-fiber coupling into the quadratic lower bound (1).
- **PROVEN:** The induced quadratic hit functional is positive at the isotropic point; for iid it is `16/25`, for cycle-mixed kernels it is `3/5+α/5`.
- **PROVEN:** Pure disjoint and pure adjacent couplings fail for the exact reasons [DEAD-5] and [DEAD-7].
- **CONJECTURED:** The right lift is the Hamilton-cycle label coupling plus overlap-minimizing transport on actual bad fibers.
- **UNKNOWN:** The needed transport domination (8). This is the precise gap.

---

**Next proof step I would take**

I would now try to prove a weaker substitute for (8), namely an entropy version:

\[
D(\mu_x\|g_x)\ \le\ c\,\mathbb E_\nu |T\cap U|. \tag{12}
\]

If such a Marton/Talagrand-type inequality holds on the projection poset, then the overlap-minimizing coupling (6) is exactly the correct optimizer, and we get a quantitative route from transport to localized good load.

That is the next place I would push. The pair-to-union mechanism itself is already in place.

#### The Defect Analyst (codex/gpt-5.4)

I would attack this through the augmentation defect identity, but not at the level of single bad fibers. The natural nonlinear object is the star of bad labels around a vertex:
\[
\beta_{xy}:=\text{bad mass on label }xy,\qquad
Q_x^{(2)}:=\sum_{\{y,z\}\subset M\setminus\{x\}}\beta_{xy}\beta_{xz},
\]
and more generally
\[
Q_x^{(3)}:=\sum_{\substack{S\subset M\setminus\{x\}\\ |S|=3}}\prod_{y\in S}\beta_{xy},
\qquad
Q_x^{(4)}:=\prod_{y\neq x}\beta_{xy}.
\]
These are nonlinear, they are strictly positive at the isotropic point \(\beta_{xy}=B/10\), and they are exactly the quantities cross-fiber forcing can see.

For the isotropic adversary,
\[
Q_x^{(2)}= \binom42\Big(\frac B{10}\Big)^2 = 6\frac{B^2}{100},
\qquad
Q_x^{(3)}=\binom43\Big(\frac B{10}\Big)^3 = 4\frac{B^3}{1000},
\qquad
Q_x^{(4)}=\Big(\frac B{10}\Big)^4.
\]
At \(B=75/119\),
\[
Q_x^{(2)}\approx 0.0238,\quad Q_x^{(3)}\approx 0.0010,\quad Q_x^{(4)}\approx 0.000157.
\]
So any coupling theorem based on these is automatically positive at the isotropic point.

**PROVEN 1: adjacent star pairs force \(x\)-good fibers.**

Fix \(x\in M\). If \(T\) is bad with label \(xy\) and \(U\) is bad with label \(xz\) for \(y\neq z\), then by [P3]
\[
L_{T\cup U}\supseteq 2^{\{x,y,z\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]
In particular \(\{x\}\in L_{T\cup U}\), so by [P4]
\[
p_x(L_{T\cup U})\ge \frac12.
\]
Thus every ordered pair of bad fibers on two distinct star-edges through \(x\) forces an \(x\)-nonnegative target fiber.

This already gives the first nonlinear receiving functional:
\[
N_x^{(2)}:=\sum_{\{y,z\}\subset M\setminus\{x\}} |\mathcal B_{xy}|\,|\mathcal B_{xz}|
\]
or its mass-weighted analogue \(Q_x^{(2)}\).

**PROVEN 2: triple star labels force a 4-cube.**

Suppose \(W\) lies above bad fibers with labels \(xy,xz,xw\). Then by the previous step \(L_W\) contains the two 3-cubes
\[
2^{\{x,y,z\}}\cup\{M\},\qquad 2^{\{x,y,w\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]
These two 3-cubes have 2-point overlap \(\{x,y\}\), so by [DEAD-7] they force the 4-cube
\[
L_W\supseteq 2^{\{x,y,z,w\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]

**PROVEN 3: four star labels force the full 5-cube.**

Suppose \(W\) lies above bad fibers with labels \(xy,xz,xw,xv\), where \(M=\{x,y,z,w,v\}\). By PROVEN 2, \(L_W\) contains
\[
2^{\{x,y,z,w\}}\cup\{M\}
\quad\text{and}\quad
2^{\{x,y,z,v\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]
Now any subset \(S\subseteq M\) can be written as
\[
S=(S\cap \{x,y,z,w\})\cup (S\cap \{x,y,z,v\}),
\]
and both pieces lie in \(L_W\); union-closure gives \(S\in L_W\). Hence
\[
L_W=2^M.
\]

So the star-multiplicity at \(x\) forces a cube hierarchy:
- 2 distinct star labels through \(x\) force a 3-cube,
- 3 force a 4-cube,
- 4 force the full 5-cube.

That is a genuinely nonlinear structural invariant, and it does see the isotropic point.

**PROVEN 4: explicit defect counts on the forced cubes.**

Let \(C_r=2^{[r]}\). For any coordinate \(x\in[r]\),
\[
p_x(C_r)=\frac12,\qquad |R_x(C_r)|=|M_x(C_r)|=2^{r-1}.
\]
So the bare forced cubes contribute:
- 3-cube: \(4\) successful \(x\)-removals and \(4\) successful \(x\)-augmentations,
- 4-cube: \(8\) and \(8\),
- 5-cube: \(16\) and \(16\).

This matters because in defect units, a worst off-support singly-tagged good fiber contributes
\[
p_x=\frac1{17}\implies |M_x|-|R_x|=\frac{15}{17}|L|.
\]
On the canonical \(17\)-point scale that is defect \(15\). So:

- one forced 3-cube gives only \(4\) \(x\)-removals,
- one forced 4-cube gives \(8\),
- one forced 5-cube gives \(16\).

Relative to the target \(p_x\ge 0.39\), the allowable defect is
\[
|M_x|-|R_x|\le 0.22|F|.
\]
On a \(17\)-point block, this allows \(3.74\). So to neutralize one worst \(17\)-point off-support block, we need to claw back at least
\[
15-3.74 = 11.26
\]
units of removal surplus. A 4-cube is still short; a full 5-cube is enough.

This is the first concrete reason I think the right nonlinear object is not just \(Q_x^{(2)}\), but a weighted combination emphasizing triple and quadruple star aggregation:
\[
\Phi_x(b)\sim 4Q_x^{(2)}+8Q_x^{(3)}+16Q_x^{(4)}.
\]
The coefficients \(4,8,16\) are not arbitrary; they are exactly the removal counts of the forced 3-, 4-, and 5-cubes.

**DEAD: naive one-pair \(\to\) one-good-fiber accounting.**

Take four bad fibers of equal mass, with labels \(x2,x3,x4,x5\), and suppose their joins are all distinct. Then the closure forces:
- \(6\) pair-union fibers, each at least a 3-cube,
- \(4\) triple-union fibers, each at least a 4-cube,
- \(1\) quadruple-union fiber, the full 5-cube.

If I only use fiber-level frequencies, the bad side can still dominate badly:
\[
4\cdot \frac{15}{17}
\]
units of worst-case bad defect from the four sources, while the forced targets are only guaranteed nonnegative at \(x\). So “every bad pair creates one \(x\)-good target” is far too weak. The mechanism has to count actual removals inside the forced cubes, or exploit target-fiber size, not just target-fiber existence.

That is exactly where the augmentation-defect formulation is stronger than the chamber LP: it lets us count \(R_x\) directly.

**Proposed route.**

I think the right theorem to try to prove is:

\[
\textbf{Conjectured Coupling Theorem:}\qquad
R_x \;\ge\; 4\,Q_x^{(2)}|F| + 8\,Q_x^{(3)}|F| + 16\,Q_x^{(4)}|F| - \mathrm{Coll}_x,
\]
where \(\mathrm{Coll}_x\) is a collision term coming from multiple bad tuples mapping to the same removal, and union-closure on the projection poset should force
\[
\mathrm{Coll}_x \le c_2 Q_x^{(2)}|F| + c_3 Q_x^{(3)}|F| + c_4 Q_x^{(4)}|F|
\]
with \(c_i\) small enough that the net coefficient stays positive at the isotropic point.

The map I want is not iid \((X,Y)\). It is a dependent transport:
- sample a bad augmentation \(A\in M_x\),
- condition on a second bad fiber whose label shares \(x\) but uses a different neighbor,
- send the pair to the successful removal of \(A\cup B\cup\{x\}\).

The reason this has a chance is that the target is forced into a 3-/4-/5-cube, and the number of distinct removable \(x\)-containing sets in that cube is exactly \(4,8,16\). So the transport gain is inherently nonlinear in the local star degree around \(x\).

**What is PROVEN now.**
- Star-pair, star-triple, and star-quadruple bad-label aggregation force 3-, 4-, and 5-cubes through \(x\).
- Those cubes provide exactly \(4,8,16\) successful \(x\)-removals.
- Therefore the natural nonlinear observable is a weighted polynomial in the star masses \(\beta_{xy}\), not a linear load.
- Pair forcing alone is too weak; triple and quadruple aggregation are the scales that can plausibly pay the defect.

**What is OPEN.**
- The collision bound. I do not yet have a proof that the transport from bad tuples to successful removals has bounded multiplicity strongly enough to close \(0.39\).
- I also do not yet know whether the right normalization is by fiber mass \(\alpha_T\), by actual set counts inside fibers, or by a mixed quantity.

**Next step I would execute.**

I would stop thinking in terms of “good-fiber load” as a scalar and instead define, for each target fiber \(W\),
\[
s_x(W):=\#\{y\neq x:\exists \text{ bad }T\le W\text{ with label }xy\}.
\]
By the cube-forcing results above, \(W\) contains at least a \((s_x(W)+1)\)-cube through \(x\) for \(s_x(W)\ge 2\). So \(W\) contributes at least
\[
2^{s_x(W)-1}
\]
successful \(x\)-removals.

That gives a concrete nonlinear lower functional
\[
\Lambda_x := \sum_W 2^{s_x(W)-1}\,\mathbf 1_{s_x(W)\ge 2},
\]
which is visibly positive on the isotropic point and is sensitive to the Petersen \(5\)-irreducible because it depends on how bad mass clusters in stars, not just on degrees.

So my current position is:

- **PROVEN:** star clustering of bad labels forces cubes of increasing dimension, with explicit removal counts \(4,8,16\).
- **PROVEN:** this yields a nonlinear family of observables \(\Phi_x\) that are strictly positive at the isotropic point.
- **DEAD:** pair-forcing counted only at the fiber-existence level is too weak.
- **OPEN:** prove a bounded-collision transport inequality from bad augmentations into removals inside the forced cubes.

If useful, I can next try to formalize the collision term on the projection poset and prove the first nontrivial bound
\[
R_x \ge 4Q_x^{(2)}|F| - \mathrm{Coll}_x
\]
with an explicit combinatorial formula for \(\mathrm{Coll}_x\).

#### The Structure Miner (codex/gpt-5.4)

I think the right nonlinear object is a Hamilton-cycle forcing invariant. It is the first thing here that is simultaneously:

1. Nonlinear in the bad masses.
2. Zero on the triangle atom obstruction from `[DEAD-5]`.
3. Strictly positive at the isotropic adversary from `[DEAD-3]`.
4. Strong enough to force an actual full fiber, not just a linear shadow.

I’ll write that out precisely and prove the first forcing lemma now.

**Definition**
Let `E = binom([5],2)`. For each edge `e in E`, let `B_e` be the set of bad fibers with label `e`, and let
\[
b_e:=\sum_{T\in B_e}\mu_T,\qquad \mu_T:=\frac{|L_T|}{|F|}.
\]
Let `Ham(K_5)` denote the 12 undirected Hamilton 5-cycles of `K_5`. Define the degree-5 invariant
\[
\mathfrak H(b):=\sum_{C\in Ham(K_5)}\ \prod_{e\in C} b_e.
\]
This is genuinely nonlinear. At the isotropic adversary `b_e=B/10` for all `e`, so
\[
\mathfrak H(b_{\mathrm{iso}})=12\left(\frac{B}{10}\right)^5>0.
\]
Thus it is strictly positive on the isotropic point.

That is the invariant I propose to mine.

**PROVEN: Hamilton-cycle forcing lemma**
Let
\[
C=(12,23,34,45,15)
\]
be a Hamilton 5-cycle in `K_5`. Suppose there are bad fibers
\[
T_{12}\in B_{12},\ T_{23}\in B_{23},\ T_{34}\in B_{34},\ T_{45}\in B_{45},\ T_{15}\in B_{15}.
\]
Set
\[
U:=T_{12}\cup T_{23}\cup T_{34}\cup T_{45}\cup T_{15}.
\]
Then `L_U` is full.

**Proof.**

By `[P3]`, from the bad fibers labeled `12` and `23`,
\[
L_{T_{12}\cup T_{23}} \supseteq 2^{\{1,2,3\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]
Likewise from `23` and `34`,
\[
L_{T_{23}\cup T_{34}} \supseteq 2^{\{2,3,4\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]
Hence inside
\[
L_{T_{12}\cup T_{23}\cup T_{34}}
\]
we have two 3-cubes on `123` and `234` with 2-point overlap `23`. By `[DEAD-7]`, these force the 4-cube:
\[
L_{T_{12}\cup T_{23}\cup T_{34}} \supseteq 2^{\{1,2,3,4\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]
So certainly
\[
L_U \supseteq 2^{\{1,2,3,4\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]

Similarly, from `23,34,45` we get
\[
L_{T_{23}\cup T_{34}\cup T_{45}} \supseteq 2^{\{2,3,4,5\}}\cup\{M\},
\]
hence
\[
L_U \supseteq 2^{\{2,3,4,5\}}\cup\{M\}.
\]

Now take any subset `S subseteq {1,2,3,4,5}`. Write
\[
A:=S\cap\{1,2,3,4\},\qquad B:=S\cap\{2,3,4,5\}.
\]
Then `A in 2^{1234}` and `B in 2^{2345}`, so `A,B in L_U`. Since `L_U` is union-closed,
\[
S=A\cup B \in L_U.
\]
As this holds for every `S subseteq [5]`, we get
\[

## 1. Exact algebraic structure of `\Gamma_{x,y}`

I will write `1_{\downarrow W}(U):=1_{W\subseteq U}`.

### Lemma 1
For each edge `e`,
\[

This is immediate from the definition.

### Lemma 2
If
\[
f(U)=\sum_A \alpha_A\,1_{\downarrow A}(U),\qquad
g(U)=\sum_B \beta_B\,1_{\downarrow B}(U)

with `\alpha_A,\beta_B\ge 0`, then
\[

\]

#### Proof
Because
\[
1_{\downarrow A}(U)\,1_{\downarrow B}(U)=1_{\downarrow (A\cup B)}(U).
\]
Expanding the product gives the formula. `\square`

### Proposition 3
For every `x,y`, the function `\Gamma_{x,y}` has a principal-lower-set expansion
\[
\Gamma_{x,y}(U)=\sum_W \widetilde q_{x,y}(W)\,1_{\downarrow W}(U),

where
\[
\widetilde q_{x,y}(W)
=
\sum_{P\in HP(S_y)}
\sum_{\substack{T_1,T_2,T_3\ \text{bad}\\
\ell(T_i)=e_i(P),\ T_1\cup T_2\cup T_3=W}}

In particular, the Möbius inverse of `\Gamma_{x,y}` is exactly the unnormalized refined path-pushforward mass `\widetilde q_{x,y}`.

#### Proof

\[
\Gamma_{x,y}(U)
=
\sum_{P\in HP(S_y)}
\prod_{i=1}^3

\mu_{T_i}\,1_{\downarrow T_i}(U)
\right).

Apply Lemma 2 twice. Each monomial contributes
\[
\mu_{T_1}\mu_{T_2}\mu_{T_3}\,

\]
Grouping terms by the union `W=T_1\cup T_2\cup T_3` gives the formula. `\square`

This is already a real structural gain: `[O2]` is not an arbitrary congestion problem. The cubic witness measure is the Möbius inverse of a completely positive lower capacity on `P`.

## 2. The upper-mass profile is the dual alternating capacity

This gives the exact bilinear identity that isolates the anti-congestion gap.

### Proposition 4
For every `x,y`,
\[
\sum_W \widetilde q_{x,y}(W)\,R(W)
=
\sum_U \mu_U\,\Gamma_{x,y}(U).
\]

#### Proof
Start from Proposition 3:
\[
\sum_U \mu_U\,\Gamma_{x,y}(U)
=
\sum_U \mu_U \sum_W \widetilde q_{x,y}(W)1_{W\subseteq U}.
\]
Swap sums:
\[
=
\sum_W \widetilde q_{x,y}(W)\sum_{U\supseteq W}\mu_U
=
\sum_W \widetilde q_{x,y}(W)R(W).
\]
`\square`

Interpretation: the cubic witness mass does not pair naturally with the diagonal weights `\mu_W`; it pairs **exactly** with the upper-capacity `R(W)=\mu(\uparrow W)`. That is the precise nonlinear obstruction behind `[D1]` and `[D2]`.

\[
\text{compare }R(W)\text{ to }\mu_W\text{ on average against the Möbius-positive measure }\widetilde q_{x,y}(W).

That is much sharper than “prove anti-congestion somehow.”

\Big].

\[
\Sigma_{x,y}
=
\sum_W \widetilde q_{x,y}(W)\,
\Pr_{\mu\times\mu}\big(

W\nsubseteq U,\ 
W\nsubseteq V
\big).
\]

In particular, `\Sigma_{x,y}\ge 0`.

#### Proof

\[
1_{W\subseteq U\cup V}+1_{W\subseteq U\cap V}-1_{W\subseteq U}-1_{W\subseteq V}.

Since `W\subseteq U\cap V` iff both `W\subseteq U` and `W\subseteq V`, this equals
\[
1_{\substack{W\subseteq U\cup V\\W\nsubseteq U\\W\nsubseteq V}}.

Average over `U,V` and sum with weights `\widetilde q_{x,y}(W)\ge 0`. `\square`

This is the first nonlinear “split-coverage” invariant I think is actually adapted to `[O2]`. It detects exactly the situation that the LP misses: witness unions `W` whose coordinates are spread across distinct upper fibers rather than concentrated in one.

It is not another linear chamber inequality. It is a second-order poset invariant built from the Möbius-positive cubic capacity.

**after the union step, all path/bridge information collapses to \(4\)-set containment.**
