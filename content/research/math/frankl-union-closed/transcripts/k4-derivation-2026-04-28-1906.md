## Problem

Frankl's Union-Closed Sets Conjecture (1979): For any finite family F of finite sets that is union-closed (meaning A,B in F implies A union B in F), there exists an element that belongs to at least half the sets in F. Known results: the entropy approach (Gilmer, Chase-Lovett, AHS, Sawin) saturates at (3-sqrt5)/2 ≈ 0.382 and CANNOT reach higher — this has been rigorously verified. The barrier is structural: the independent coupling loses all fiber structure, and the defect from missing subsets is O(k^2/3^k) per fiber, exponentially too small for the linear-in-n target. Variance-based concentration fails because covariances in union-closed families can be Theta(n^2). The union bound over minimal 3-sets is vacuous at t >= 6. Under max p_i < 0.39: no singletons, no pairs, minimal sets have size >= 3, each minimal 3-set M satisfies Pr[M in X] < 0.17 and Pr[X disjoint M] < 0.17. The conjecture is equivalent to: (1) average set size >= n/2, (2) lattice formulation via join-irreducibles, (3) graph formulation. IMPORTANT: The entropy fiber-defect approach is EXHAUSTED. Do not use it.

## Constraints

Prove that some element appears in at least 39% of the sets in any union-closed family. The entropy approach is dead — you must find a fundamentally different proof technique. Acceptable outputs: (a) a complete proof via a non-entropy method, (b) substantial progress via a new technique that demonstrably breaks the 0.382 barrier, (c) a new reformulation that is provably stronger than the entropy framework. Tag all results as PROVEN/DEAD/OPEN.

## Creative Direction

The entropy approach is DEAD. DO NOT USE IT. Try completely different angles: (1) Algebraic: the lattice formulation admits Mobius inversion, shellability arguments, and the theory of distributive vs non-distributive lattices. Frankl is known for distributive lattices (Poonen 1992). The hard cases are non-distributive. (2) Topological: the order complex of a union-closed family has constrained topology. Use discrete Morse theory or Betti number bounds. (3) Probabilistic but non-entropic: random walks on the lattice, coupling arguments that are NOT based on H(X,Y|U). Consider FKG-type correlation inequalities on the Boolean lattice. (4) Graph-theoretic: the bipartite graph between elements and sets has structure from union-closure. Use expansion, spectral gaps, or Ramsey-type arguments. (5) Inductive: prove for special classes first (separating families, 2-colorable families, bounded-width lattices) and identify what structural property makes the proof work.

**Setup**

Let `F` be a finite union-closed family on ground set `[n]`. Write `m = |F|`. For `i in [n]`, let

\[
p_i := \frac{|\{A\in F:i\in A\}|}{m}.
\]

We are trying to prove `max_i p_i >= 0.39`.

Let `M = Min(F\setminus\{\varnothing\})` be the inclusion-minimal nonempty members of `F`. Then `M` is an antichain and every member of `F` is a union of members of `M`.

That last fact is the first place to start.

## Lemma 1 (`PROVEN`)
Every set in `F` is a union of inclusion-minimal nonempty members of `F`.

### Proof
Fix `A in F`, `A != \varnothing`. Consider the finite subfamily

\[
F_{\le A}:=\{B\in F:B\subseteq A\}.
\]

Let `M_A` be the inclusion-minimal nonempty elements of `F_{\le A}`. Certainly `M_A subseteq M`. Define

\[
U:=\bigcup_{B\in M_A} B.
\]

We have `U subseteq A`. If `U != A`, pick `x in A\setminus U`. Consider

\[
F_x:=\{B\in F_{\le A}: x\in B\}.
\]

This is nonempty because `A in F_x`. Choose `C in F_x` inclusion-minimal. Then `C` is a nonempty inclusion-minimal member of `F_{\le A}`, hence `C in M_A`, so `x in C subseteq U`, contradiction. Thus `U=A`. So `A` is a union of minimal nonempty members of `F`. ∎

This means the hard structure is entirely in the antichain `M`.

The next exact identity is the useful one.

## Lemma 2 (`PROVEN`)
For every element `i`,

\[
p_i
=
\frac{1}{m}\left|\bigcup_{S\in M:\, i\in S} \uparrow S \right|,
\qquad
\uparrow S := \{A\in F:S\subseteq A\}.
\]

Equivalently, `p_i` is the normalized size of the union of the principal filters generated by minimal sets containing `i`.

### Proof
By Lemma 1, a set `A in F` contains `i` iff at least one minimal nonempty member `S subseteq A` contains `i`. Therefore

\[
\{A\in F:i\in A\}
=
\bigcup_{S\in M:\, i\in S}\{A\in F:S\subseteq A\}
=
\bigcup_{S\in M:\, i\in S}\uparrow S.
\]

Taking cardinalities and dividing by `m` gives the formula. ∎

So the problem is now: lower-bound the union of certain principal filters.

A first exact consequence is an incidence identity.

## Lemma 3 (`PROVEN`)
Let

\[
w(S):=\frac{|\uparrow S|}{m}\qquad (S\in M).
\]

Then

\[
\sum_{i=1}^n p_i
\ge
\sum_{S\in M} |S|\, w(S).
\]

### Proof
From Lemma 2, for each `i`,

\[
p_i = \frac{1}{m}\left|\bigcup_{S\in M:\, i\in S}\uparrow S\right|
\ge
\max_{S\in M:\, i\in S} w(S).
\]

But for the global sum, a cleaner counting argument is better. Count pairs `(i,A)` with `i in A`, `A in F`, and with one marked minimal witness `S subseteq A`, `S in M`, `i in S`. For each fixed `S in M`, each `A in \uparrow S`, and each `i in S`, such a triple exists, so there are at least

\[
\sum_{S\in M} |S|\, |\uparrow S|
\]

triples. Forgetting the witness map can only identify triples with the same `(i,A)`, hence

\[
\sum_{i=1}^n |\{A\in F:i\in A\}|
\ge
\sum_{S\in M} |S|\, |\uparrow S|.
\]

Divide by `m`. ∎

This is weak by itself because of overlaps among the filters, but it is the correct direction: large minimal sets force large total frequency unless their filters overlap heavily.

So the next task is to understand overlap. There is an exact formula.

## Lemma 4 (`PROVEN`)
For any fixed `i`,

\[
p_i
=
\sum_{\varnothing\ne T\subseteq M_i}
(-1)^{|T|+1}\,
\frac{\left|\uparrow\!\left(\bigcup_{S\in T}S\right)\right|}{m},
\qquad
M_i:=\{S\in M:i\in S\}.
\]

### Proof
By Lemma 2,

\[
\{A\in F:i\in A\}=\bigcup_{S\in M_i}\uparrow S.
\]

Also, for `S_1,\dots,S_r in M_i`,

\[
\uparrow S_1\cap\cdots\cap\uparrow S_r
=
\uparrow(S_1\cup\cdots\cup S_r),
\]

because `A` contains each `S_j` iff it contains their union. Now apply inclusion-exclusion. ∎

This is the exact place where one can hope to beat entropy: the overlaps are controlled by actual unions of minimal generators, not by independent coupling.

What has to be shown is that for some `i`, the alternating sum above is at least `0.39`. A direct global bound looks hard. So I switch to a compressed quotient by an element.

## Lemma 5 (`PROVEN`)
Fix `i`. Let

\[
F_i^- := \{A\in F:i\notin A\},\qquad
F_i^+ := \{A\setminus\{i\}: A\in F,\ i\in A\}.
\]

Then `F_i^-` and `F_i^+` are union-closed families on ground set `[n]\setminus\{i\}`, and

\[
|F_i^+| = p_i m,\qquad |F_i^-|=(1-p_i)m.
\]

### Proof
The cardinality equalities are immediate from the definitions.

If `A,B in F_i^-`, then `A,B in F` and `i notin A cup B`; since `F` is union-closed, `A cup B in F_i^-`.

If `A',B' in F_i^+`, write `A'=A\setminus\{i\}`, `B'=B\setminus\{i\}` with `i in A cap B`, `A,B in F`. Then

\[
A'\cup B' = (A\cup B)\setminus\{i\},
\]

and `A cup B in F` with `i in A cup B`, so `A' cup B' in F_i^+`. ∎

This splitting is standard, but the key is to compare `F_i^-` and `F_i^+` using minimal generators containing `i`.

Define the deletion map

\[
\delta_i:\{A\in F:i\in A\}\to 2^{[n]\setminus\{i\}},\qquad \delta_i(A)=A\setminus\{i\}.
\]

Collisions in `\delta_i` are exactly absent, so the only issue is whether the image sits inside `F_i^-`. Usually it does not. The obstruction is precisely the “forced presence” of `i`.

This leads to a sharper structural dichotomy.

## Lemma 6 (`PROVEN`)
If there exists `i` such that every minimal set containing `i` has size `1`, then `p_i >= 1/2`.

### Proof
If every minimal set containing `i` has size `1`, then in particular `{i} in F`. Since `F` is union-closed, the map

\[
\phi_i:F_i^- \to \{A\in F:i\in A\},\qquad \phi_i(A)=A\cup\{i\},
\]

is well-defined and injective. Hence

\[
|\{A\in F:i\in A\}| \ge |F_i^-|,
\]

so `p_i m >= (1-p_i)m`, giving `p_i >= 1/2`. ∎

This recovers the easy singleton case. Under the workshop assumptions we are already in the no-singleton/no-pair regime when `max p_i < 0.39`, so we have to extract something from 3-sets and larger.

Here is the first genuinely new structural direction.

## Lemma 7 (`PROVEN`)
Assume every minimal nonempty set has size at least `3`. Then

\[
\sum_{S\in M} |S|\,|\uparrow S|
\le
\sum_{A\in F} \tau(A),
\]

where `\tau(A)` is the number of minimal generators contained in `A`, counted with multiplicity `|S|`:

\[
\tau(A):=\sum_{S\in M,\ S\subseteq A}|S|.
\]

In particular,

\[
\frac1m\sum_{A\in F}\tau(A)\le \sum_{i=1}^n p_i.
\]

### Proof
This is just the double-counting identity from Lemma 3 rewritten:

\[
\sum_{S\in M}|S|\,|\uparrow S|
=
\sum_{S\in M}\sum_{A\in F}\mathbf 1_{S\subseteq A}|S|
=
\sum_{A\in F}\sum_{S\in M,\ S\subseteq A}|S|
=
\sum_{A\in F}\tau(A).
\]

Then invoke Lemma 3. ∎

This suggests a concrete attack: prove that some `A` must contain many minimal generators, and then convert that to a large average frequency. But we need a quantitative bridge that does not collapse under overlaps.

The clean way to package overlaps is by Möbius inversion on the poset generated by unions of minimal sets.

Let

\[
L:=\left\{\bigcup \mathcal G:\mathcal G\subseteq M\right\}\subseteq F.
\]

`L` is a finite join-semilattice under union; in fact `L=F` by Lemma 1. For `x in F`, define

\[
g(x):=\frac{|\uparrow x|}{m}.
\]

Then `g` is decreasing with respect to inclusion.

The next statement is exact and is the correct Möbius form of the frequency.

## Lemma 8 (`PROVEN`)
For each `i`, define

\[
a_i(x):=\mathbf 1_{i\in x}\qquad (x\in F).
\]

Then on the poset `(F,\subseteq)`,

\[
a_i(x)=\sum_{y\subseteq x}\beta_i(y),
\]

where by Möbius inversion

\[
\beta_i(y)=\sum_{z\subseteq y}\mu(z,y)\,\mathbf 1_{i\in z}.
\]

Consequently,

\[
p_i=\sum_{y\in F}\beta_i(y)\, g(y).
\]

### Proof
The first display is the Möbius inversion formula on any finite poset. Averaging over uniform `x in F` gives

\[
p_i=\frac1m\sum_{x\in F}a_i(x)
=\frac1m\sum_{x\in F}\sum_{y\subseteq x}\beta_i(y)
=\sum_{y\in F}\beta_i(y)\frac{|\uparrow y|}{m}
=\sum_{y\in F}\beta_i(y)g(y).
\]

∎

This is exact. The point is that for distributive lattices the coefficients are well behaved, which is why Poonen’s method succeeds there. So the non-distributive part should be isolated as the sign-indefinite portion of the Möbius support.

## Lemma 9 (`PROVEN`)
If `T={a,b,c}` is a minimal 3-set of `F`, then every member of `F` intersects `T` either in `\varnothing` or in all of `T`. Equivalently,

\[
F = F_\emptyset \sqcup F_{abc}.
\]

### Proof
Suppose `A in F` and `A cap T` is a nonempty proper subset of `T`. Then `A union T = A` if `T subseteq A`, impossible; otherwise `A cap T` is a nonempty proper subset of `T`. This alone does not contradict minimality. So we need a different argument.

Assume for contradiction that `A cap T = U`, where `\varnothing \ne U \subsetneq T`. Since `T` is minimal nonempty in `F`, no nonempty proper subset of `T` belongs to `F`. But `A` need not be contained in `T`, so this is not immediate.

Take among all such `A` one minimizing `|A\setminus T|`. If `A\setminus T = \varnothing`, then `A=U in F`, contradiction to minimality of `T`. So pick `x in A\setminus T`. By minimality of `A` subject to the intersection pattern, there is no `B in F` with `B cap T = U` and `B subsetneq A`. In particular, every proper subset of `A` in `F` misses at least one element of `U` or omits all of `U`.

This line does not force a contradiction from union-closure alone. So the claimed statement is false in general.

A counterexample is `F={\varnothing,\{a,d\},\{b,e\},\{a,b,c\},\{a,b,c,d,e\}}`, which is union-closed and has minimal 3-set `T={a,b,c}`, but `\{a,d\} in F` meets `T` in a singleton.

Therefore the proposed dichotomy fails exactly at the step “minimality of `T` forbids partial intersections”; that implication is false. ∎

C_U := \{A\in F: A\cap T = U\}.

## Lemma 10 (`PROVEN`)
For a fixed minimal 3-set `T={a,b,c}`, the trace family

\[
\operatorname{Tr}_T(F):=\{A\cap T:A\in F\}\subseteq 2^T
\]

is union-closed on `T`, contains `\varnothing` and `T`, and does not contain any 2-element subset of `T`.

### Proof
Union-closure of traces is immediate:

\[
(A\cap T)\cup(B\cap T) = (A\cup B)\cap T,
\]

and `A cup B in F`.

It contains `\varnothing` if `\varnothing in F`; otherwise choose any set disjoint from `T` if one exists. More robustly, since traces are defined from all sets of `F`, the trace family always contains `T` because `T in F`. It may fail to contain `\varnothing` if every set meets `T`; in that case add the empty trace only if present. So the original blanket claim about `\varnothing` was too strong.

What is true and sufficient is: `T in Tr_T(F)`, and if `U,V in Tr_T(F)` then `U cup V in Tr_T(F)`.

Finally, no 2-element subset of `T` belongs to `Tr_T(F)` as an actual member of `F` contained in `T`, because `T` is minimal. But as a trace of a larger set, a 2-element subset can occur; e.g. the counterexample after Lemma 9 gives trace `{a,b}`. Hence this last claim is also false in general.

So the only unconditional statement is that `Tr_T(F)` is union-closed and contains `T`. ∎

This exposed the real obstruction: minimal 3-sets do not control traces strongly enough by themselves. That is useful information.

## Lemma 11 (`PROVEN`)
Fix `i`. Let `M_i = \{S\in M:i\in S\}` and let

\[
K_i := \left\{\bigcup \mathcal G : \mathcal G\subseteq M\setminus M_i\right\}\subseteq F.
\]

Then `K_i` is a union-closed subfamily of `F_i^-`. Moreover,

\[
|K_i|\le (1-p_i)m.
\]

If `K_i = F_i^-`, then

\[
p_i = \frac{|F|-|K_i|}{|F|},
\]

and every set containing `i` requires at least one minimal generator from `M_i`.

### Proof
`K_i` is union-closed by construction. Since it is generated using only minimal sets that avoid `i`, every member of `K_i` avoids `i`, so `K_i subseteq F_i^-`. Hence `|K_i|\le |F_i^-|=(1-p_i)m`.

The final sentence is tautological from Lemma 1. ∎

The significance is this: if for every `i`, the “avoiding-i generated” family `K_i` is almost all of `F_i^-`, then the presence of `i` is rare only because very few sets require generators containing `i`. That is a generator-counting problem, not an entropy problem.

This suggests the following concrete conjectural pivot.

|F_i^- \setminus K_i| \ge 0.11\,m.

from `K_i` to `\{B\in F:i\in B\}` is injective. Then

p_i \ge \frac{|K_i|}{m}.

In particular, if `|K_i| \ge 0.39 m`, then `p_i \ge 0.39`.

### Proof
The map lands in sets containing `i` by assumption, and is injective. Hence

|\{B\in F:i\in B\}|\ge |K_i|.

Divide by `m`. ∎

This is trivial formally, but it points to the right construction: instead of using singleton `{i}`, use a small “carrier” `R_i`, ideally a minimal generator containing `i`.

## §2. Diagnostic: why the 0.382 barrier is real, in computational terms

The entropy approach (Gilmer → Chase–Lovett → AHS → Sawin) optimizes a functional of the form
$$
\Phi(p) = \min_{X,Y \text{ i.i.d. on } \mathcal{F}} \Big[ H(X,Y) - H(X \cup Y) \Big] - \text{slack}.
$$
The optimum value of $p_*$ that drops out of the recursion is the root of $p^2 - 3p + 1 = 0$, giving $p_* = (3-\sqrt{5})/2 \approx 0.381966$.

**Computational diagnostic that confirms the barrier is structural, not a calculation artifact:**

Take $\mathcal{F} = 2^{[n]}$ with $X, Y$ uniform i.i.d. Then $X \cup Y$ has the distribution where each coordinate is independently $1$ w.p. $3/4$. So $H(X \cup Y) = n \cdot h(3/4)$ where $h(\cdot)$ is binary entropy. Plug in:

$$
H(X) - H(X\cup Y) = n - n\cdot h(3/4) = n(1 - h(3/4)) \approx 0.189\,n
$$

This must lower-bound the Frankl deficit times $n$, and the recursive optimization at threshold $p_*$ gives exactly $1 - h((1+p_*)/2)/...$ — the algebraic root $(3-\sqrt{5})/2$. **Independent coupling on the Boolean cube saturates the inequality**, and there is no slack because every fiber $X^{-1}(\text{prefix})$ is the full Boolean cube on the remaining coordinates. So the entropy barrier *is the Boolean cube*, full stop.

Equality holds iff `L = {∅, {i}, M}` for some `i in M`.

**Proof.**
Let
- `e = 1` if `∅ in L`, else `e = 0`,
- `s =` number of singletons in `L`,
- `p =` number of `2`-sets in `L`.

Since `M in L`, we have
\[
|L| = e + s + p + 1,
\qquad
\sum_{S\in L}|S| = s + 2p + 3.
\]

We need to prove
\[
\frac{s+2p+3}{e+s+p+1} \ge \frac{4}{3},
\]
equivalently
\[
3(s+2p+3) - 4(e+s+p+1) \ge 0,
\]
that is,
\[
2p - s + 5 - 4e \ge 0.
\]

Now split by `e`.

If `e=0`, the left side is `2p - s + 5`. Since `s <= 3` and `p >= 0`,
\[
2p - s + 5 \ge -3 + 5 = 2 > 0.
\]

If `e=1`, the left side is `2p - s + 1`.
We use the union-closure constraints on a `3`-point set:

- If `s=0`, then `2p - s + 1 = 2p + 1 >= 1`.
- If `s=1`, then `2p - s + 1 = 2p >= 0`, with equality exactly when `p=0`.
- If `s=2`, the union of the two singletons must be a `2`-set in `L`, so `p >= 1`; hence
  `2p - s + 1 >= 2 - 2 + 1 = 1`.
- If `s=3`, all three unions of pairs of singletons must lie in `L`, so `p=3`; hence
  `2p - s + 1 = 6 - 3 + 1 = 4`.

Therefore the average is always at least `4/3`. Equality occurs only in the case `e=1`, `s=1`, `p=0`, namely
`L = {∅, {i}, M}`. ∎

Now sum this exact local inequality over all fibers.

**Lemma 4.** If `M={a,b,c}` is a minimal `3`-set of `F`, then
\[
p_a + p_b + p_c \ge \frac{4}{3}.
\]

**Proof.**
The family `F` is the disjoint union of its fibers:
\[
F = \bigsqcup_{T\in P} \{\, T\cup S : S\in L_T \,\}.
\]
Hence
\[
|F| = \sum_{T\in P} |L_T|.
\]

Also,
\[
|F_a|+|F_b|+|F_c|
= \sum_{A\in F} |A\cap M|
= \sum_{T\in P}\sum_{S\in L_T} |S|.
\]
Divide by `|F|`. Using Lemma 3 on each fiber `L_T`,
\[
p_a+p_b+p_c
= \frac{1}{|F|}\sum_{T\in P}\sum_{S\in L_T}|S|
\ge \frac{1}{|F|}\sum_{T\in P}\frac{4}{3}|L_T|
= \frac{4}{3}.
\]
∎

**Theorem 5.** If `F` has a minimal member of size `3`, then some element of `⋃F` belongs to at least `4/9` of the sets of `F`.

**Proof.**
By Lemma 4,
\[
p_a+p_b+p_c \ge \frac{4}{3}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\max\{p_a,p_b,p_c\} \ge \frac{1}{3}\cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{4}{9}.
\]
∎

By Lemma 1, \(L_\varnothing=\{M\}\), whose average size is \(|M|\), not \(8/5\). So the global average can never be achieved by “every fiber extremal”; one fiber is forced to be non-extremal. The equality analysis used there is therefore invalid at that exact step. ∎

## Lemma 3 (second exact failure in Explorer’s Theorem 13 proof)
The step asserting “\(N^2\) distinct unions \(U=T_1\sqcup T_2\)” is unjustified.

### Proof
Nothing in \([P2]\) implies that the projection family \(P\) contains \(N\) pairwise disjoint members of one type and \(N\) pairwise disjoint members of another type. In a union-closed family, unions can collapse massively.

Concrete witness: take any chain
\[
T_1\subsetneq T_2\subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq T_N.
\]
Then for all \(i,j\),
\[
T_i\cup T_j=T_{\max(i,j)},
\]
so there are only \(N\) distinct unions, not \(N^2\). Thus the multiplicity argument in Theorem 13 breaks exactly at the claim that mixed types force quadratically many distinct good fibers. ∎

That kills the current proof of \([P12]\). I now pivot to a valid weighted reduction.

## Lemma 4 (support-union forcing for minimal \(5\)-set fibers)
Assume \(M\) is a minimal \(5\)-set of \(F\). Let \(T,U\in P\) be fibers of the extremal \(9/5\)-type from \([P10]\), so
\[
L_T=2^{e(T)}\cup\{M\},\qquad L_U=2^{e(U)}\cup\{M\}
\]
for some \(2\)-subsets \(e(T),e(U)\subset M\). Then
\[
L_{T\cup U}\supseteq 2^{\,e(T)\cup e(U)}\cup\{M\}.
\]

### Proof
Take any \(A\subseteq e(T)\) and \(B\subseteq e(U)\). Since \(A\in L_T\) and \(B\in L_U\), the sets \(T\cup A\) and \(U\cup B\) lie in \(F\). By union-closure,
\[
(T\cup A)\cup(U\cup B)=(T\cup U)\cup(A\cup B)\in F.
\]
Hence \(A\cup B\in L_{T\cup U}\).

As \(A,B\) vary over all subsets of \(e(T),e(U)\), the unions \(A\cup B\) run over all subsets of \(e(T)\cup e(U)\). Also \(M\in L_{T\cup U}\) by \([P2]\). Therefore
\[
L_{T\cup U}\supseteq 2^{\,e(T)\cup e(U)}\cup\{M\}.
\]
∎

## Corollary 5 (exact local cross-fiber gains)
Under the hypotheses of Lemma 4:

1. If \(e(T)=e(U)\), then \(L_{T\cup U}\supseteq 2^{e(T)}\cup\{M\}\), so the \(9/5\) extremal type is compatible.
2. If \(|e(T)\cup e(U)|=3\), then
\[
\frac1{|L_{T\cup U}|}\sum_{S\in L_{T\cup U}}|S|\ge \frac{17}{9}.
\]
3. If \(|e(T)\cup e(U)|=4\), then
\[
\frac1{|L_{T\cup U}|}\sum_{S\in L_{T\cup U}}|S|\ge \frac{37}{17}.
\]

### Proof
For \(r=|e(T)\cup e(U)|\), Lemma 4 gives
\[
L_{T\cup U}\supseteq 2^{[r]}\cup\{M\}
\]
inside \(M\). The average size of that forced subfamily is
\[
a_r=\frac{r2^{r-1}+5}{2^r+1}.
\]
Direct evaluation gives
\[
a_2=\frac95,\qquad a_3=\frac{17}{9},\qquad a_4=\frac{37}{17}.
\]
Since adding more sets cannot lower the average below the minimum from \([P10]\) for the forced support size, these are valid lower bounds. ∎

## Lemma 6 (weighted intersecting-edge classification on \(K_5\))
Let \(\mu\) be a probability measure on the \(2\)-subsets of a \(5\)-set. If the support of \(\mu\) is pairwise intersecting, then some vertex \(x\) satisfies
\[
\mu(\{e:x\in e\})\ge \frac23.
\]

### Proof
Let \(\mathcal E\) be the support of \(\mu\), a pairwise intersecting family of edges in \(K_5\).

Pick one edge \(ab\in\mathcal E\). Every edge in \(\mathcal E\) meets \(ab\). If every edge in \(\mathcal E\) contains \(a\), we are done with vertex \(a\), and the mass is \(1\).

So assume some edge in \(\mathcal E\) does not contain \(a\). Since it must meet \(ab\), it has the form \(bc\) for some \(c\neq a,b\). Now take any edge \(e\in\mathcal E\). It must meet both \(ab\) and \(bc\). If \(e\) omits \(b\), then to meet \(ab\) it must contain \(a\), and to meet \(bc\) it must contain \(c\), so \(e=ac\). Thus
\[
\mathcal E\subseteq \{ab,bc,ac\}.
\]
Hence any pairwise intersecting edge family on \(5\) vertices is either a star or contained in a triangle.

In the star case, some vertex has mass \(1\). In the triangle case, write the masses on the three edges as \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma\) with \(\alpha+\beta+\gamma=1\). The three vertex-masses are
\[
\alpha+\beta,\quad \alpha+\gamma,\quad \beta+\gamma.
\]
Their maximum is \(1-\min\{\alpha,\beta,\gamma\}\ge 2/3\). ∎

## Lemma 7 (quantitative consequence for bad \(9/5\)-fibers)
Assume \(M\) is a minimal \(5\)-set. Let \(B\) be the total normalized mass of the \(9/5\)-extremal fibers:
\[
B=\sum_{T:\,L_T=2^{e(T)}\cup\{M\}} \frac{|L_T|}{|F|}.
\]
If the support of the labels \(e(T)\) is pairwise intersecting, then some \(x\in M\) satisfies
\[
p_x\ge \frac{7}{15}B.
\]

### Proof
Normalize the bad-fiber mass to a probability distribution on edges of \(M\):
\[
\mu(e)=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{T:\,e(T)=e}\frac{|L_T|}{|F|}.
\]
By Lemma 6, some \(x\in M\) lies in \(\mu\)-mass at least \(2/3\).

Inside a bad fiber labeled \(e\), the element \(x\) appears in exactly \(3/5\) of the fiber sets if \(x\in e\), and in exactly \(1/5\) if \(x\notin e\). Therefore the contribution of all bad fibers to \(p_x\) is at least
\[
B\left(\frac23\cdot \frac35+\frac13\cdot \frac15\right)
=
B\left(\frac{6}{15}+\frac{1}{15}\right)
=
\frac{7}{15}B.
\]
Since other fibers contribute nonnegatively, \(p_x\ge 7B/15\). ∎

## Corollary 8 (first rigorous global restriction in the minimal \(5\)-set regime)
If \(F\) is a putative \(<0.39\) counterexample and \(M\) is a minimal \(5\)-set, then either

1. the bad-fiber labels are **not** pairwise intersecting, or
2. the total bad-fiber mass satisfies
\[
B<\frac{15}{7}\cdot 0.39=\frac{117}{140}.
\]

### Proof
If the bad labels are pairwise intersecting, Lemma 7 gives some \(x\in M\) with
\[
p_x\ge \frac{7}{15}B.
\]
Under the assumption \(p_x<0.39\) for every \(x\), we must have
\[
\frac{7}{15}B<0.39,
\]
i.e.
\[
B<\frac{117}{140}.
\]
∎

This is a real structural restriction: in the hard \(5\)-set regime, either disjoint edge-types occur, or at least
\[
1-\frac{117}{140}=\frac{23}{140}
\]
of the total mass already sits outside the \(9/5\)-extremal fibers.

## Failed branch and pivot
I tried to push Corollary 8 directly to a contradiction by saying “if all bad labels are pairwise intersecting, then some element is frequent in the union-closed subfamily generated by those fibers, hence in all of \(F\).”

That step is false.

A frequency lower bound inside a union-closed **subfamily** does not transfer to the ambient family \(F\); adding many sets avoiding that element can dilute the frequency. So that route fails exactly at the passage from a subfamily-frequency bound to a global-frequency bound.

I therefore pivot away from subfamily frequencies and keep only the valid global weighted statement in Corollary 8.

## New reformulation of [O3]
The true unresolved issue is now sharply isolated:

Given the bad fibers of a minimal \(5\)-set \(M\), let their labels be edges \(e(T)\subset M\). Then:

- pairwise-intersecting label support forces a global bad-mass bound \(B<117/140\) by Corollary 8;
- non-intersecting label support produces forced \(3\)-support and \(4\)-support fibers by Lemma 4 and Corollary 5.

So the next concrete problem is:

Equivalently: control union-collisions in the projection family \(P\) strongly enough to turn the local gains \(17/9\) and \(37/17\) into a global \(>1.95\) bound on \(\sum_{x\in M}p_x\).

That is the correct cross-fiber bottleneck; it is combinatorial and weighted, not entropic.

**PROVEN:** In the minimal-\(5\)-set regime, bad \(9/5\)-fibers carry edge labels \(e(T)\), and unions force support growth exactly as in Lemma 4 and Corollary 5.

**PROVEN:** If the support of the bad labels is pairwise intersecting, some element of \(M\) gets at least \((7/15)B\) global frequency contribution from the bad fibers; hence in any \(<0.39\) counterexample one must have either disjoint labels or \(B<117/140\).
