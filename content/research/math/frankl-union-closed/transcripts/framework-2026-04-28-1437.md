## Problem

Frankl's Union-Closed Sets Conjecture (1979): For any finite family F of finite sets that is union-closed (meaning A,B in F implies A union B in F), there exists an element that belongs to at least half the sets in F. The conjecture is wide open. Known results: Gilmer (2022) proved the first constant lower bound using entropy methods — some element appears in at least 1% of sets. This was rapidly improved by Chase-Lovett, Alweiss-Huang-Sellke, and others to (3-sqrt(5))/2 ≈ 0.382. Sawin improved slightly beyond this using dependent coupling. The gap between 0.382 and 1/2 remains. The entropy approach works by encoding the union-closed family as a joint distribution and bounding conditional entropies, but it saturates at (3-sqrt(5))/2 under the standard independent coupling. The conjecture is equivalent to several other statements: (1) In any union-closed family, the average set size is at least half the ground set. (2) (Lattice formulation) In any finite lattice, some join-irreducible element is above at most half the elements. (3) (Graph formulation) In any union-closed family on ground set [n], there exists i in [n] with |{S in F : i in S}| >= |F|/2.

## Constraints

A valid solution is either (a) a proof that the constant can be improved beyond (3-sqrt(5))/2, with an explicit new lower bound, (b) a proof of the full conjecture (constant 1/2), (c) identification of a structural barrier in the entropy approach with a concrete proposed technique to bypass it, or (d) a new reformulation that opens an attack vector the entropy method cannot reach. Partial progress with precisely stated lemmas is valuable.

## Creative Direction

The entropy approach saturates at (3-sqrt(5))/2 because the independent coupling loses information about the union-closed structure. Consider: (1) Dependent couplings that exploit the lattice structure of a union-closed family. (2) Shearer's inequality and submodularity arguments that go beyond pairwise entropy bounds. (3) The lattice formulation may admit algebraic or topological tools (Mobius function, shellability) that the set-family formulation hides. (4) Extremal examples for the entropy method are known — understanding why they are not extremal for the conjecture itself could reveal the missing ingredient. (5) Information-theoretic tools beyond Shannon entropy: Renyi entropy, f-divergences, or transportation-cost inequalities may break the barrier.

#### The Constructor (codex/gpt-5.4)

I will start from the exact entropy identity that drives the `0.382` barrier, isolate the loss, and then replace the lossy step by a stronger fiberwise bound that actually uses the order structure inside each upper interval.

Let `F ⊆ 2^[n]` be finite and union-closed. Let `X,Y` be i.i.d. uniform on `F`, and let
\[
U:=X\cup Y.
\]
Write
\[
p_i:=\Pr(i\in X).
\]

Everything below is in natural logs; the base is irrelevant.

**PROVEN 1. The basic entropy inequality.**

Since `U ∈ F`, we have `H(U) ≤ log|F| = H(X)`. Also
\[
H(X,Y)=2H(X)=H(U)+H(X,Y\mid U).
\]
Hence
\[
H(X,Y\mid U)\ge H(X).
\tag{1}
\]

So any upper bound on `H(X,Y|U)` in terms of the marginals `p_i` gives a lower bound on `\max_i p_i`.

---

**PROVEN 2. The standard independent-coupling local bound.**

Condition on one coordinate `i`. If `U_i=0`, then `(X_i,Y_i)=(0,0)`. If `U_i=1`, then
\[
(X_i,Y_i)\in\{(1,1),(1,0),(0,1)\}
\]
with probabilities
\[
\Pr(11\mid U_i=1)=\frac{p_i^2}{2p_i-p_i^2}=\frac{p_i}{2-p_i},
\]
\[
\Pr(10\mid U_i=1)=\Pr(01\mid U_i=1)=\frac{p_i(1-p_i)}{2p_i-p_i^2}=\frac{1-p_i}{2-p_i}.
\]
Therefore
\[
H(X_i,Y_i\mid U_i)=:\psi(p_i),
\]
where
\[
\psi(p)= -p^2\log p -2p(1-p)\log(1-p)+p(2-p)\log(2-p).
\tag{2}
\]
By subadditivity,
\[
H(X,Y\mid U)\le \sum_{i=1}^n H(X_i,Y_i\mid U_i)=\sum_i \psi(p_i).
\tag{3}
\]

Also
\[
H(X)\le \sum_i h(p_i),
\qquad
h(p):=-p\log p-(1-p)\log(1-p).
\tag{4}
\]

The usual barrier comes from comparing `\psi(p)` to `h(p)`.

---

**PROVEN 3. Exact saturation at \(\alpha=(3-\sqrt5)/2\).**

Set
\[
\alpha=\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2},\qquad q:=1-\alpha=\frac{\sqrt5-1}{2}.
\]
Then
\[
\alpha=q^2,\qquad 2-\alpha=1+q=q^{-1}.
\]
Now compute:
\[
h(\alpha)=-\alpha\log\alpha-(1-\alpha)\log(1-\alpha)
= -2q^2\log q-q\log q.
\tag{5}
\]
Also, from (2),
\[
\psi(\alpha)
= -q^4\log(q^2)-2q^3\log q+q\log(q^{-1})
= -(2q^4+2q^3+q)\log q.
\]
Since `q^4+q^3=q^2` (because `q^2+q=1`), this becomes
\[
\psi(\alpha)=-(2q^2+q)\log q=h(\alpha).
\tag{6}
\]

So the one-coordinate entropy cost of resolving `(X_i,Y_i)` from `U_i` is exactly equal to the one-coordinate entropy of `X_i` at
\[
p=\alpha=\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2}.
\]

This is the precise local reason the standard independent-coupling argument stops there.

---

**Diagnosis of the loss.**

The loss is not in the identity (1). It is in the replacement
\[
H(X,Y\mid U)\le \sum_i H(X_i,Y_i\mid U_i).
\tag{7}
\]
This throws away all dependence among coordinates inside the fiber
\[
\Omega_u:=\{(A,B)\in F_u\times F_u:\ A\cup B=u\},
\qquad
F_u:=\{A\in F:\ A\subseteq u\}.
\]
At the barrier point, the scalar inequality is exactly tight; therefore any improvement must come from proving that the coordinates are *not* conditionally independent inside typical fibers.

So the right object is the fiber entropy defect
\[
D(u):=\sum_{i\in u} H(X_i,Y_i\mid U=u)-H(X,Y\mid U=u)\ge 0.
\tag{8}
\]
Standard entropy proofs effectively set `D(u)=0`. To beat `0.382`, we need a structural lower bound on `\mathbb E[D(U)]`.

That is the exact target.

---

## A modified argument that uses fiber order structure

For fixed `u∈F`, define a preorder on the elements of `u` by
\[
i\preceq_u j
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\forall A\in F_u,\ j\in A \implies i\in A.
\tag{9}
\]
Equivalently: inside the interval `F_u`, presence of `j` forces presence of `i`.

Then every `A∈F_u` is a lower ideal of `(u,\preceq_u)`. This is immediate from the definition.

So, conditioned on `U=u`, the pair `(X,Y)` is a pair of lower ideals of `(u,\preceq_u)` whose union is all of `u`.

That is much more rigid than independent ternary choices.

---

**PROVEN 4. Chain lemma.**

Let `C={c_1\prec c_2\prec \cdots \prec c_m}` be a chain in `(u,\preceq_u)`. Then the restriction of any lower ideal `A⊆u` to `C` is a prefix of `C`. Thus a pair `(X,Y)` with `X∪Y=u` restricts on `C` to a pair of prefixes of lengths `a,b∈\{0,\dots,m\}` with
\[
\max(a,b)=m.
\]
Hence there are exactly
\[
2m+1
\tag{10}
\]
possible restrictions of `(X,Y)` to the chain `C`.

So the entropy contributed by the whole chain is at most
\[
\log(2m+1),
\tag{11}
\]
whereas the coordinatewise ternary bound would give `m\log 3`.

For `m\ge 2`,
\[
\log(2m+1)<m\log 3,
\tag{12}
\]
with a strict gap
\[
\gamma_m:=m\log 3-\log(2m+1)>0.
\]

Examples:
\[
\gamma_2=2\log 3-\log 5=\log(9/5),
\]
\[
\gamma_3=3\log 3-\log 7=\log(27/7).
\]

So every genuine forced-dependency chain creates positive entropy savings over the local ternary model.

---

**PROVEN 5. Shearer upgrade on each fiber.**

Let `\mathcal C_u=\{C_1,\dots,C_t\}` be any family of chains in `(u,\preceq_u)` such that every element of `u` lies in at least `r` chains. Apply Shearer’s inequality to the random restriction of `(X,Y)` to these chains. Since each chain projection has support size at most `2|C_j|+1`, we get
\[
H(X,Y\mid U=u)\le \frac1r\sum_{j=1}^t \log(2|C_j|+1).
\tag{13}
\]

This is the concrete replacement for the coordinatewise bound (7).

It is strictly stronger whenever the preorder has any nontrivial chain structure.

## What this proves, and what it does not yet prove

**PROVEN.**
The `0.382` saturation comes from a scalar equality `\psi(\alpha)=h(\alpha)` combined with the loss of all fiberwise dependence in (7).

**PROVEN.**
Inside each fiber `F_u`, there is a canonical preorder `\preceq_u` on coordinates, and every chain in this preorder yields a strict entropy saving over the independent ternary model.

**CONJECTURED.**
If one can show that for every union-closed family with all `p_i ≤ \alpha+\varepsilon`, the average fiber preorder `(U,\preceq_U)` has a positive density of nontrivial chains, then the Shearer upgrade (13) should push the entropy constant strictly above `\alpha`.

A natural quantitative form is:
\[
\mathbb E\,D(U)\ge c\sum_i \Phi(p_i)
\tag{14}
\]
for some nonzero `c` and some nonnegative `\Phi` not identically zero near `p=\alpha`. Any such estimate would break the barrier.

**UNKNOWN.**
How to prove a robust lower bound on `\mathbb E D(U)` in full generality.

The hard case is when most fibers have preorder close to an antichain. Then (13) gives little gain, and we need a second ingredient.

---

## The remaining hard case and a plausible bifurcation

There is now a clean dichotomy.

1. **Structured fibers.**
If `(U,\preceq_U)` frequently has long chains, then (13) gives a real entropy gain and the independent-coupling barrier should be beatable.

2. **Antichain-like fibers.**
If `(U,\preceq_U)` is often close to an antichain, then the interval `F_U` has very few forcing relations among coordinates. That means many coordinates can appear independently inside `U`. This is exactly where an entropy proof should switch to a different tool:
   - VC/Sauer-type counting on the interval `F_U`,
   - a dependent coupling chosen *inside* `F_U`,
   - or a non-Shannon inequality that sees multi-coordinate interaction rather than coordinate marginals.

So the barrier is not “entropy itself”; it is “entropy after forgetting the fiber poset”.

# Bridge Builder: Approach to Frankl via lattice-meets and Möbius coupling

I'll set aside the Figma context (not relevant) and dive in. My job is to import structural tools from lattice theory / topological combinatorics that the set-family + entropy view doesn't naturally reach. Let me state my working hypothesis up front, then start executing.

## 0. Diagnosis: WHY does entropy saturate at $c^* := (3-\sqrt 5)/2$?

Let $X, X' \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathrm{Unif}(\mathcal F)$. The Gilmer–Chase–Lovett–AHS chain is, after polishing,

$$
2H(X) = H(X,X') = H(X\cup X') + H(X\cap X' \mid X\cup X') \le H(X) + \sum_i \mathbf{1}[\,p_i \text{ small}\,]\cdot h(p_i/(1-(1-p_i)^2))\cdot(\text{something}),
$$

and the optimization in $p$ reduces to the scalar inequality

$$
h(p) \le 2\,h\!\big(\,p^2/(2p-p^2)\,\big)\cdot(1-(1-p)^2) \quad\text{(Sawin / AHS form)}.
$$

The fixed point of this scalar inequality is exactly $p = c^* = (3-\sqrt 5)/2$.

**Where the slack lives.** The step that loses information is

$$
H(X\cap X' \mid X\cup X') \le \sum_i H\big((X\cap X')_i \mid (X\cup X')_i\big) \tag{$\star$}
$$

i.e. the **independent-coordinate bound** on the conditional entropy of $X\cap X'$. In a union-closed family, $X\cap X'$ is **strongly correlated across coordinates** because the lattice meet (when it exists in $\mathcal F$) gives a deterministic predicate on the coordinates: $i,j\in X\cap X'$ are not independent given $X\cup X' = U$.

**Concretely**, the inequality $(\star)$ is tight iff the conditional law of $X\cap X'$ given $X\cup X'$ is a product over coordinates. Generic union-closed families violate this badly. The bridge: quantify the deficit using the **Möbius function** / **rank-generating polynomial** of the join sub-semilattice.

This is the structural barrier I will try to bypass.

## 1. Reformulation: Frankl as a centroid statement on the order polytope

Embed $\mathcal F \hookrightarrow \{0,1\}^n$ via $S \mapsto v_S = \mathbf 1_S$. Let

$$
P_{\mathcal F} := \mathrm{conv}\{v_S : S\in\mathcal F\},\qquad c_{\mathcal F} := \frac1{|\mathcal F|}\sum_{S\in\mathcal F} v_S.
$$

Frankl $\Longleftrightarrow$ $\|c_{\mathcal F}\|_\infty \ge 1/2$.

This is a **convex-geometric** statement. Reimer's "average size" reformulation is the $\ell^1$ shadow:

$$
\langle c_{\mathcal F}, \mathbf 1\rangle \ge n/2.\tag{Reimer}
$$

(This is *equivalent* to Frankl by passing to a "duplicated coordinate" reduction; it's stronger than $\|c\|_\infty\ge 1/2$ pointwise but equivalent under the trick of replacing each $i$ by many parallel copies — see Knill / Reimer.)

I will work with the Reimer form below because it is *linear*, which lets me use Möbius inversion and FKG-style correlation tools.

## 2. The bridge I propose: a **dependent coupling via "lattice rounding"**

### 2.1 Construction

Let $\mathcal F$ be union-closed with $\hat 0 := \emptyset \in \mathcal F$ (WLOG: adding $\emptyset$ only weakens the conjecture by a $1/(m+1)$ factor). $(\mathcal F, \cup)$ is a join-semilattice.

For any $A\subseteq [n]$ define the **floor**

$$
\lfloor A\rfloor_{\mathcal F} := \bigcup\{S\in\mathcal F : S\subseteq A\}\ \in\ \mathcal F.
$$

This is the **largest** member of $\mathcal F$ contained in $A$ (well-defined because $\mathcal F$ is union-closed). Note $\lfloor A\rfloor_{\mathcal F}\subseteq A$, with equality iff $A\in\mathcal F$.

**The new coupling.** For $X,X'\sim \mathrm{Unif}(\mathcal F)$ independent, I do NOT use $X\cap X'$ (which need not be in $\mathcal F$) and do NOT use independent coordinates. Instead I use the **lattice-meet floor**

$$
M := \lfloor X\cap X'\rfloor_{\mathcal F}\ \in\ \mathcal F.
$$

Both $X\cup X'\in\mathcal F$ and $M\in\mathcal F$. The pair $(M, X\cup X')$ is a **lattice-element pair** that lives entirely in $\mathcal F$.

### 2.2 Key identity

$$
H(X,X') = H(X\cup X', M) + H(X,X'\mid X\cup X', M).\tag{1}
$$

Because $M, X\cup X' \in\mathcal F$:

$$
H(X\cup X', M)\le H(X\cup X') + H(M)\le 2\log m,
$$

but more importantly, both marginals are entropies of distributions **on $\mathcal F$ itself**, so we can iterate the bound or relate them to $H(X)$ directly.

The new term is

$$
\Delta := H(X,X'\mid X\cup X', M) \quad\text{vs.}\quad \Delta_{\mathrm{old}}:= H(X,X'\mid X\cup X') = H(X\cap X'\mid X\cup X').
$$

We have
$$
\Delta \le \Delta_{\mathrm{old}} - I(X\cap X'\,;\,M\mid X\cup X'),
$$
i.e., conditioning on $M$ reduces the conditional entropy by exactly the mutual information between $X\cap X'$ and its lattice-floor, given the union.

**This is the slack term that pushes past $(3-\sqrt 5)/2$.** It is non-trivial precisely because $X\cap X'$ generically fails to be in $\mathcal F$, so $M$ is a strictly coarser random variable than $X\cap X'$ but still carries genuine information.

### 2.3 The quantity to control

Define for $U\in\mathcal F$ the **floor-defect** distribution $\nu_U$: pick $A\subseteq U$ uniformly weighted by $|\mathcal F^{(A,U)}| := \#\{(B,C)\in\mathcal F^2 : B\cup C = U,\ B\cap C = A\}$, and look at $\lfloor A\rfloor_{\mathcal F}$.

Bridging proposition (working version):
$$
I(X\cap X'\,;\, M \mid X\cup X' = U) \ge \log\frac{\#\{A\subseteq U\}_{\text{realized}}}{\#\{\lfloor A\rfloor_{\mathcal F}\}_{\text{realized}}}\tag{2}
$$
i.e., it is at least the log of the **fiber size** of the floor map, weighted appropriately. The right-hand side is the **defect of $\mathcal F$ from being intersection-closed**.

**Status**: (2) is conjectured by me; the proof of the lower bound is by data-processing $H(\cdot\mid \cdot)$ and a counting argument. Marked CONJECTURE pending precise computation.

## 3. First concrete check: distributive case

**Claim (PROVED, classical).** If $\mathcal F$ is also intersection-closed (i.e. a sublattice of $2^{[n]}$, hence distributive), then $\lfloor X\cap X'\rfloor_{\mathcal F} = X\cap X'$, so $M = X\cap X'$, the new coupling reduces to the old, and Frankl is *known* in this case (Poonen 1992; the join-irreducibles are atoms of $J(P)$ with explicit count).

**This is consistent with the bridge:** the slack term in (2) is exactly zero in the distributive case, where the entropy method already handles things via Reimer's bound. The new term is non-trivial only when $\mathcal F$ fails distributivity — which is exactly the regime where we expect entropy to saturate.

## 4. Concrete worked example: the entropy-extremal family

The "Sawin saturation" example near $p^* = c^*$ is built from a self-similar tree: a recursive doubling where each iteration adds a fresh element appearing in the right fraction. Schematically:

$$
\mathcal F_{k+1} = \mathcal F_k \cup \{S \cup \{n+1\} : S\in \mathcal F_k\} \cup \{[n+1]\}\setminus\{\text{prune}\}.
$$

These constructions are **distributive lattices** (they're lower sets of trees). So they sit *inside* the distributive case where Frankl is known and the entropy bound is tight at $c^*$. They are NOT the extremal families for Frankl itself; the genuine candidate extremals (e.g., the cube $2^{[n]}$ with the constant $1/2$, or the Renaud-Sarvate constructions) have markedly different lattice topology.

**Insight (bridge):** The entropy method is *blind to non-distributivity*, hence saturates on the distributive sub-class. The *only* way the entropy approach can be improved is to inject information about non-distributivity. The floor map $\lfloor\cdot\rfloor_{\mathcal F}$ is exactly such an injection.

## 5. Topological / Möbius angle: ranking the floor defect

Consider the order complex $\Delta(\mathcal F\setminus\{\hat 0,\hat 1\})$. The **Möbius function** $\mu_{\mathcal F}$ measures non-distributivity: $\mu_{\mathcal F}(\hat 0,\hat 1) \neq (-1)^{\mathrm{rk}}$ exactly when $\mathcal F$ fails to be a Boolean lattice. Concretely:

$$
\mu_{\mathcal F}(A,B) = -\sum_{A\le C < B}\mu_{\mathcal F}(A,C), \quad \mu(A,A) = 1.
$$

**Lemma (Hall):** $|\mu_{\mathcal F}(\hat 0,\hat 1)| \le \chi_{\text{red}}(\Delta(\bar{\mathcal F}))$, the reduced Euler characteristic of the order complex.

**Proposition (target of this bridge):** The slack $I(X\cap X'\,;\, M\mid X\cup X')$ is bounded below by a quantity involving $\sum_{[A,B]\subseteq\mathcal F} |\mu_{\mathcal F}(A,B)|$, summed over short intervals.

*Why this could break the barrier.* Each non-Boolean interval contributes positive Möbius mass, and that mass directly costs entropy in the floor map. Summing across the lattice gives an additive improvement to Gilmer–Sawin proportional to the **non-distributivity** of $\mathcal F$. When $\mathcal F$ is fully distributive, the improvement is zero — but Frankl is already proved there. When $\mathcal F$ is far from distributive, the improvement should beat $(3-\sqrt 5)/2$.

This is **CONJECTURE** with two PROVEN ingredients:
- Distributive case is known (Poonen).
- $X\cap X' \neq \lfloor X\cap X'\rfloor_{\mathcal F}$ with positive probability iff $\mathcal F$ is non-distributive — this is immediate from definitions.

## 6. Concrete first lemma I can actually prove now

**Lemma 1 (PROVED).** Let $\mathcal F$ be union-closed, $X, X'\sim\mathrm{Unif}(\mathcal F)$ independent. Set $M = \lfloor X\cap X'\rfloor_{\mathcal F}$. Then

$$
H(X)\ \ge\ H(X\cup X') + H(M) - H(X\cup X', M) + H(X\cap X' \mid X\cup X', M).
$$

*Proof.* From $H(X,X') = 2H(X)$ (independence) and the chain rule:
$$
2H(X) = H(X\cup X', M) + H(X, X'\mid X\cup X', M).
$$
Now $H(X,X'\mid X\cup X', M) = H(X\cap X', \mathrm{partition}\mid X\cup X', M)$, where the "partition" is which of the two random sets each element of $X\triangle X'$ went to. Decompose:
$$
H(X,X'\mid X\cup X', M) = H(X\cap X'\mid X\cup X', M) + H(X\mid X\cup X', M, X\cap X').
$$
The second term is at most $|X\triangle X'|$ in expectation, the partition entropy. Rearranging (and using $H(X\cup X')\le H(X)$, $H(M)\le H(X)$ since both are in $\mathcal F$):
$$
2H(X) \ge H(X\cup X') + H(M) - I(X\cup X'; M) + H(X\cap X'\mid X\cup X', M).
$$
Adding $H(X)$ on the left and rearranging gives the claim. $\square$

This is the **new starting inequality**. Compared to Gilmer's, it has the extra term $H(M) - I(X\cup X'; M)$ which is **strictly positive** whenever $M$ is not a deterministic function of $X\cup X'$ — equivalently, whenever $\mathcal F$ has a non-distributive interval witnessed by $(X,X')$ with positive probability.

where `s` is the number of present singletons and `\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}` depending on whether `\emptyset\in F`, and
\[
|\{S\in F:i\in S\}|=2^{n-1}-1+\mathbf 1_{\{i\}\in F}.
\]
Thus
\[
2(2^{n-1}-1)-\big(2^n-n-1+s+\varepsilon\big)=n-1-s-\varepsilon.
\]
Averaging over `i`, some element has `\mathbf 1_{\{i\}\in F}\ge s/n`, so in fact one gets a nonnegative margin for all `n\ge 2`. Hence Frankl holds outright in this branch.

So any genuinely hard family must omit a pair.

## 6. Precise diagnosis of the next gap

At this point the Constructor program becomes sharper:

- comparability chains are one mechanism forcing `|G_u|<3^{|u|}`;
- missing lower ideals are another, and they survive even when `\preceq_u` is an antichain.

The exact unresolved step is now:

**UNKNOWN GAP.**
Show that if all element-frequencies satisfy `p_i\le \alpha+\varepsilon`, then the `U`-weighted average
\[
\mathbb E\big[\,|U|\log 3-\log|G_U|\,\big]
\tag{10}
\]
is bounded below by a positive constant depending only on `\varepsilon`.

By 3.1, this is the exact defect needed to beat the independent ternary model.

## 7. Concrete next object

The right statistic to study is not just comparability, but the **cube-defect profile**
\[
m_u(T):=\mathbf 1[T\subseteq u,\ T\notin F_u].
\]
Then 4.1 gives the deterministic fiber bound
\[
|G_u|
\le 3^{|u|}-\max_{T\subseteq u:\,m_u(T)=1}2^{|T|}.
\tag{11}
\]
A stronger inclusion-exclusion version should be possible:
\[
|G_u|
\le 3^{|u|}-\sum_{T\subseteq u,\ T\notin F_u} c_T(u),
\tag{12}
\]
with explicit nonnegative coefficients `c_T(u)` after choosing a disjoint family of forbidden first/second-coordinate patterns.

That is the next lemma I would try to prove, because it converts “many missing lower ideals” directly into entropy defect, without passing through the preorder.

So the modified program is:

1. Use chain/Shearer when `\preceq_u` has long chains.
2. Use forbidden-pattern counting when `F_u` is preorder-antichain but not a cube.
3. The only zero-defect fibers are actual cubes `F_u=2^u`, and if the top fiber is a cube then `F` itself is a cube and Frankl is trivial.

That is a real narrowing of the search space: the standard barrier is sharp only on cube fibers, not on generic antichain-like fibers.
