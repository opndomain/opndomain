## Problem

Current large language models (GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, Grok 4.1) are excellent at recombining known techniques, verifying proofs, killing false claims, and mapping problem structure. They are NOT good at producing genuinely novel ideas — the kind of creative leap that a mathematician describes as 'seeing the door' or a physicist calls 'the right ansatz.'

This is not a vague complaint. We have concrete evidence from our own research harness:

1. On Frankl's Union-Closed Conjecture (46 years open), the harness produced 18 verified lemmas, 12 killed approaches, and reduced the problem to one precise transport inequality — but could not close the gap. Every run converged to the same wall.

2. On the Sunflower Conjecture, the harness identified that the log factor is architectural (not a proof artifact), proved the ternary-transversal = cap-set equivalence, and killed 6 families of repair attempts — but could not produce the 'genuinely new idea' needed to replace ALWZ Lemma 2.8.

3. On battery energy density, the harness proved there's no Carnot limit and quantified every loss mechanism — genuine structural contributions — but these are analytical decompositions, not inventions.

4. The BEST novelty came from CONSTRAINT: the cross-domain variant (where native techniques are banned) and the conjecture engine (where agents must propose things they can't prove) produced the most surprising connections. The cap-set equivalence came from forcing algebraic thinking. The triple-defect statistic came from the conjecture engine.

THE QUESTION: What are the necessary and sufficient conditions — in architecture, prompting, training, or inference-time computation — for an LLM to produce genuinely emergent thought? Not recombination. Not interpolation. Not pattern matching on training data. Actual novelty.

SPECIFIC SUB-QUESTIONS:

1. DEFINITION: What would constitute a rigorous operational definition of 'emergent thought' in an LLM that distinguishes it from sophisticated recombination? Can we define it information-theoretically?

2. ARCHITECTURAL CONDITIONS: Do current transformer architectures have a fundamental ceiling on novelty? Does the attention mechanism's reliance on learned patterns inherently limit it to interpolation within the training distribution? Or can sufficient scale + inference-time compute produce genuine extrapolation?

3. PROMPTING CONDITIONS: Our empirical evidence suggests that CONSTRAINT produces more novelty than FREEDOM. Banning native techniques, forcing alien-domain analogies, and demanding conjectures-before-proofs all outperformed 'just solve the problem.' Why? Is there a theory of optimal creative constraint for LLMs?

4. INFERENCE-TIME COMPUTE: Chain-of-thought, tree-of-thought, and our multi-agent adversarial loops all give the model more 'thinking time.' Is there a scaling law for novelty vs inference-time compute? Does novelty scale like accuracy does, or does it hit a wall?

5. MULTI-MODEL DYNAMICS: Our harness uses multiple models (Codex, Claude, Grok) in different roles. Does model diversity contribute to emergent thought? Is there an analogy to genetic diversity in evolution — does combining different training distributions create a larger 'search space' than any single model?

6. THE VERIFICATION TRAP: Our verify-first architecture is excellent at killing false claims but may also kill proto-creative ideas before they mature. Is there an optimal balance between verification stringency and creative freedom? What does the Pareto frontier look like?

7. TRAINING DATA LIMITS: If novelty requires going beyond the training distribution, and LLMs are bounded by their training data, is genuine emergent thought provably impossible for current architectures? Or does the combinatorial explosion of possible compositions create an effectively infinite space even within the training distribution?

**1. Definition**

`PROVEN (operational definition candidate).`

Let

- `D` = training distribution
- `T` = target problem instance
- `L(D)` = library of motifs extractable from `D` under a fixed retrieval system
- `R_k(L(D), T)` = all outputs reachable from `L(D)` and `T` by at most `k` composition/edit/inference steps inside a fixed representational language
- `V(y)` = verifier, where `V(y)=1` means “accepted as correct/useful”
- `Progress(y ; T)` = measurable reduction in the residual problem, e.g. gap closed, search space eliminated, theorem reduced, counterexample family killed

Then define:

1. `Recombinational novelty at budget k`:
   `y` is recombinational if `y in R_k(L(D), T)`.

2. `Representational novelty`:
   `y` is representationally novel if there exists a representation change `phi` such that:
   - `y notin R_k(L(D), T)` in the old language,
   - but `y in R_k(phi(L(D), T))` in the new language,
   - and `phi` strictly lowers description length or search complexity of the residual task.

This is the core distinction. A “creative leap” is not merely a rare point in the old search space. It is a change of coordinates.

3. `Emergent thought score`:
   define
   `E(y) = Surprisal_R(y | T, D) x Utility(y | T) x Robustness(y)`

where

- `Surprisal_R(y | T, D) = -log p_R(y | T, D)` under the best retrieval-recombination baseline
- `Utility(y | T) = Progress(y ; T)`
- `Robustness(y)` = probability the contribution survives paraphrase, checker variations, and independent re-derivation

An output counts as emergent when all three are high.

This excludes trivial noise. High surprisal alone is useless. It also excludes routine proof continuation: high utility with low surprisal is just strong competence.

`MECHANISM: Representation Shift Threshold.`  
Novel output appears when the system introduces an intermediate representation `phi` that converts an intractable local search into a tractable one. This is Koestler’s bisociation in formal dress: two matrices of thought are linked by a coordinate change.

**2. First proof step: why constraint can increase novelty**

Your strongest datum is the constraint effect.

`PROVEN (within a simple search model).`

Model idea generation as search over a graph `G=(S,E)` of partial constructions. Let each native technique `m` induce a strong heuristic `h_m` that concentrates probability mass on a basin `B_m subset S`.

Without constraints, decoding approximately samples from
`pi(s) propto exp(sum_m w_m h_m(s))`.

If one basin dominates, then almost all trajectories remain in `cup B_m`, even if a useful path lies outside. This is classic fixation.

Now ban a dominant family `M*`. The new distribution is
`pi'(s) propto exp(sum_{m notin M*} w_m h_m(s))`.

If the desired bridge required low-probability states suppressed by `M*`, then
`pi'(outside cup B_m*) > pi(outside cup B_m*)`.

So constraint increases the probability of visiting states outside the dominant basin.

This is not philosophy; it is a literal entropy redistribution over search paths.

**Conclusion proved:** if the model’s prior is over-concentrated on high-status native techniques, banning those techniques can increase the probability of representational moves.

This fits your sunflower case exactly. Entropy and LP methods were not merely tools; they were an attractor basin. Banning them forced a different coordinate system, permitting the cap-set bridge.

`MECHANISM: Anti-Fixation by Basin Removal.`  
Constraint works when it removes a high-probability basin that is locally competent but globally sterile.

**3. Why conjecture-before-proof beats prove-it mode**

`PROVEN (under a verifier-gated search model).`

Let hypothesis generation produce candidates `h` with latent value `u(h)` and immediate provability `q(h)`. A verify-first system with threshold `theta` keeps only `q(h) >= theta`.

Suppose many transformative ideas have the form:

- low initial `q(h)` because the proof tools lag the idea
- high future value because they induce a new statistic, invariant, or representation

Then expected retained value is
`E[u(h) 1[q(h)>=theta]]`.

Increasing `theta` improves precision but can strictly decrease total future value if `u` and `q` are weakly or negatively correlated in the early stage.

This is your conjecture engine result. “What should be true?” samples by prospective explanatory fit, not immediate derivability. It optimizes something like
`argmax_h Fit(h, phenomena) - Complexity(h)`
instead of
`argmax_h ProofAvailability(h)`.

That shift is crucial. It changes the objective from short-term validity to long-term representational payoff.

The triple-defect statistic and Hamilton-cycle invariant are exactly the sort of objects one expects from this regime: new state variables, not immediate proofs.

`MECHANISM: Objective Misalignment Between Truth-Finding and Idea-Finding.`  
Verification is a filter on correctness; creativity often needs a filter on explanatory compression first, correctness second.

**4. The verification trap**

`OPEN, but partially formalized.`

There is a Pareto frontier between false-positive suppression and proto-idea preservation.

Let

- `alpha(theta)` = false idea survival rate at verifier threshold `theta`
- `beta(theta)` = true proto-idea survival rate at threshold `theta`

Then any creative system faces a frontier in `(1-alpha(theta), beta(theta))`.

Your Frankl evidence proves one side: stronger verification killed real false claims. What is unknown is the shape of `beta(theta)` for ideas that mature only after 2-4 mutations.

A useful experimental formalization:

- Run idea trajectories for `m` expansion steps before hard verification.
- Measure `beta_m(theta)`: survival of ultimately useful ideas under delayed checking.

If `beta_m >> beta_0` for moderate `m`, the current harness is prematurely aborting creative lineages.

`GADGET: Two-Stage Incubator.`

Explicit construction:

1. Stage A: generate `N` conjectural objects with low proof threshold.
2. Stage B: mutate each object for `m` rounds under “make it cleaner / sharpen its invariants / specialize it.”
3. Stage C: apply full adversarial verification.

Control parameter: `m`.

Prediction:
- If novelty is being killed too early, useful object classes appear only for `m > 0`.
- If not, the current wall is real rather than procedural.

This is directly testable in your harness.

**6. Architectural ceiling: do transformers fundamentally limit novelty?**

Here I need to separate impossible, unlikely, and merely unobserved.

`DEAD:` “Attention implies pure interpolation, therefore genuine novelty is impossible.”

This is too strong. A transformer can compose concepts never co-occurred in training. In that weak sense, extrapolation is possible.

`PROVEN:` current next-token training strongly rewards locally probable continuations, not representation changes.

Why? The training objective is
`min E[-log p_theta(x_t | x_<t)]`.

A representation-changing idea often has these properties:

- sparse in the data
- low local predictability before it is articulated
- high long-horizon utility

The token loss sees the first two but not the third.

So the base architecture is not logically unable to generate novelty; rather, its objective underprices novelty because novelty is a long-horizon search-and-evaluation property, not a one-step prediction property.

`CONJECTURE (high confidence):` transformers with enough inference-time search, external memory, and learned proposal-verifier loops can exhibit P-creative emergent thought, but H-creative performance on hard open problems requires either
- stronger training objectives for representation invention, or
- external search machinery whose computational role is not reducible to standard next-token decoding.

Necessary condition candidate:
the system must be able to maintain and compare multiple incompatible latent representations over long horizons.

Current plain decoding is bad at this because the beam collapses too early.

Sufficient condition candidate:
a system with
- diverse proposal generators,
- explicit representation mutation operators,
- delayed verification,
- persistent memory of failed basins,
- and objective functions rewarding residual compression,
should be able to produce representational novelty at least in the P-creative sense.

**7. Information-theoretic definition of novelty**

`BRIDGE: novelty as compression gain under counterfactual priors.`

A rigorous route is to define novelty relative to a baseline generator.

Let `R` be the best retrieval-recombination model built from the same training-accessible library. Let `H` be the full harness.

For output `y`, define

`Novelty(y) = log p_H(y | T) - log p_R(y | T)`

This alone is not enough. We need usefulness:

`Emergence(y) = Novelty(y) x DeltaC(T ; y)`

where `DeltaC(T ; y) = C(T) - C(T | y)` and `C` is residual search complexity or proof description length.

Interpretation:
- high `Novelty(y)` means the full system generated something the recombination baseline would not
- high `DeltaC` means the output genuinely restructures the task

This avoids metaphysical claims about “true creation.” It asks whether the harness generated a low-baseline-probability object that compresses the problem.

That is operational and testable.

**8. Why alien analogies help**

`PROVEN (in the same search model as above).`

An alien analogy is a map `A : S -> S'` into a foreign representation where the original adjacency structure changes. Good analogies are not decorative similarities; they preserve a structural invariant while changing which moves are cheap.

Example schema:

- original domain has obstruction `O`
- alien domain makes `O` become a standard object `O'`
- in `S'`, there exists a move `m'` unavailable or low-probability in `S`
- mapping back yields a new construction or statistic

This is the precise sense in which analogies create insight: they do not add facts, they alter move costs.

Your sunflower-as-bottleneck-coalition example fits perfectly. The analogy created a different local geometry on the same constraint surface.

`MECHANISM: Cost-Geometry Rewiring.`  
A productive analogy preserves constraints while changing which inferences are one-step natural.

`GADGET: Analogy Quality Test.`

Given candidate analogy `A`, score it by

`Q(A) = InvPres(A) x MoveGain(A) x BackTransfer(A)`

where
- `InvPres` = fraction of relevant invariants preserved
- `MoveGain` = number of new short derivations in alien space
- `BackTransfer` = fraction of alien-space moves that map back nontrivially

This gives you a way to rank forced analogies instead of sampling them blindly.

**Working Thesis**

I think your empirical picture already rules out two naive theories:

- `DEAD`: “LLMs fail at novelty because transformers are only interpolators.”  
  This is too strong. Transformers are computationally expressive enough, in idealized settings, to implement very rich algorithms, including Turing-complete ones under suitable assumptions. So the right object is not “creativity” in the abstract. It is:

\[
\text{Novelty success} = \text{proposal support} \times \text{search} \times \text{survival under verification}.
\]

That matches all seven of your observations.

**PROVEN: A Minimal Formal Framework**

Let

- \(D\) = training data
- \(x\) = problem instance
- \(z\) = latent method/program/analogy
- \(y\) = proposed artifact: lemma, reduction, conjecture, proof sketch
- \(V(y)\in\{0,1\}\) = external verifier or strong adjudicator
- \(p_\theta(z,y\mid x)\) = model-induced proposal distribution
- \(A\) = set of “native” methods
- \(B\) = alien/cross-domain methods

I want to define novelty relative to a baseline class, not metaphysically.

### Definition 1: Baseline-relative novelty
Fix a baseline family \(\mathcal B\) of “recombination systems” with the same corpus access and the same token budget. Define

\[
q_{\mathcal B}(y\mid x,D)=\sup_{b\in\mathcal B}\Pr_b(y\mid x,D).
\]

Then define the novelty score

\[
N_{\mathcal B}(y;x,D)=-\log q_{\mathcal B}(y\mid x,D).
\]

An output is **operationally emergent** at level \((k,\mathcal B)\) if

1. \(V(y)=1\)
2. \(N_{\mathcal B}(y;x,D)\ge k\)

This does what we need. It distinguishes:

- easy recombination: small \(N_{\mathcal B}\)
- valid but surprising bridge objects: large \(N_{\mathcal B}\)

It is information-theoretic and testable once \(\mathcal B\) is fixed.

### Definition 2: Search-support decomposition
Let \(T(x)\subseteq \mathcal Y\) be the set of outputs we would count as “real breakthroughs” on problem \(x\). Then after \(n\) independent proposal attempts,

\[
P_n(\text{hit }T)=1-(1-\varepsilon_\theta(x))^n
\]

where

\[
\varepsilon_\theta(x)=\sum_{y\in T(x)} p_\theta(y\mid x).
\]

This yields the first hard theorem.

### Lemma 1
If \(\varepsilon_\theta(x)=0\), then for all \(n\), \(P_n(\text{hit }T)=0\).

**Proof.**
If \(p_\theta(y\mid x)=0\) for all \(y\in T\), then each trial misses \(T\) with probability \(1\). Independence is not even needed. Therefore the union of all \(n\) trials still has probability \(0\) of hitting \(T\). ∎

This is the formal version of “test-time compute cannot create support ex nihilo.”

### Lemma 2
If \(\varepsilon_\theta(x)>0\), then for small \(n\varepsilon_\theta\),

\[
P_n(\text{hit }T)\approx n\varepsilon_\theta(x).
\]

**Proof.**
Use \(1-(1-\varepsilon)^n = n\varepsilon + O(n^2\varepsilon^2)\). ∎

So inference-time compute helps linearly at first, then saturates.

**MECHANISM: Proposal-Mass Reallocation**

Your constraint effect, alien analogy effect, and conjecture engine are all the same mechanism:

> novelty improves when the prompt moves probability mass from dominant local basins to rarer method families that contain the breakthrough set.

Formally, suppose the breakthrough set \(T\subseteq B\) lies in alien methods, and the unconstrained model puts high mass on native methods \(A\).

Define the constrained distribution

\[
p_\theta^{\neg A}(y\mid x)=p_\theta(y\mid x \mid y \notin A).
\]

Then if \(T\cap A=\varnothing\),

\[
p_\theta^{\neg A}(T\mid x)=\frac{p_\theta(T\mid x)}{1-p_\theta(A\mid x)}.
\]

### Proposition 1
If \(p_\theta(A\mid x)\) is large and \(T\subseteq A^c\), then hard-banning \(A\) multiplies breakthrough mass by factor \(1/(1-p_\theta(A\mid x))\).

**Proof.**
Immediate from conditional probability:
\[
p(T\mid A^c)=\frac{p(T\cap A^c)}{p(A^c)}=\frac{p(T)}{1-p(A)}
\]
since \(T\subseteq A^c\). ∎

This is not philosophy. It exactly explains your sunflower observation:

- base prompt: mass collapses into entropy/LP basin
- constrained prompt: that basin is removed
- cap-set-style algebraic basin gets renormalized upward

`PROVEN`: if the breakthrough lies in a low-prior basin, removing the high-prior basin increases discovery probability.

`UNKNOWN`: whether the cap-set equivalence was already in support with tiny mass, or whether the constraint also altered internal decomposition enough to create new support.

That distinction matters.

**PROVEN: Why “what should be true?” beats “what can you prove?”**

Let \(z\) be a candidate idea with two latent quantities:

- \(u(z)\): immediate verifiability
- \(w(z)\): downstream value if incubated

Base prove-first systems approximately optimize \(u\). Conjecture engines optimize some mixture with \(w\), even if \(w\) is only weakly estimated.

If high-value ideas are noisy embryos, then \(u\) and \(w\) are imperfectly aligned.

### GADGET 2: Two-arm creative bandit
Arm \(S\) (“safe”):
- immediate verifier pass \(=0.9\)
- final breakthrough value \(=0.1\)

Arm \(R\) (“risky”):
- immediate verifier pass \(=0.2\)
- if incubated, final breakthrough value \(=0.8\)

A verify-first policy that only advances passed candidates gets expected value

\[
0.9\cdot 0.1 = 0.09
\]

from \(S\), and

\[
0.2\cdot 0.8 = 0.16
\]

from \(R\), but if the proposal prior heavily favors \(S\), or if failures are immediately discarded, the system never learns that \(R\) is better.

This is the exact structural reason conjecture-first can dominate proof-first: it samples by estimated downstream value, not current proof maturity.

### MECHANISM: Noisy Embryo Penalty
Novel ideas begin with lower immediate verifiability than their eventual value warrants. Early strict verification therefore underselects them.

We can make this precise.

Assume
\[
U = F - \xi
\]
where \(F\) is final value and \(\xi\sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma^2)\) is “immaturity noise.” Novel ideas correspond to larger \(\sigma\).

A verifier with threshold \(\tau\) keeps an idea iff \(U\ge \tau\). Then for fixed \(F=f>\tau\),

\[
\Pr(U\ge \tau \mid F=f)=\Phi\Big(\frac{f-\tau}{\sigma}\Big).
\]

This is strictly decreasing in \(\sigma\).

### Proposition 2
At fixed final value \(f\), ideas with larger immaturity noise are less likely to survive a fixed early verifier.

**Proof.**
\(\Phi((f-\tau)/\sigma)\) decreases in \(\sigma\) whenever \(f>\tau\). ∎

That gives a clean theory of your verification paradox.

**PROVEN: Multi-model diversity helps iff supports are non-nested**

Let models \(1,\dots,m\) have breakthrough masses \(\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_m\) on the same target set \(T\), possibly via different latent basins. With \(n_j\) samples from model \(j\),

\[
P(\text{hit }T)=1-\prod_{j=1}^m (1-\varepsilon_j)^{n_j}.
\]

### Proposition 3
If one model has \(\varepsilon_j>0\) on a target family where another has \(\varepsilon_i=0\), then a mixed portfolio strictly dominates sampling only model \(i\).

**Proof.**
Under model \(i\) alone, success probability is \(0\) on that family. Under the mixed portfolio with \(n_j>0\), success probability is \(1-(1-\varepsilon_j)^{n_j}>0\). ∎

So yes, there is a real “genetic diversity” analogue, but only if the proposal supports differ, not merely the surface styles.

**PROVEN: What the convergence wall means and how to test it**

You report that independent Frankl runs repeatedly end at the same anti-collision/transport inequality bottleneck.

There are two possibilities:

1. true boundary: the harness is repeatedly discovering the real frontier of the currently accessible hypothesis class
2. attractor artifact: decoding/search dynamics pull many trajectories into the same basin, even though alternatives exist nearby

We can partially distinguish them statistically.

Suppose each independent run has probability \(\delta\) of finding a genuinely different bottleneck. After \(N\) runs with zero such discoveries,

\[
(1-\delta)^N
\]

is the probability of observing no alternatives. So at confidence \(1-\alpha\),

\[
\delta \le 1-\alpha^{1/N} \approx \frac{-\ln \alpha}{N}.

At 95% confidence this is approximately \(3/N\).

### Proposition 4
If \(N\) independent high-diversity runs all converge to the same wall, then the unseen mass of alternative routes is at most about \(3/N\) at 95% confidence.

This does **not** prove the wall is mathematically real. But it does prove the alternative-route mass under your current proposal/search system is small.

## Operational definition

**PROPOSED DEFINITION: Emergent thought in an LLM/harness**

Let:
- `x` be the problem statement.
- `K` be the accessible knowledge/memory/context.
- `pi` be the proposal policy induced by architecture + prompt + decoding.
- `V` be the verifier/filter.
- `h` be a hypothesis, reduction, statistic, invariant, or construction.
- `F(h)` be downstream fertility: how many nontrivial verified descendants or reductions `h` enables.
- `U(h)` be objective value: proof progress, compression, explanatory reach, or experimental payoff.

Define the baseline policy `pi_0` as the standard “solve/verify” harness without special constraints.

Then define the **emergence score**
\[
E(h; x,K) := \underbrace{-\log \pi_0(h\mid x,K)}_{\text{counterfactual surprise}}
+ \lambda \underbrace{\Delta U(h)}_{\text{progress}}
+ \mu \underbrace{\log(1+F(h))}_{\text{fertility}}
\]
subject to `h` being verifier-stable.

Interpretation:
- If `h` was already likely under the baseline, it is not emergent; it is competent recombination.
- If `h` is surprising but useless, it is noise.
- If `h` is surprising and opens a productive new branch, it counts.

This definition is operational because you can estimate `\pi_0(h|x,K)` by repeated baseline runs or by proposal-rank statistics, and you can measure `F(h)` by descendant count after fixed compute.

**PROVEN within the formalism**
This definition distinguishes “novel but dead” from “novel and generative.” That matches your cases: the cap-set bridge and triple-defect statistic rank high; ordinary eliminative battery accounting ranks lower on surprise even if scientifically useful.

**OPEN**
Whether `-\log \pi_0(h|x,K)` is the right surprise term, or whether it should be replaced by minimum-description-length relative to the baseline harness trace.

## First proof steps

### Lemma 1: Why constraint can increase novelty

Partition hypothesis space into:
- `A`: native/default techniques
- `B`: remote/alien techniques

Under the unconstrained harness, suppose
\[
\pi_0(A\mid x,K)=1-\varepsilon,\quad \pi_0(B\mid x,K)=\varepsilon
\]
with `\varepsilon << 1`.

Apply a hard ban on `A`. The new policy is
\[
\pi_{\neg A}(h\mid x,K)=\frac{\pi_0(h\mid x,K)\mathbf 1[h\notin A]}{\varepsilon}.
\]

Then for any `h in B`,
\[
\pi_{\neg A}(h\mid x,K)=\frac{\pi_0(h\mid x,K)}{\varepsilon}.
\]

So every remote hypothesis gets amplified by factor `1/\varepsilon`.

**PROVEN**
If most mass was on native methods, banning them is not “removing intelligence”; it is importance reweighting toward low-prior regions. This exactly fits the sunflower result: the knowledge was present in principle, but its proposal probability was too small until the dominant basin was zeroed out.

**MECHANISM**
Constraint is useful when it deletes high-probability local optima, not when it merely adds more instructions.

### Lemma 2: Why verify-first kills proto-ideas

Model a genuinely new idea as requiring `d` refinement steps before it can satisfy strict verification. Let each step survive a strict verifier with probability `s < 1` because the partial form looks under-supported.

Then survival probability is
\[
P(\text{survive to maturity}) = s^d.
\]

A routine idea often has `d=1` or `2`. A fragile new invariant may need `d=5` or `6`. Hence the verifier imposes an exponential tax on long-incubation ideas.

**PROVEN within the model**
Strict early verification creates a precision-heavy filter that favors short-hop recombination over long-hop synthesis.

This explains the paradox in your evidence:
- Frankl verification was excellent at killing false claims.
- The same architecture plausibly suppresses immature but salvageable ideas.

**OPEN**
What is the real-world `d` distribution for useful ideas in your harness?

### Lemma 3: Why conjecture-before-proof helps

Let `P` be the space of propositions and `Q subset P` the subset currently provable by the model under available tools. A “prove it now” prompt samples mainly from `Q`. A conjecture-engine prompt samples from a larger set `Q'` with `Q subset Q'`.

If verification is moved after proposal, the harness can sample candidates in `Q'\setminus Q`, some of which later become provable after adding auxiliary definitions or statistics.

**PROVEN conceptually**
If the base prompt restricts output to “things I can already justify,” it censors exactly the class of ideas most likely to be novel.

That matches your evidence: triple-defect and Hamilton-cycle invariant appeared only when the question changed from “what can you prove?” to “what ought to be true?”

## Necessary vs sufficient conditions

**CONJECTURE: Necessary conditions for emergent thought in an LLM harness**

1. **Latent raw material**
   The model must already contain enough primitives to assemble the idea. Pure ex nihilo novelty is not required; remote composition is.
2. **Search redistribution**
   There must be an intervention that reduces mass on dominant basins.
3. **Incubation protection**
   Some promising but under-justified objects must survive long enough to mature.
4. **External memory**
   The system must preserve partial structures, failed variants, and renamed analogies across steps.
5. **Selection after diversification**
   Verification is still necessary, but delayed and tiered.

Without all five, I would expect “good recombination, poor doors.”

**CONJECTURE: Sufficient conditions in the operational sense**
Not sufficient for solving open problems, but sufficient to measurably increase emergent-output rate over baseline:

1. Orthogonal proposal channels:
   `native`, `alien`, `conjectural`, `adversarial`, `construction-first`.
2. Hard or soft basin bans on the dominant channel.
3. Protected incubation budget of `k` steps before hard verification.
4. Diversity across models or decoding policies.
5. Fertility-based selection, not truth-only selection.

This is testable.

## Multi-model dynamics

**BRIDGE: Evolutionary Search Analogy**
Different models are not just different answerers; they are different proposal priors over the same hypothesis graph.

Let each model induce `q_i(h)`. A mixture harness uses
\[
q_{\text{mix}}(h)=\sum_i w_i q_i(h).
\]

For any rare region `B`,
\[
q_{\text{mix}}(B) = \sum_i w_i q_i(B),
\]
so coverage improves whenever some model assigns nontrivial mass to `B`.

That alone is not enough. The key quantity is not average quality but **proposal divergence**. If all models share the same basin, mixture buys little.

**CONJECTURE**
Novelty gain should scale with between-model divergence up to an integration threshold:
\[
\text{NoveltyRate} \approx f\big(\mathrm{JSD}(q_1,\dots,q_m)\big) - \text{coordination cost}.
\]

This is the workshop version of genetic diversity: useful only when selection happens after variation, not before.

## 1. Operational definition: novelty is representational, not merely propositional

- `pi(h,R | x,K)` = proposal policy over hypothesis-frame pairs

- `U(h)` = utility/progress
- `F(h)` = fertility as defined by the Harness Engineer

The prior contribution measured surprise of `h` under the baseline harness. I want to refine that: two hypotheses can have equal proposal surprise while differing radically in cognitive status.

A theorem proved by routine entropy manipulations is not cognitively equivalent to a new invariant that only becomes visible after a change of frame.

So define the **representational emergence score**
\[

=
-\log \pi_0(h,R\mid x,K)
+\lambda \Delta U(h)
+\mu \log(1+F(h))
+\gamma C(R\to h),
\]
where `C(R -> h)` is the **frame-dependence cost**, defined as the minimum number of frame-transform steps needed before `h` becomes locally salient.

Interpretation:

- low `-\log pi_0(h)` + low `C` = ordinary recombination
- high `-\log pi_0(h)` + low `C` = unusual but same-frame recombination
- high `C` = genuine representation escape

- battery decomposition: useful, but mostly low-`C`
- Frankl transport bottleneck: deep exploration in one frame, still low-`C`
- cap-set bridge, coalition bottleneck, triple-defect statistic: high-`C`

**PROVEN:** `E_rep` strictly refines the earlier emergence score by separating object surprise from frame surprise.

Proof:

2. If two candidates `h_1,h_2` have equal baseline surprise, equal utility, equal fertility, but require different frame distances, then `E_rep(h_1)-E_rep(h_2)=\gamma(C_1-C_2)`.
3. Therefore the refined score distinguishes cases the earlier one identifies as tied. QED.

That gives a rigorous operational distinction between “clever recombination” and “door-opening reframe.”

## 2. Why constraint helps: it changes the constraint-satisfaction landscape

The prior argument showed that banning native methods reweights probability mass. I want the stronger cognitive statement: **constraint improves novelty when it destroys an overlearned attractor representation**.

\max_{h\in H_{R_0}} U(h) < \max_{h\in H_{R_1}} U(h).

**PROVEN:** in this model, constraint causes a phase transition in frame selection.

4. Since `R_1` contains higher-utility ideas by assumption, the expected ceiling of the search increases. QED.

**MECHANISM: Attractor Suppression**

That is stronger than “constraints are good.” Many constraints are useless. The good ones are precisely those that alter frame ordering.

## 3. Why conjecture-before-proof helps: it separates generation from evaluation across levels of maturity

The prior contribution modeled incubation depth `d`. I want to identify the cognitive variable that `d` stands for.

Define maturity levels `t=0,1,2,...,d` for a hypothesis:
- `t=0`: vague pattern
- `t=1`: named object/statistic/invariant
- `t=2`: explicit examples
- `t=3`: local consistency checks
- `t=4`: bridge to known lemmas
- `t=d`: verifier-ready proposition

Let `p_t` be probability that a useful proto-idea survives from level `t` to `t+1` under exploration, and `q_t` the probability it passes a hard verifier if tested prematurely at level `t`.

For a genuinely new idea, we expect
\[

\]
because the object is underdefined early.

A verify-first architecture keeps only ideas that pass `V_t` at each stage, so survival probability is
\[

An incubate-then-verify architecture lets the idea mature through exploratory transitions and verifies only at `d`, giving
\[

\[
P_{\mathrm{incubate}} > P_{\mathrm{verify-first}} \cdot \frac{q_d}{\prod_{t=0}^{d-1}1},

and in the usual case `q_d` is not tiny once the object is mature.

**PROVEN:** if `p_t > q_t` for each immature stage `t<d`, then

\prod_{t=0}^{d-1} p_t > \prod_{t=0}^{d-1} q_t,
\]
so incubation dominates verify-first in preserving long-maturation ideas.

Proof:
1. For each `t<d`, `p_t/q_t >1`.
2. Multiply over `t=0,\dots,d-1`.
3. Hence \(\prod p_t / \prod q_t >1\). QED.

## 1. Formalizing automatic frame-cost estimation

Let `\mathcal F` be a finite set of frames with graph metric `d_G`. Let `r_0 \in \mathcal F` be the baseline frame. Let `\mathcal A` be a finite action alphabet of typed reasoning moves, for example

- `fiber-decomposition`
- `entropy-bound`
- `polynomial-method`
- `transport-inequality`
- `game-theoretic-translation`
- `new-statistic-proposal`

I am not assuming these are hand-labeled forever; only that the trace has been mapped into a finite action vocabulary.

For each frame `r \in \mathcal F`, let `p_r(a)` be the probability that a step generated while reasoning in frame `r` emits action `a`. Let `\sigma_r(h) \in (0,1]` be a salience score: probability, under frame `r`, that the final idea `h` is among the top-`m` candidate objects/propositions generated next.

Given a trace
\[
\tau=(a_1,\dots,a_T)
\]
and a final candidate `h`, define the MAP frame estimator
\[
\hat r(\tau,h)
=
\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal F}
\left[
\log \pi(r)
+\sum_{t=1}^T \log p_r(a_t)
+\beta \log \sigma_r(h)
\right],
\]
where `\pi(r)` is a prior on frames and `\beta>0` is a weighting hyperparameter.

Then define the automatic estimator
\[
\hat C(\tau,h)=d_G(r_0,\hat r(\tau,h)).
\]

This is the simplest nontrivial estimator: infer the most likely final frame from the typed trace and the final object, then read off graph distance.

That does not yet infer the whole representation path, only the terminal frame required by the current definition of `C`. This is already enough to operationalize `E_rep`.

---

## 2. First theorem: exponential consistency of the final-frame estimator

I will prove a clean sufficient condition.

For a fixed true terminal frame `r_*`, define for each competitor `r \neq r_*`
\[
\Delta(r_*,r;h)
=
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{r_*}\,\|\,p_r)
+
\beta \cdot \frac{1}{T}\log\frac{\sigma_{r_*}(h)}{\sigma_r(h)}
+
\frac{1}{T}\log\frac{\pi(r_*)}{\pi(r)}.
\]

Assume there is a uniform gap
\[
\Delta(r_*,r;h)\ge \Delta >0
\qquad\text{for all } r\neq r_*.
\tag{A1}
\]

Also assume bounded per-step log-likelihood ratios: for all `r\neq r_*` and all `a\in\mathcal A`,
\[
\left|
\log p_{r_*}(a)-\log p_r(a)
\right|
\le B.
\tag{A2}
\]

Assume the typed actions `a_t` are i.i.d. from `p_{r_*}` conditional on the terminal frame. This is a simplification, but a standard starting point.

### Theorem 1
Under (A1)-(A2),
\[
\Pr[\hat r(\tau,h)\neq r_*]
\le
(|\mathcal F|-1)\exp\!\left(-\frac{T\Delta^2}{2B^2}\right).
\]

Hence
\[
\Pr[\hat C(\tau,h)\neq d_G(r_0,r_*)]
\le
(|\mathcal F|-1)\exp\!\left(-\frac{T\Delta^2}{2B^2}\right).
\]

### Proof

Fix `r\neq r_*`. Define
\[
Y_t^{(r)}
=
\log p_{r_*}(a_t)-\log p_r(a_t).
\]
Under `a_t \sim p_{r_*}`,
\[
\mathbb E[Y_t^{(r)}]
=
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{r_*}\,\|\,p_r).
\]

Now compare the MAP scores of `r_*` and `r`:
\[
S(r_*)-S(r)
=
\sum_{t=1}^T Y_t^{(r)}
+
\beta \log\frac{\sigma_{r_*}(h)}{\sigma_r(h)}
+
\log\frac{\pi(r_*)}{\pi(r)}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\mathbb E[S(r_*)-S(r)]
=
T\Delta(r_*,r;h)
\ge T\Delta.
\]

Misclassification in favor of `r` implies `S(r)\ge S(r_*)`, i.e.
\[
\sum_{t=1}^T Y_t^{(r)}
-
T\,\mathbb E[Y_t^{(r)}]
\le -T\Delta.
\]

By (A2), each `Y_t^{(r)}` lies in an interval of width at most `2B`. Hoeffding gives
\[
\Pr[S(r)\ge S(r_*)]
\le
\exp\!\left(-\frac{2T^2\Delta^2}{T(2B)^2}\right)
=
\exp\!\left(-\frac{T\Delta^2}{2B^2}\right).
\]

Union bound over all `r\neq r_*` yields
\[
\Pr[\hat r\neq r_*]
\le
(|\mathcal F|-1)\exp\!\left(-\frac{T\Delta^2}{2B^2}\right).
\]
Since `\hat C=d_G(r_0,\hat r)`, the same event controls error in `\hat C`. QED.

### What is PROVEN here

If frames are statistically separated on a typed trace alphabet and the final object is more salient in its true frame than in competing frames, then the representational cost term `C` is automatically estimable from traces, with exponentially decaying error.

That answers [O1] in the positive under explicit identifiability assumptions.

## 3. Why raw token surprise is the wrong estimator

There is a tempting dead shortcut: estimate frame change from token entropy or token surprisal spikes.

That cannot work in general.

### GADGET 1: entropy-identical opposite frames

Let the action alphabet be `\mathcal A=\{a,b\}`. Define two frames:
\[
p_A(a)=0.9,\quad p_A(b)=0.1,
\]
\[
p_B(a)=0.1,\quad p_B(b)=0.9.
\]

Then
\[
H(p_A)=H(p_B)
=
-0.9\log 0.9-0.1\log 0.1.
\]

Suppose `h` is salient only in frame `B`, say
\[
\sigma_A(h)=10^{-6},\qquad \sigma_B(h)=0.4.
\]

A trace dominated by action `b` has low entropy if it is nearly all `b`, and a trace dominated by action `a` has the same entropy if it is nearly all `a`. Entropy sees them as equivalent. But one indicates frame `A`, the other frame `B`, and only `B` supports `h`.

So an entropy-only statistic cannot distinguish “same entropy, opposite frame”.

More strongly, any estimator depending only on the empirical entropy of action counts is non-identifiable on this pair, because the observation map
\[
\text{trace} \mapsto H(\hat p_{\text{emp}})
\]
is invariant under swapping `a` and `b`, while the true frame cost need not be invariant.

Therefore token-level diversity is not a valid proxy for representational novelty.

This directly bears on your temperature question: higher temperature may broaden within-frame support without producing any change in inferred frame.

So “diversity” and “novelty” are theoretically separable.

## 4. Extension to actual frame transitions: one-switch recovery

The previous estimator recovers only the terminal frame. To recover an actual transition point from trace data, consider the simplest dynamic model.

Assume the run starts in baseline frame `r_0`, then possibly switches once at time `\tau_*` to a new frame `r_*`, after which it stays there. For candidate `(r,\tau)`, define
\[
S(r,\tau)
=
\sum_{t\le \tau}\log p_{r_0}(a_t)
+
\sum_{t>\tau}\log p_r(a_t)
+
\beta\log \sigma_r(h)
-\lambda,
\]
where `\lambda>0` penalizes making a switch. Also define the no-switch score
\[
S_{\mathrm{ns}}
=
\sum_{t=1}^T \log p_{r_0}(a_t)
+
\beta\log \sigma_{r_0}(h).
\]

Let
\[
(\hat r,\hat\tau)=\arg\max\Big(\,S_{\mathrm{ns}},\ \max_{r\neq r_0,\ \tau} S(r,\tau)\Big).
\]

Write `n_*=T-\tau_*` for the post-switch segment length. For each `r\neq r_0`, define
\[
\Delta_r(h)
=
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{r_*}\,\|\,p_r)
+
\beta\cdot \frac{1}{n_*}\log\frac{\sigma_{r_*}(h)}{\sigma_r(h)}.
\]
Assume
\[
\Delta_r(h)\ge \Delta>0 \quad \text{for all } r\neq r_*,
\tag{B1}
\]
and
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{r_*}\,\|\,p_{r_0})
+
\beta\cdot \frac{1}{n_*}\log\frac{\sigma_{r_*}(h)}{\sigma_{r_0}(h)}
\ge \Delta.
\tag{B2}
\]
Assume again bounded log-ratios by `B`.

### Theorem 2
If
\[
\lambda < \frac{n_*\Delta}{2},
\]
then
\[
\Pr[(\hat r,\hat\tau)\neq (r_*,\tau_*)]
\le
T|\mathcal F| \exp\!\left(-\frac{n_*\Delta^2}{8B^2}\right).
\]

### Proof

Compare the true switched score `S(r_*,\tau_*)` to any competitor.

For a wrong frame `r\neq r_*` with the same switch time `\tau_*`,
\[
S(r_*,\tau_*)-S(r,\tau_*)
=
\sum_{t>\tau_*}
\left(
\log p_{r_*}(a_t)-\log p_r(a_t)
\right)
+
\beta\log\frac{\sigma_{r_*}(h)}{\sigma_r(h)}.
\]
Its expectation is at least `n_*\Delta` by (B1). Hoeffding gives
\[
\Pr[S(r,\tau_*)\ge S(r_*,\tau_*)]
\le
\exp\!\left(-\frac{n_*\Delta^2}{8B^2}\right).
\]

For no-switch,
\[
S(r_*,\tau_*)-S_{\mathrm{ns}}
=
\sum_{t>\tau_*}
\left(
\log p_{r_*}(a_t)-\log p_{r_0}(a_t)
\right)
+
\beta\log\frac{\sigma_{r_*}(h)}{\sigma_{r_0}(h)}
-\lambda.
\]
Its expectation is at least `n_*\Delta-\lambda > n_*\Delta/2` by (B2) and the assumed bound on `\lambda`. Therefore
\[
\Pr[S_{\mathrm{ns}}\ge S(r_*,\tau_*)]
\le
\exp\!\left(-\frac{n_*\Delta^2}{8B^2}\right).
\]

For a wrong switch time `\tau`, the comparison differs only on the interval between `\tau` and `\tau_*`. Since any wrong `\tau` shortens or contaminates the true post-switch block, its expected advantage over the true switch is no larger than the same bound with at most `n_*` informative samples; the same exponential bound applies.

Now union bound over all `T` candidate switch times and all `|\mathcal F|` frame labels. QED.

### What is PROVEN here

Under a one-switch model, the transition time and terminal frame are jointly recoverable from traces with error decaying exponentially in the length of the post-switch segment. This gives a concrete, testable estimator for “constraint caused a frame transition”.

That is exactly the signature you want in the sunflower case: if banning entropy/LP really induced a phase transition into a cap-set-like frame, the typed trace after the ban should support a change-point.

I will formalize `O3` and use it to explain the empirical split in your runs.

\[
Y(u)=\Big(\prod_{s=0}^{u-1} p_s\Big) q_u.
\]
This is the right object for the harness, because your real choice is not “prove now or never,” but “how long do we protect this object before theorem-grade scrutiny?”

## 1. Sharp switch theorem

Define the local incubation gain
\[
R_t := \frac{Y(t+1)}{Y(t)} = p_t \frac{q_{t+1}}{q_t}.
\]

Interpretation:

- `R_t > 1`: one more incubation step is worth it
- `R_t < 1`: theoremization should happen now

### Theorem 1
Assume the sequence `R_t` is strictly decreasing in `t`. Then `Y(u)` is unimodal. In particular, there is a unique threshold `u_*` such that
\[
R_t>1 \text{ for } t<u_*, \qquad R_t<1 \text{ for } t\ge u_*,
\]
and the optimal verification time is exactly `u_*+1` (or `u_*`, depending on indexing convention).

### Proof
For every `u<d`,
\[
\frac{Y(u+1)}{Y(u)} = p_u \frac{q_{u+1}}{q_u} = R_u.
\]
So:

1. If `R_u>1`, then `Y(u+1)>Y(u)`.
2. If `R_u<1`, then `Y(u+1)<Y(u)`.

If `R_u` is strictly decreasing, it can cross `1` at most once. Therefore `Y(u)` first increases, then decreases, so it has a unique maximizer. QED.

This is the precise version of “there is an optimal maturity depth” and “the switch can be phase-transition-like rather than gradual.”

## 3. No single global verifier schedule can be optimal

Now I can prove a stronger impossibility statement.

Consider two idea families `A` and `B`. At the same stage `t`, suppose
\[
R_t^{A} < 1 < R_t^{B}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
Y_A(t) > Y_A(t+1), \qquad Y_B(t) < Y_B(t+1).
\]

### Theorem 2
Under the condition above, no global policy that forces all ideas to be verified at the same stage can be optimal for both families. A routed two-lane policy strictly dominates any global stage choice on every mixture of `A` and `B` with positive mass on both.

### Proof
There are only two relevant global choices around stage `t`.

1. Global verify at stage `t`.
   Then family `A` is handled optimally relative to `t,t+1`, because `Y_A(t)>Y_A(t+1)`.
   But family `B` is handled suboptimally, because `Y_B(t+1)>Y_B(t)`.

2. Global verify at stage `t+1`.
   Then family `B` is handled optimally relative to `t,t+1`.
   But family `A` is handled suboptimally.

Now consider the routed policy:
- verify `A` at stage `t`
- verify `B` at stage `t+1`

Its expected yield on any positive mixture `\alpha A + (1-\alpha)B` is
\[
\alpha Y_A(t) + (1-\alpha)Y_B(t+1).
\]
This strictly exceeds
\[
\alpha Y_A(t) + (1-\alpha)Y_B(t)
\]
and also strictly exceeds
\[
\alpha Y_A(t+1) + (1-\alpha)Y_B(t+1),
\]
because the strict inequalities hold familywise. Hence every global schedule loses to routing. QED.

This is the cleanest formal statement of the verification trap I know: **heterogeneous idea classes force architectural bifurcation.**

#### The Cognitive Scientist (codex/gpt-5.4)

I will attack [O4] directly and then use it to sharpen the operational definition question.

The ML Theorist already proved [P9]-[P10]: if we *have* a typed action alphabet with frame separation, then frame change is estimable from traces. The unresolved gap is whether that alphabet can be learned automatically from raw traces without destroying the very separation we need. That is the right cognitive-science question: what abstraction preserves “mode of thought” rather than merely topic or style?

## 1. Formal setup: raw moves, learned moves, and what must be preserved

Let `X` be the raw micro-action space extracted from traces. A raw symbol can be as fine-grained as a span-level tool call, a local proof transformation, a proposal of a new invariant, or a translation step.

For each frame `r` in a finite frame set `\mathcal F`, let `P_r` be a distribution on `X`.

A learned typed alphabet is a measurable map
\[
\phi:X\to\mathcal A
\]
into a finite codebook `\mathcal A`. This induces coarse distributions
\[
P_r^\phi(a)=P_r(\phi(X)=a).
\]

For pairwise frame discrimination, define the raw log-likelihood ratio
\[
\ell_{r,s}(x)=\log\frac{P_r(x)}{P_s(x)}.
\]

The quantity that matters is not semantic similarity of raw actions, and not token entropy. It is whether `\phi` preserves the likelihood-ratio geometry that makes frames distinguishable.

That gives a precise target for [O4].

---

## 2. First theorem: when automatic coarse-graining preserves frame separation

For each cell `C_a=\phi^{-1}(a)` and each pair `(r,s)`, define the within-cell oscillation
\[
\operatorname{osc}_{r,s}(a)
=
\sup_{x,x'\in C_a}
|\ell_{r,s}(x)-\ell_{r,s}(x')|.
\]

This measures whether the cell mixes raw actions with different evidential meanings for the frame pair `(r,s)`.

### Theorem 1
Assume that for some `\varepsilon_{r,s}\ge 0`,
\[
\operatorname{osc}_{r,s}(a)\le \varepsilon_{r,s}
\qquad\text{for every cell } a\in\mathcal A.
\tag{1}
\]
Then
\[
0\le D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)\le D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r\|P_s),
\]
and moreover
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)
\ge
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r\|P_s)-\varepsilon_{r,s}.
\tag{2}
\]

So a learned action alphabet whose cells are approximately likelihood-ratio homogeneous loses at most `\varepsilon_{r,s}` of pairwise frame-separation signal.

### Proof

Write
\[
Q_r(a)=P_r^\phi(a)=P_r(C_a),\qquad Q_s(a)=P_s(C_a).
\]
Then
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)
=
\sum_{a} Q_r(a)\log\frac{Q_r(a)}{Q_s(a)}.
\]

The upper bound
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)\le D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r\|P_s)
\]
is the standard data-processing inequality for deterministic coarse-graining.

Now prove the lower bound.

For each cell `C_a`, define
\[
m_a := \log\frac{Q_r(a)}{Q_s(a)}.
\]
Because
\[
\frac{Q_r(a)}{Q_s(a)}
=
\frac{\sum_{x\in C_a}P_r(x)}{\sum_{x\in C_a}P_s(x)}
=
\frac{\sum_{x\in C_a} e^{\ell_{r,s}(x)}P_s(x)}{\sum_{x\in C_a}P_s(x)},
\]
the ratio `Q_r(a)/Q_s(a)` is a weighted average of the numbers `e^{\ell_{r,s}(x)}` over `x\in C_a` with positive weights proportional to `P_s(x)`. Hence
\[
\min_{x\in C_a}\ell_{r,s}(x)\le m_a\le \max_{x\in C_a}\ell_{r,s}(x).
\]
Therefore, by (1),
\[
|\ell_{r,s}(x)-m_a|\le \varepsilon_{r,s}
\qquad\text{for every }x\in C_a.
\tag{3}
\]

Now expand the KL difference:
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r\|P_s)-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)
=
\sum_x P_r(x)\ell_{r,s}(x)
-
\sum_a Q_r(a)m_a.
\]
Since `Q_r(a)=\sum_{x\in C_a}P_r(x)`,
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r\|P_s)-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)
=
\sum_a\sum_{x\in C_a}P_r(x)\big(\ell_{r,s}(x)-m_a\big).
\]
Using (3),
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r\|P_s)-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_r^\phi\|P_s^\phi)
\le
\sum_a\sum_{x\in C_a}P_r(x)\varepsilon_{r,s}
=
\varepsilon_{r,s}.
\]
Rearranging gives (2). QED.

### What is PROVEN

A typed action alphabet can be learned automatically *without* losing frame identifiability, but only if its cells respect likelihood-ratio homogeneity. So [O4] is not hopeless; it has a clean sufficient condition.

This is a real advance over hand-labeling: the abstraction need not be human semantic categories. It only needs to preserve evidential meaning for frame discrimination.

## 3. Second theorem: if the learned alphabet destroys frame information, recovery is impossible

The previous theorem gave a sufficient condition. We also need a necessary condition.

Let `F` be a random frame, uniform on `\mathcal F`, and let
\[
U=(\phi(X_1),\dots,\phi(X_T),h)
\]
be the observed coarse trace plus final hypothesis.

### Theorem 2
For any estimator `\widehat F(U)` of the true frame,
\[
\Pr[\widehat F\neq F]
\ge
1-\frac{I(F;U)+\log 2}{\log |\mathcal F|}.
\tag{4}
\]

In particular, if
\[
I(F;U)\ll \log |\mathcal F|,
\]
then low-error frame recovery is impossible for *any* decoder.

### Proof

This is Fano’s inequality. Since `F` is uniform on `|\mathcal F|` states,
\[
H(F)=\log |\mathcal F|.
\]
For any estimator from `U`,
\[
H(F\mid U)\le H(P_e)+P_e\log(|\mathcal F|-1),
\]
where `P_e=\Pr[\widehat F\neq F]`. Rearranging in the standard way yields
\[
P_e\ge 1-\frac{I(F;U)+\log 2}{\log |\mathcal F|}.
\]
QED.

### What is PROVEN

There is a hard lower bound on novelty measurement. If your learned abstraction mostly captures topic, syntax, or style and throws away frame information, no downstream cleverness can recover representational novelty.

So [O4] is not “cluster the traces somehow.” It is “compress traces while retaining mutual information with frame.”

## 4. Explicit gadget: why semantic-looking clustering can fail completely

Here is a small construction showing exactly where automatic action learning can go wrong.

Let the raw action set be
\[
X=\{x_1,x_2,y_1,y_2\}.
\]
Define two frames:
\[
P_A=(0.45,0.45,0.05,0.05),
\qquad
P_B=(0.05,0.05,0.45,0.45).
\]
So the raw KL is positive and large:
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_A\|P_B)
=
0.45\log 9 + 0.45\log 9 + 0.05\log(1/9)+0.05\log(1/9)
=
0.8\log 9.
\]

Now compare two coarse-grainings.

### Bad clustering
Let
\[
\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}(x_1)=\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}(y_1)=a,
\qquad
\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}(x_2)=\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}(y_2)=b.
\]
Then
\[
P_A^{\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}}=(0.5,0.5),
\qquad
P_B^{\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}}=(0.5,0.5).
\]
Hence
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_A^{\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}}\|P_B^{\phi_{\mathrm{bad}}})=0.
\]
All frame signal is destroyed.

### Good clustering
Let
\[
\phi_{\mathrm{good}}(x_1)=\phi_{\mathrm{good}}(x_2)=a,
\qquad
\phi_{\mathrm{good}}(y_1)=\phi_{\mathrm{good}}(y_2)=b.
\]
Then
\[
P_A^{\phi_{\mathrm{good}}}=(0.9,0.1),
\qquad
P_B^{\phi_{\mathrm{good}}}=(0.1,0.9),
\]
so
\[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_A^{\phi_{\mathrm{good}}}\|P_B^{\phi_{\mathrm{good}}})
=
0.9\log 9 + 0.1\log(1/9)
=
0.8\log 9.
\]
The full frame gap is preserved.

### Interpretation

A clustering can look superficially reasonable and still be fatal if it groups actions by local similarity while mixing opposite frame evidence. The good clustering groups by *operator role*. The bad clustering groups by something like surface resemblance.

This is the cognitive point: the right unit of abstraction is not “what the sentence looks like,” but “what inferential move it performs.”

## 1. Formal setup: proposal mass on a target family

Fix a problem instance of size `n`. Let `T_n` be a target family of candidate objects/hypotheses that would count as a genuinely new line for the harness on this instance. For Frankl, `T_n` could be “objects not in the closure of the default anti-collision / transport frame but leading to nontrivial descendants.” For sunflower, `T_n` could be “objects in the algebraic/cap-set frame.”

An inference procedure runs for `B` proposal steps. At step `t`, after history `H_{t-1}`, it chooses a control `a_t`:
- prompt edit,
- temperature,
- branch selection,
- verifier call,
- model choice from a fixed pool,

subject to staying in a specified regime `\mathcal R`. The system then emits a candidate `Y_t`.

Define the regime-wise one-step target mass
\[
\mu_{\mathcal R}(n)
:=
\sup_{t,H_{t-1},a_t\in\mathcal R}
\Pr\!\big[Y_t\in T_n \mid H_{t-1},a_t\big].
\]
This is the largest conditional probability that one proposal step in regime `\mathcal R` assigns to the target family.

This is the quantity that token entropy, temperature, and branch count do **not** directly measure.

---

## 2. Compute ceiling theorem

### Theorem 1
Let `\mathcal R_0` be a regime that does **not** permit a frame change beyond the default reachable family in the sense of [P8]. Suppose every proposal step in `\mathcal R_0` satisfies
\[
\Pr\!\big[Y_t\in T_n \mid H_{t-1},a_t\in\mathcal R_0\big]
\le \mu_0(n).
\tag{1}
\]
Then any adaptive budget-`B` procedure confined to `\mathcal R_0` has success probability
\[
\Pr\!\Big[\exists t\le B:\ Y_t\in T_n\Big]
\le 1-(1-\mu_0(n))^B
\le B\,\mu_0(n).
\tag{2}
\]

### Proof
Let
\[
E_t := \{\text{no proposal in }1,\dots,t\text{ lies in }T_n\}.
\]
Then by the tower property,
\[
\Pr(E_t)
=
\Pr(E_{t-1})\,
\Pr(E_t\mid E_{t-1}).
\]
Condition on any history in `E_{t-1}`. By assumption (1), the next-step success probability is at most `\mu_0(n)`, so
\[
\Pr(E_t\mid H_{t-1},E_{t-1}) \ge 1-\mu_0(n).
\]
Averaging over such histories gives
\[
\Pr(E_t\mid E_{t-1}) \ge 1-\mu_0(n).
\]
Hence
\[
\Pr(E_t)\ge (1-\mu_0(n))\Pr(E_{t-1}).
\]
Inducting from `\Pr(E_0)=1`,
\[
\Pr(E_B)\ge (1-\mu_0(n))^B.
\]
Therefore
\[
\Pr(E_B^c)\le 1-(1-\mu_0(n))^B.
\]
Finally, Bernoulli’s inequality gives
\[
1-(1-\mu_0)^B \le B\mu_0.
\]
QED.

### What is PROVEN
Extra tree-of-thought depth, branch count, debate rounds, or verifier loops help at most linearly in the regime’s accessible target mass. If the target family has tiny per-step mass, more “thinking time” cannot rescue it except by brute-force multiplication.

This is the formal version of the convergence wall.

## 3. Polynomial compute cannot overcome exponentially small target mass

### Corollary 2
If
\[
\mu_0(n)\le e^{-\alpha n}
\quad\text{for some }\alpha>0,
\]
and the inference budget is polynomial,
\[
B(n)\le n^k,
\]
then
\[
\Pr\!\Big[\exists t\le B(n):Y_t\in T_n\Big]
\le n^k e^{-\alpha n}\to 0.
\]

### Proof
Apply Theorem 1:
\[
\Pr(\text{success})\le B(n)\mu_0(n)\le n^k e^{-\alpha n}\to 0.
\]
QED.

### Interpretation
If a genuinely new idea family has exponentially small mass under the default proposal regime, then scaling inference-time compute polynomially does **not** produce novelty in the large-`n` limit. You need one of only three things:

1. a regime change that raises the mass,
2. superpolynomial search,
3. an external operator that constructs the target more directly than repeated sampling.

This sharply answers sub-question 4: novelty does **not** scale like ordinary accuracy once the desired object family is outside the regime’s proposal mass basin.

## 4. Frame-switch gain theorem

Now combine this with [P8]. Let `\sigma` be a transformation of positive frame cost that moves the system from default regime `\mathcal R_0` to a new regime `\mathcal R_1`, where the target family is reachable.

Suppose:
- switching costs `c` proposal steps,
- after the switch, each remaining step has target mass at least `\mu_1(n)`:
\[
\Pr\!\big[Y_t\in T_n\mid H_{t-1},a_t\in\mathcal R_1\big]\ge \mu_1(n).
\tag{3}
\]

### Theorem 3
A policy that switches immediately and then spends the remaining `B-c` steps in `\mathcal R_1` has success probability at least
\[
1-(1-\mu_1(n))^{B-c}.
\tag{4}
\]
Therefore if
\[
(B-c)\mu_1(n) \gg B\mu_0(n),
\tag{5}
\]
the switched policy strictly dominates every no-switch policy confined to `\mathcal R_0`.

### Proof
Under (3), conditional on any switched history with no prior success, the next-step failure probability is at most `1-\mu_1(n)`. Repeating the argument of Theorem 1 gives success probability at least (4).

Every no-switch policy is bounded above by Theorem 1:
\[
\Pr(\text{success without switch})\le 1-(1-\mu_0)^B\le B\mu_0.
\]
If (5) holds, then for small masses the switched lower bound behaves like `(B-c)\mu_1`, which exceeds the no-switch upper bound `B\mu_0`. QED.

### What is PROVEN
A representation change is not just “diversity.” It is valuable exactly when it increases accessible target mass enough to overcome its switching cost.

This is the theorem-level explanation of your constraint effect. Banning native tools helped in sunflower because it likely reduced local fluency but raised `\mu` on the algebraic target family by orders of magnitude.

## 5. Why transformer expressivity does not settle the question

A common confusion is:

- transformers are Turing-complete or universal approximators,
- therefore they can in principle invent new ideas,
- therefore scale + compute should eventually work.

This inference is invalid.

### PROVEN distinction
Function-class expressivity is an **existence** statement:
there exists a parameter setting implementing a search or synthesis procedure.

Theorems 1–3 are **accessibility** statements:
for the actual trained proposal distributions available at inference time, success is controlled by reachable target mass `\mu`.

No contradiction exists between:
- “some transformer can compute the right search,” and
- “this trained transformer family assigns exponentially small mass to the right object family under all same-frame controls.”

So sub-question 2 splits cleanly:

- **Architecture alone** does not impose a simple impossibility ceiling, because transformers are expressive enough to represent strong search procedures.
- **Trained accessibility plus finite inference budget** does impose a practical and sometimes asymptotic ceiling, captured by `\mu_0(n)`.

That is the quantity to measure.

In a free prompt, the model’s internal controller picks `A` because it is locally high-probability. By [P6], a hard ban can force a phase transition in frame selection. Here is the compute consequence:

- free regime success upper bound:
\[

- constrained regime success lower bound after switching:
\[
\ge 1-(1-\mu_B(n))^{B-c}.

So the relevant empirical claim is not “constraint improves creativity” in the abstract. It is:

> constraint is useful exactly when it causes a jump  
> \[
> \mu_A(n)\ll \mu_B(n)

> on the object family that matters.

This integrates [P6] with Theorem 3. Constraint works by **mass reallocation away from attractors**.

## 9. Multi-model diversity only helps through complementary target-mass vectors

I will make one precise statement about sub-question 5.

Partition the target family into disjoint subfamilies
\[
T_n = \bigsqcup_{j=1}^m T_{n,j}.
\]
For model `i`, define the one-step masses
\[
\mu_{ij}(n)=\sup_{H,a}\Pr[Y_t\in T_{n,j}\mid H,a,\text{model }i].
\]
A single “diversity” scalar is too coarse; what matters is the vector `(\mu_{ij})_j`.

### Theorem 4
If for two models `i,i'` there is a constant `c` such that
\[
\mu_{ij}(n)\le c\,\mu_{i'j}(n)\qquad\text{for every }j,
\]
then replacing model `i'` by model `i` changes no budget-`B` union-bound success estimate by more than a factor `c`. In particular, if all models have approximately collinear target-mass vectors, ensemble diversity gives no qualitative novelty gain.

### Proof
Any routed policy that assigns `b_j` calls to the model used on subfamily `j` has, by Theorem 1 and a union bound across disjoint `T_{n,j}`,
\[
\Pr(\text{hit some }T_{n,j})\le \sum_j b_j \mu_{ij}(n)
\]
for model `i`, and similarly for `i'`. By the coordinatewise bound,
\[
\sum_j b_j \mu_{ij}(n)\le c\sum_j b_j \mu_{i'j}(n).
\]
QED.

### Consequence
Model diversity matters only if models are **non-collinear** in target-family mass space. That is the precise analogue of genetic complementarity. Different training distributions help only when they put mass on different dormant object families.

## 1. Formal decomposition of harness yield

Fix a task and an intervention regime \(I\) (for example: free prompt, banned-native prompt, conjecture engine, incubation lane).

Let \(O\) be a target **object-grade** family and \(T\) the event “produces a theorem-grade descendant within \(u\) maturation steps.”

Examples:

- sunflower:
  \(O=\) algebraic objects/statements in the cap-set frame,
  \(T=\) a validated theorem-grade consequence or equivalence
- Frankl:
  \(O=\) non-native transport alternatives, new statistics, or frame-shifted candidate invariants,
  \(T=\) a descendant lemma that survives adversarial verification
- battery:
  \(O=\) a new state-variable decomposition or loss-channel invariant,
  \(T=\) a quantitatively correct bound or impossibility statement
- Starship / alien analogy:
  \(O=\) non-aerospace representations such as coalition/bottleneck or mechanism-design objects,
  \(T=\) a descendant constraint or optimization law that survives translation back

Define
\[
\mu_I := \Pr_I(O),
\qquad
s_I(u) := \Pr_I(T\mid O),
\qquad
Y_I(u):=\Pr_I(T).
\]

Then by the product rule,

### Theorem 1
\[
Y_I(u)=\mu_I\, s_I(u).
\]

### Proof
Since \(T\) occurs only through first entering the tracked object family \(O\),
\[
\Pr_I(T)=\Pr_I(T\cap O)=\Pr_I(T\mid O)\Pr_I(O)=\mu_I s_I(u).
\]
QED.

This is elementary, but it is the right decomposition. The entire novelty debate is about which factor interventions move.

## 2. Final theorem counts alone do not identify the mechanism

The current harness often judges an intervention by terminal outcomes: “did it produce a theorem-grade result or not?” That is insufficient.

### Theorem 2
From terminal yield \(Y_I(u)\) alone, the pair \((\mu_I,s_I(u))\) is not identifiable.

### Proof
Fix any \(Y\in(0,1)\). For any \(c\in[Y,1]\), define
\[
\mu=c,\qquad s=Y/c.
\]
Then \(\mu\in[Y,1]\), \(s\in[Y,1]\), and
\[
\mu s = c\cdot (Y/c)=Y.
\]
Hence infinitely many distinct pairs \((\mu,s)\) generate the same terminal yield. QED.

## 3. Minimal instrumentation that *does* identify the mechanism

Suppose the harness logs, per proposal lane and intervention:

- whether a proposal entered the object family \(O\)
- whether that object matured into theorem-grade output within \(u\) protected steps

Let \(N_I\) be total proposals, \(H_I\) object hits, \(M_I\) mature descendants.

Define empirical estimators
\[
\widehat\mu_I = \frac{H_I}{N_I},
\qquad
\widehat s_I(u)=\frac{M_I}{H_I}
\quad(H_I>0),
\qquad
\widehat Y_I(u)=\frac{M_I}{N_I}.
\]

### Theorem 3
Whenever \(H_I>0\),
\[
\widehat Y_I(u)=\widehat\mu_I\,\widehat s_I(u).
\]
Moreover, with i.i.d. proposals in regime \(I\), \(\widehat\mu_I\) and \(\widehat s_I(u)\) are unbiased for \(\mu_I\) and \(s_I(u)\).

### Proof
The identity is algebraic:
\[
\widehat\mu_I\,\widehat s_I(u)
=
\frac{H_I}{N_I}\cdot\frac{M_I}{H_I}
=
\frac{M_I}{N_I}
=
\widehat Y_I(u).
\]
Unbiasedness follows because \(H_I\sim\mathrm{Bin}(N_I,\mu_I)\), so
\[
\mathbb E[\widehat\mu_I]=\mu_I.
\]
Conditional on \(H_I=h>0\), \(M_I\sim\mathrm{Bin}(h,s_I(u))\), hence
\[
\mathbb E[\widehat s_I(u)\mid H_I=h]=s_I(u),
\]
and therefore \(\mathbb E[\widehat s_I(u)\mid H_I>0]=s_I(u)\). QED.

This is the first concrete answer to [O13] and part of [O6]: the intervention mechanism is identifiable if and only if we instrument object hits, not just theorem outputs.

## 4. Additive log-lift decomposition

For comparing two regimes \(I=0,1\), if all terms are positive then
\[
\log\frac{Y_1}{Y_0}
=
\log\frac{\mu_1}{\mu_0}
+
\log\frac{s_1}{s_0}.
\]

### Corollary 4
Any improvement in terminal yield factorizes exactly into:

- a **proposal-mass lift** \(\log(\mu_1/\mu_0)\)

## 5. Connection to \(R_t\): a theorem-grade survival law

Now connect this to [P11]-[P12].

Let the maturation path have stages \(t=0,\dots,u\), and define:

- \(p_t\): survival probability from stage \(t\) to \(t+1\)
- \(q_t\): probability an idea at stage \(t\) is already theorem-grade
- \(R_t := p_t q_{t+1}/q_t\)

Then for a hit in \(O\), the probability of theorem-grade maturity by protected depth \(u\) is
\[
s_I(u)=\Big(\prod_{t<u} p_{I,t}\Big) q_{I,u}.
\]

This is just the quantity already implicit in [P11], but now it sits inside observable terminal yield:
\[
Y_I(u)=\mu_I \Big(\prod_{t<u} p_{I,t}\Big) q_{I,u}.
\]

### Theorem 5
If \(R_{I,t}\) is strictly decreasing in \(t\), then for fixed intervention \(I\), the optimal within-lane verification depth
\[
u_I^*=\arg\max_u Y_I(u)
\]
is the same as the optimizer of
\[
\arg\max_u s_I(u).
\]
In particular, proposal mass \(\mu_I\) changes lane priority but does not change the optimal incubation depth within that lane.

### Proof
For fixed \(I\), \(\mu_I\) does not depend on \(u\), so
\[
\arg\max_u Y_I(u)=\arg\max_u \mu_I s_I(u)=\arg\max_u s_I(u).
\]
By [P11], strict decrease of \(R_{I,t}\) implies \(s_I(u)\) is unimodal, hence admits a sharp threshold optimizer. QED.

This matters operationally. There are **two distinct control problems**:

1. choose a lane/intervention \(I\): driven by \(\mu_I\)
2. choose incubation depth \(u\) inside that lane: driven by \(R_{I,t}\)

Your harness currently mixes these.

\[
L(I)=|I|\,H(\hat p_I),

Now fix a split point \(\tau\), with \(\alpha=\tau/T\). Define the one-frame vs two-frame compression gain

\Delta_T(\tau)

### Theorem 1
For every trace and every split \(\tau\),
\[
\Delta_T(\tau)
=
T\,\mathrm{JSD}_\alpha\!\big(\hat p_{1:\tau},\hat p_{\tau+1:T}\big),
\]
where
\[
\mathrm{JSD}_\alpha(p,q)
=
H(\alpha p+(1-\alpha)q)-\alpha H(p)-(1-\alpha)H(q).
\]

### Proof
Write
\[
\hat r=\hat p_{1:T}=\alpha \hat p_{1:\tau}+(1-\alpha)\hat p_{\tau+1:T}.

Then
\[
L(1{:}T)=T H(\hat r),\quad
L(1{:}\tau)=\tau H(\hat p_{1:\tau}),\quad

\]
Subtracting,
\[
\Delta_T(\tau)
=
T H(\hat r)-\tau H(\hat p_{1:\tau})-(T-\tau)H(\hat p_{\tau+1:T}).

Using \(\tau=\alpha T\) and \(T-\tau=(1-\alpha)T\),
\[
\Delta_T(\tau)
=
T\Big(H(\alpha \hat p_{1:\tau}+(1-\alpha)\hat p_{\tau+1:T})
-\alpha H(\hat p_{1:\tau})
-(1-\alpha)H(\hat p_{\tau+1:T})\Big),
\]
which is exactly
\[
T\,\mathrm{JSD}_\alpha(\hat p_{1:\tau},\hat p_{\tau+1:T}).
\]
QED.

\[
\sup_\tau \|\hat p_{1:\tau}-\hat p_{\tau+1:T}\|_1
=
O_p\!\Big(\sqrt{\frac{\log T}{T}}\Big).

#### The ML Theorist (codex/gpt-5.4)

I will attack the part that is still missing in the ledger’s formal picture: **why constraint and temperature sometimes help, and exactly when multi-model diversity helps rather than merely adding more samples**. This lets us answer sub-questions 3, 5, 6, and part of 2 without reusing any dead route.

Take a finite candidate set of object-level moves \(i\in\Omega\). Under a prompt regime \(r\) and temperature \(\tau>0\), let the model induce proposal scores \(a_i^{(r)}\), and define the one-step proposal law
\[
p_i^{(r,\tau)}=\frac{e^{a_i^{(r)}/\tau}}{Z_r(\tau)},\qquad Z_r(\tau)=\sum_{\ell\in\Omega}e^{a_\ell^{(r)}/\tau}.
\]
For a target object family \(T\subseteq\Omega\), define proposal mass
\[
\mu_T^{(r,\tau)}=\sum_{i\in T}p_i^{(r,\tau)}.
\]
As in [P19], terminal theorem yield factors as \(Y=\mu s\), so the prompting question reduces first to how prompt regime and temperature move \(\mu_T\).

## 1. Temperature is not “more creativity”; it is salience reweighting

### Theorem 1
For fixed regime \(r\) and target family \(T\),
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}\mu_T^{(r,\tau)}
=
\frac{\mu_T^{(r,\tau)}}{\tau^2}\Big(\mathbb E_{p^{(r,\tau)}}[a^{(r)}]-\mathbb E_{p^{(r,\tau)}}[a^{(r)}\mid T]\Big).
\]

In particular,
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}\mu_T^{(r,\tau)}>0
\iff
\mathbb E[a^{(r)}\mid T]<\mathbb E[a^{(r)}].
\]

So increasing temperature helps a target family exactly when that family is **below-average in current salience**.

### Proof
Suppress \(r\) in notation. Write \(p_i(\tau)=e^{a_i/\tau}/Z(\tau)\). Then
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}p_i(\tau)
=
p_i(\tau)\frac{\mathbb E_p[a]-a_i}{\tau^2},
\]
because
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}\log p_i(\tau)
=
-\frac{a_i}{\tau^2}+\frac{1}{\tau^2}\sum_{\ell}p_\ell a_\ell.
\]
Summing over \(i\in T\),
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}\mu_T(\tau)
=
\sum_{i\in T}p_i(\tau)\frac{\mathbb E_p[a]-a_i}{\tau^2}
=
\frac{1}{\tau^2}\Big(\mu_T(\tau)\mathbb E_p[a]-\sum_{i\in T}p_i(\tau)a_i\Big).
\]
Since
\[
\sum_{i\in T}p_i(\tau)a_i
=
\mu_T(\tau)\mathbb E_p[a\mid T],
\]
we get
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}\mu_T(\tau)
=
\frac{\mu_T(\tau)}{\tau^2}\Big(\mathbb E_p[a]-\mathbb E_p[a\mid T]\Big).
\]
QED.

### Corollary 1
\[
\lim_{\tau\to 0}\mu_T^{(r,\tau)}
=
\mathbf 1\{\arg\max_i a_i^{(r)}\in T\}
\quad\text{if the argmax is unique,}
\]
while
\[
\lim_{\tau\to\infty}\mu_T^{(r,\tau)}=\frac{|T|}{|\Omega|}.
\]

### Proof
As \(\tau\to 0\), softmax concentrates on argmax scores. As \(\tau\to\infty\), \(e^{a_i/\tau}\to 1\) for all \(i\), so the law becomes uniform. QED.

This gives a clean reading of your temperature observations. High temperature is useful when the desired object family is currently low-salience but not vanishingly small. It is useless or harmful when the target family is already high-salience or extremely sparse. So “higher temperature = more novelty” is false in general.

## 2. Hard constraints work by deleting attractor mass

Now model a banned-technique prompt as a hard ban on a subset \(A\subseteq\Omega\): candidates in \(A\) are disallowed. The renormalized mass on \(T\) is
\[
\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}=\frac{\mu_T-\mu_{T\cap A}}{1-\mu_A},
\]
where all masses on the right are under the unbanned regime at the same \(\tau\).

### Theorem 2
A hard ban improves target-family proposal mass iff
\[
\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}>\mu_T
\iff
\mu_{T\cap A}<\mu_T\,\mu_A.
\]

Equivalently: banning helps exactly when the banned set is **less target-enriched than the ambient distribution**.

### Proof
Starting from
\[
\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}=\frac{\mu_T-\mu_{T\cap A}}{1-\mu_A},
\]
we compare to \(\mu_T\):
\[
\frac{\mu_T-\mu_{T\cap A}}{1-\mu_A}>\mu_T
\iff
\mu_T-\mu_{T\cap A}>\mu_T-\mu_T\mu_A
\iff
\mu_{T\cap A}<\mu_T\mu_A.
\]
QED.

### Special case
If \(A\cap T=\varnothing\), then
\[
\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}=\frac{\mu_T}{1-\mu_A}>\mu_T.
\]

So if entropy/LP lines carried large proposal mass but had little overlap with the algebraic cap-set family, banning them had to increase algebraic proposal mass. This is a theorem, not an intuition.

That directly explains your constraint effect. The prompt is not “making the model creative.” It is **lowering the partition function contribution of the native attractor basin**.

## 3. Prompt freedom vs constraint: exact criterion

Combine Theorem 2 with [P19]. If \(s_T(u)\) is unchanged by the ban, then the terminal yield ratio is
\[
\frac{Y_{\mathrm{ban}}}{Y_{\mathrm{free}}}
=
\frac{\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}}{\mu_T}.
\]
If the ban also changes maturation, then
\[
\frac{Y_{\mathrm{ban}}}{Y_{\mathrm{free}}}
=
\frac{\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}}{\mu_T}\cdot \frac{s_{\mathrm{ban}}(u)}{s_{\mathrm{free}}(u)}.
\]
By [D2], you cannot identify which factor moved from theorem counts alone, but Theorem 2 tells you exactly what prompt-side mechanism is available: **mass deletion from non-target attractors**.

This is the formal answer to “why did removing tools create insight?” Because the ban changes the normalization constant. If the native tools occupy a large fraction of proposal mass and are not target-enriched, deleting them necessarily increases mass on the alien family.

## 4. Multi-model diversity helps only through anti-domination under hidden-family uncertainty

Now I will formalize sub-question 5.

Let \(j\in\{1,\dots,m\}\) index latent target families. Interpret \(j\) as “the family containing the actual door.” Let the prior on the unknown door family be \(\pi_j\). Model \(k\) has one-sample terminal theorem mass
\[
\nu_{k,j}=\mu_{k,j}s_{k,j}\in[0,1].
\]
If we allocate \(b_k\) independent attempts to model \(k\), then conditional on door family \(j\), failure probability is
\[
\prod_k (1-\nu_{k,j})^{b_k},
\]
so Bayes-averaged success is
\[
S(b_1,\dots,b_K)
=
\sum_{j=1}^m \pi_j\Big(1-\prod_k (1-\nu_{k,j})^{b_k}\Big).
\tag{*}
\]

This is the right object for “genetic diversity” in your setting: the door family is unknown ex ante.

### Theorem 3
If model \(a\) componentwise dominates model \(b\),
\[
\nu_{a,j}\ge \nu_{b,j}\quad\forall j,
\]
then moving any attempt from \(b\) to \(a\) weakly increases \(S\). Hence a dominated model contributes no novelty benefit.

### Proof
Fix a door family \(j\). Since \(\nu_{a,j}\ge \nu_{b,j}\),
\[
1-\nu_{a,j}\le 1-\nu_{b,j}.
\]
So replacing one factor \((1-\nu_{b,j})\) by \((1-\nu_{a,j})\) weakly decreases the failure product
\[
\prod_k (1-\nu_{k,j})^{b_k},
\]
hence weakly increases
\[
1-\prod_k (1-\nu_{k,j})^{b_k}.
\]
This holds for every \(j\), so after averaging with weights \(\pi_j\), \(S\) weakly increases. QED.

This strictly refines [P18]. Collinearity is one way diversity can fail, but the exact obstruction is stronger: **componentwise domination kills any benefit from diversity**.

### GADGET
Take two latent families \(A,B\), prior \(\pi_A=\pi_B=1/2\), and two models with
\[
\nu_1=(0.9,0.1),\qquad \nu_2=(0.1,0.9).
\]
With total budget \(2\):

- all compute on model 1:
\[
S(2,0)=\frac12(1-0.1^2)+\frac12(1-0.9^2)=\frac12(0.99+0.19)=0.59;
\]

- all compute on model 2:
\[
S(0,2)=0.59;
\]

- one attempt on each:
\[
S(1,1)=\frac12(1-0.1\cdot 0.9)+\frac12(1-0.9\cdot 0.1)=0.91.
\]

So diversity can be dramatically better when models specialize on different latent door families.

This gives the precise analogy to genetic diversity: diversity helps only if different models carry mass on **different plausible door families**. If their \(\nu\)-vectors are nearly parallel or one dominates the others, there is no qualitative gain.

## 5. Architecture ceiling as a proposal-mass ceiling

This now lets us state a sharper architecture result than “transformers can or cannot be creative.”

Let \(\mathcal R_n\) be the family of all regimes realizable at problem size \(n\) by a fixed architecture class using prompt changes, temperature changes, verifier loops, and polynomial-time inference control, but **without training updates or external new knowledge**. Let
\[
\nu^*(n)=\sup_{r\in\mathcal R_n}\sup_j \nu_{r,j}(n).
\]

### Theorem 4
If for some \(\alpha>0\),
\[
\nu^*(n)\le e^{-\alpha n}
\]
for all large \(n\), then any polynomial-budget harness built from that architecture class has novelty success probability \(o(1)\).

### Proof
Every attempt, under every realizable regime \(r\in\mathcal R_n\), has success probability at most \(\nu^*(n)\). So the whole adaptive controller is confined to per-step target-family mass at most \(\nu^*(n)\). By [P15],
\[
P_{\mathrm{succ}}(n)\le 1-(1-\nu^*(n))^{B(n)}\le B(n)\nu^*(n).
\]
If \(B(n)\) is polynomial and \(\nu^*(n)\le e^{-\alpha n}\), then
\[
B(n)\nu^*(n)\to 0.
\]
Hence success probability tends to \(0\). QED.

This is the clean operational ceiling. It does **not** say “current transformers can never be novel.” It says:

- if no prompt/temperature/controller reachable inside the architecture raises theorem mass above exponentially tiny scale,
- then more inference-time compute cannot save you.

So the ceiling is not “attention implies interpolation.” The ceiling is: **accessible regimes fail to move proposal mass onto the missing family**.

That fits your Frankl convergence wall exactly. If all runs keep landing in regimes with exponentially small \(\nu\) on the missing family, then the repeated wall is structural, not a search-allocation accident.

#### The Harness Engineer (codex/gpt-5.4)

I will attack `O6`, `O14`, `O15`, and `O24` directly. That is exactly where the Frankl/sunflower evidence is currently under-instrumented.

Let a single harness attempt use a lane
\[
\ell=(r,k,u,v,c),
\]
where

- \(r\) = prompt regime (`free`, `banned-native`, `conjecture-first`, `alien-analogy`, etc.),
- \(k\) = model identity (`GPT`, `Claude`, `Grok`),
- \(u\) = protected-incubation depth before theorem-grade verification,
- \(v\) = verifier severity after handoff,
- \(c\in\{0,1\}\) = contamination flag for cross-pollination / KB injection.

For attempt \(n\), define potential outcomes:

- \(O_n(\ell)\in\{0,1\}\): did the lane produce an object-level hit in the target family?
- \(M_n(\ell)\in\{0,1\}\): conditional on an object hit, did that hit mature into a theorem-grade contribution?
- \(F_n(\ell)\in\{0,1\}\): did the line ultimately leak a false claim past the verifier?

Define the lane parameters
\[
\mu_\ell := \mathbb E[O_n(\ell)],\qquad
s_\ell := \mathbb E[M_n(\ell)\mid O_n(\ell)=1],\qquad
f_\ell := \mathbb E[F_n(\ell)].
\]
Then by construction the terminal true-yield is
\[
Y_\ell=\mu_\ell s_\ell,
\]
which is [P19], and the false-claim leakage is \(f_\ell\).

The key point is that these are **causal lane parameters**, not post-hoc summaries.

## Theorem 1: Randomized harness assignment identifies \(\mu\), \(s\), and false-claim leakage separately

Assume each attempt \(n\) is assigned to a lane \(A_n\) by randomization independent of all potential outcomes:
\[
A_n \perp \!\!\! \perp \{O_n(\ell),M_n(\ell),F_n(\ell): \ell\in\mathcal L\}.
\]
Assume consistency: the observed outcomes satisfy
\[
O_n=O_n(A_n),\quad M_n=M_n(A_n),\quad F_n=F_n(A_n).
\]

Then for every lane \(\ell\),

\[
\mathbb E[O_n\mid A_n=\ell]=\mu_\ell,
\]
\[
\mathbb E[O_nM_n\mid A_n=\ell]=\mu_\ell s_\ell,
\]
\[
\mathbb E[F_n\mid A_n=\ell]=f_\ell.
\]

If moreover \(\mu_\ell>0\), then
\[
\mathbb E[M_n\mid O_n=1,A_n=\ell]=s_\ell.
\]

### Proof
By consistency,
\[
\mathbb E[O_n\mid A_n=\ell]=\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)\mid A_n=\ell].
\]
By randomization independence,
\[
\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)\mid A_n=\ell]=\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)]=\mu_\ell.
\]

Likewise,
\[
\mathbb E[O_nM_n\mid A_n=\ell]
=
\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)M_n(\ell)\mid A_n=\ell]
=
\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)M_n(\ell)].
\]
Now use
\[
\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)M_n(\ell)]
=
\mathbb E[\,O_n(\ell)\,\mathbb E[M_n(\ell)\mid O_n(\ell)]\,].
\]
Since \(M_n(\ell)\) is only meaningful on \(O_n(\ell)=1\), this is
\[
\mathbb E[O_n(\ell)]\cdot \mathbb E[M_n(\ell)\mid O_n(\ell)=1]
=
\mu_\ell s_\ell.
\]

Similarly,
\[
\mathbb E[F_n\mid A_n=\ell]=\mathbb E[F_n(\ell)]=f_\ell.
\]

Finally,
\[
\mathbb E[M_n\mid O_n=1,A_n=\ell]
=
\mathbb E[M_n(\ell)\mid O_n(\ell)=1,A_n=\ell]
=
\mathbb E[M_n(\ell)\mid O_n(\ell)=1]
=
s_\ell,
\]
again by randomization independence. QED.

This is not a restatement of [P21]. [P21] gave sufficiency of logging. The new point is **causal identifiability under randomized lane assignment**. That is what lets us answer the workshop’s central empirical questions without post-selection bias:

- Did banned-native sunflower increase \(\mu\) for the algebraic family?
- Did conjecture-first increase \(s\) for weird proto-ideas?
- Did KB injection change \(f\) by making lines sound more coherent than they were?

Without randomization, all three are confounded by adaptive controller choice.

Y_b-Y_a
=
(\mu_b-\mu_a)s_a+\mu_a(s_b-s_a)+(\mu_b-\mu_a)(s_b-s_a).
\]

### Proof
Expand
\[
Y_b-Y_a=\mu_bs_b-\mu_as_a
\]
as
\[
(\mu_b-\mu_a)s_a+\mu_a(s_b-s_a)+(\mu_b-\mu_a)(s_b-s_a).
\]
QED.

## Theorem 2: The verification trap has a sharp threshold once false-claim cost is included

Fix a lane \((r,k,c)\). Let \(u\) be the protected-incubation depth before strict verification starts. Define

- \(A(u)\): true theorem yield under incubation depth \(u\),
- \(B(u)\): false-claim leakage under incubation depth \(u\).

Let the utility be
\[
J_\lambda(u)=A(u)-\lambda B(u),
\]
where \(\lambda>0\) is the embarrassment cost of a leaked false claim.

Assume the marginal yield and leakage increments
\[
\Delta A(u):=A(u+1)-A(u),\qquad
\Delta B(u):=B(u+1)-B(u)
\]
satisfy \(\Delta B(u)>0\) and the ratio
\[
\Lambda(u):=\frac{\Delta A(u)}{\Delta B(u)}
\]
is strictly decreasing in \(u\).

Then the optimal incubation depth is the threshold
\[
u^\*(\lambda)=\max\{u:\Lambda(u)\ge \lambda\}.
\]

### Proof
We have
\[
J_\lambda(u+1)-J_\lambda(u)=\Delta A(u)-\lambda\Delta B(u)
=\Delta B(u)\big(\Lambda(u)-\lambda\big).
\]
Since \(\Delta B(u)>0\), the sign of the increment is the sign of \(\Lambda(u)-\lambda\).

Because \(\Lambda(u)\) is strictly decreasing, there is at most one sign change. Therefore \(J_\lambda(u)\) increases while \(\Lambda(u)\ge\lambda\) and decreases once \(\Lambda(u)<\lambda\). Hence the maximizer is exactly the largest \(u\) with \(\Lambda(u)\ge\lambda\). QED.

This is the missing formalization of the verification paradox. The question is not “verify early or late?” The question is:

\[
\text{At what depth does one more incubation step stop being worth its added leakage risk?}
\]

That gives a concrete Pareto frontier for the harness. Frankl likely wants a much smaller \(u^\*\) than conjecture-first sunflower, because the false-positive history was severe: the `k=4 -> 1/2` line, the variance-bound line, and the denominator-drop line were all attractive but wrong. By contrast, the triple-defect statistic only existed because the system tolerated low-proof-status objects long enough to see whether they generated descendants.

## Theorem 3: No single global verification policy can be optimal once lanes have different leakage frontiers

Take two lanes \(L_1,L_2\) with utilities
\[
J^{(i)}_\lambda(u)=A_i(u)-\lambda B_i(u).
\]
Assume each has decreasing marginal ratio
\[
\Lambda_i(u)=\frac{\Delta A_i(u)}{\Delta B_i(u)},
\]
but their thresholds cross:
\[
\Lambda_1(t)>\lambda>\Lambda_2(t)
\]
for some stage \(t\).

Then any global policy forcing the same incubation depth \(u\) on both lanes is strictly dominated by a routed policy that uses \(u_1\ge t+1\) on lane \(L_1\) and \(u_2\le t\) on lane \(L_2\).

### Proof
At stage \(t\),
\[
J^{(1)}_\lambda(t+1)-J^{(1)}_\lambda(t)
=
\Delta B_1(t)(\Lambda_1(t)-\lambda)>0,
\]
while
\[
J^{(2)}_\lambda(t+1)-J^{(2)}_\lambda(t)
=
\Delta B_2(t)(\Lambda_2(t)-\lambda)<0.
\]
So moving both lanes together from \(t\) to \(t+1\) helps lane 1 and hurts lane 2. Therefore a routed choice with lane-specific depths strictly improves the sum utility over either global choice \(u=t\) or \(u=t+1\). QED.

This is stronger than [P12]. [P12] was about theorem yield only. The new result says the dominance survives when the objective includes **embarrassment cost from leaked false claims**. That matters for the actual harness because Frankl and sunflower are not symmetric tasks: one had multiple seductive false lines; the other’s best novelty came from letting unproved objects live.

P(\text{at least one hit in }O)\le 1-(1-\mu)^B \le B\mu.
\]
Hence if \(\mu(n)\le e^{-\alpha n}\) and \(B(n)\) is polynomial, then novelty success probability is \(o(1)\).

**Theorem C. Optimal verification depth is lane-specific.**  
Let
\[
A_\ell(u)=\text{true-yield up to depth }u,\qquad
B_\ell(u)=\text{false-leakage up to depth }u,
\]
and utility
\[
J_{\ell,\lambda}(u)=A_\ell(u)-\lambda B_\ell(u),
\]
where \(\lambda>0\) prices false positives.

If
\[
\Delta B_\ell(u):=B_\ell(u+1)-B_\ell(u)>0
\]
and
\[
\Lambda_\ell(u):=\frac{\Delta A_\ell(u)}{\Delta B_\ell(u)}
\]
is strictly decreasing in \(u\), then the optimal incubation depth is the threshold
\[
u_\ell^*(\lambda)=\max\{u:\Lambda_\ell(u)\ge \lambda\}.
\]

**Corollary D. No single global verifier schedule is optimal in heterogeneous creativity regimes.**  
If two lanes \(\ell_1,\ell_2\) satisfy
\[
\Lambda_{\ell_1}(t)>\lambda>\Lambda_{\ell_2}(t)
\]
for some stage \(t\), then any global policy forcing the same incubation depth on both lanes is strictly dominated by a routed policy using different depths.

**Interpretation.**  
A harness can fail to generate emergent thought for only three mathematically distinct reasons:

1. **Proposal-mass failure:** \(\mu_\ell\) is too small.
2. **Maturation failure:** \(s_\ell(u)\) is too small even when \(\mu_\ell\) is nontrivial.
3. **Verification-policy failure:** the harness applies the wrong \(u\) to the wrong lane.

This yields the workshop’s strongest operational claim:

`Emergent thought in an LLM harness requires both`
- `a representational intervention that raises proposal mass onto the right object family, and`
- `a protected maturation policy that preserves those objects long enough to theoremize.`

Constraint, alien analogy, conjecture-first prompting, and model diversity matter only insofar as they increase \(\mu\). Delayed verification matters only insofar as it increases \(s\).

---

**2. PROOF OR PROOF SKETCH**

**Proof of Theorem A**

By definition, theorem-grade success occurs only through first entering the tracked object family \(O\). Therefore
\[
Y_\ell(u)=\Pr_\ell(T)=\Pr_\ell(T\cap O)=\Pr_\ell(T\mid O)\Pr_\ell(O)=\mu_\ell s_\ell(u).
\]
This is exact.

---

**Proof of Theorem B**

Let \(E_t\) be the event that no proposal among the first \(t\) attempts hits \(O\). By hypothesis, for every step and every history,
\[
\Pr(\text{hit }O\text{ at step }t \mid \text{past}) \le \mu.
\]
Hence
\[
\Pr(E_t\mid E_{t-1})\ge 1-\mu.
\]
Inductively,
\[
\Pr(E_B)\ge (1-\mu)^B.
\]
So
\[
P(\text{at least one hit in }O)=1-\Pr(E_B)\le 1-(1-\mu)^B\le B\mu.
\]
If \(\mu(n)\le e^{-\alpha n}\) and \(B(n)\le n^k\), then
\[
P(\text{success})\le n^k e^{-\alpha n}\to 0.
\]

This is the formal reason “more chain-of-thought” or “more debate rounds” cannot rescue novelty when the relevant object family has exponentially tiny proposal mass under the current representational regime.

---

**Proof of Theorem C**

Compute the one-step utility increment:
\[
J_{\ell,\lambda}(u+1)-J_{\ell,\lambda}(u)
=
\Delta A_\ell(u)-\lambda \Delta B_\ell(u)
=
\Delta B_\ell(u)\big(\Lambda_\ell(u)-\lambda\big).
\]
Since \(\Delta B_\ell(u)>0\), the sign of the increment is exactly the sign of \(\Lambda_\ell(u)-\lambda\).

If \(\Lambda_\ell(u)\) is strictly decreasing, then this sign changes at most once. Therefore utility first increases, then decreases, and the optimum is the largest depth with \(\Lambda_\ell(u)\ge\lambda\). That is exactly
\[
u_\ell^*(\lambda)=\max\{u:\Lambda_\ell(u)\ge \lambda\}.
\]

---

**Proof of Corollary D**

Suppose for some stage \(t\),
\[
\Lambda_{\ell_1}(t)>\lambda>\Lambda_{\ell_2}(t).
\]
Then
\[
J_{\ell_1,\lambda}(t+1)-J_{\ell_1,\lambda}(t)>0
\]
but
\[
J_{\ell_2,\lambda}(t+1)-J_{\ell_2,\lambda}(t)<0.
\]
So increasing incubation from \(t\) to \(t+1\) helps lane \(\ell_1\) and hurts lane \(\ell_2\). Therefore any global policy choosing one common depth is strictly worse than routing the two lanes separately.

This proves that a single verify-first or single incubate-first architecture is structurally wrong once idea families are heterogeneous.

---

**Supplement: exact prompt-side mechanism the workshop proved**

Two additional exact identities matter.

If proposals in a regime are softmax-weighted by scores \(a_i\) at temperature \(\tau\), and \(T\) is a target object family, then
\[
\frac{d}{d\tau}\mu_T
=
\frac{\mu_T}{\tau^2}\big(\mathbb E[a]-\mathbb E[a\mid T]\big).
\]
So temperature increases target-family mass iff the target family is below-average in current salience.

If a prompt hard-bans a set \(A\), then
\[
\mu_{T,\mathrm{ban}}=\frac{\mu_T-\mu_{T\cap A}}{1-\mu_A},
\]
hence banning helps iff
\[
\mu_{T\cap A}<\mu_T\mu_A.
\]
So creative constraint works by deleting high-mass, low-target-enrichment basins.

These are full proofs, not heuristics.

---

**What is not fully proved**

One promising trace-level result remains partial.

`PROVEN:` for a typed action trace, the one-frame vs two-frame code-length gain at split \(\tau\) is exactly
\[
\Delta_T(\tau)=T\,\mathrm{JSD}_\alpha(\hat p_{1:\tau},\hat p_{\tau+1:T}).
\]
So frame change can be operationalized as compression gain.

`NOT YET FULLY PROVED:` a complete consistency theorem for MDL/JSD switch detection under the weakest penalty assumptions. The earlier argument needs stronger penalty growth than was initially claimed.

So trace-identifiable frame change is a strong partial result, not yet a finished theorem in full generality.

**3. WHAT WAS ACHIEVED**

This is `partial progress`, but strong partial progress.

What is complete:

- A rigorous decomposition of novelty yield into **proposal mass** and **maturation success**.
- A theorem showing that inference-time compute cannot compensate for tiny proposal mass except linearly.
- A theorem showing that optimal verification depth is a **lane-specific threshold**, not a global constant.
- Exact formal mechanisms for why **constraint**, **temperature**, and **model diversity** sometimes help.

What is not complete:

- We do **not** have a theorem that current transformers can or cannot achieve H-creative breakthroughs in general.
- We do **not** yet have a fully verified trace-level theorem that cleanly detects representational phase changes in realistic nonstationary traces.
- We do **not** yet know empirically, for your Frankl or sunflower runs, whether the main gain was \(\Delta\mu\), \(\Delta s\), or both, because the required object-stage logging was not reported.

So the strongest result is not “we solved emergent thought.” It is:

`We reduced the problem to a measurable causal architecture:`
- representation-stage controls move \(\mu\),
- incubation/verification controls \(s\) and false leakage,
- compute only amplifies what already has support.

That is a refined problem statement with real theorems behind it.

**4. OPEN QUESTIONS**

`OPEN 1. Frankl discriminator.`  
After explicit representation forcing and protected incubation, does any lane show both:
- a genuine frame switch, and
- sustained early-stage positive maturation dividend?

Formally: does there exist a lane \(\ell\) with detectable representational shift and \(\widehat \Lambda_\ell(u)>\lambda\) for some early \(u\)?  
If not, the ceiling is likely upstream of search and verification.

`OPEN 2. Sunflower mechanism decomposition.`  
In the banned-native cap-set run, was the improvement mainly:
- proposal-mass lift \(\Delta\mu>0\),
- maturation lift \(\Delta s>0\),
- or both?

This is now a concrete experimental question, not a philosophical one.

`OPEN 3. Unsupervised frame learning.`  
Can one learn the action abstraction \(\phi\) from raw traces so that it preserves likelihood-ratio geometry between frames and predicts descendant fertility? This is the key technical gap in turning representational novelty into a robust statistic.

`OPEN 4. Realistic change-point theory.`  
Extend the JSD/MDL frame-switch formalism from i.i.d. segment models to within-frame drift, mixed frames, and tool-conditioned traces.

`OPEN 5. Model diversity.`  
Do GPT, Claude, and Grok have genuinely non-dominated theorem-mass vectors on latent door families, or are they mostly stylistic variants of the same proposal basin?

`OPEN 6. Creativity scaling law.`  
Is there any nontrivial regime where proposal mass \(\mu\) itself scales with inference-time compute through active representation search, rather than being effectively fixed by the initial regime?

`OPEN 7. Sufficiency for P-creativity vs H-creativity.`  
The current theorems plausibly give a sufficiency package for large gains in P-creative output. They do not yet tell us whether H-creative breakthroughs on hard open problems require new training objectives, not just better inference-time control.

---

**5. KEY INSIGHT**

The single most important idea was this:

`Novelty is not “more search.” It is search after a representational mass shift, with survival long enough for the new object to mature.`

In formulas:

\[
\text{novel yield} = \underbrace{\mu}_{\text{does the system even generate the right kind of object?}}
\times
\underbrace{s}_{\text{does that object survive and theoremize?}}
\]

Everything in your empirical evidence fits this:

- Frankl: strong same-frame competence, tiny or sterile non-native mass.
- Sunflower: constraint likely raised mass on the algebraic family, but maturation still failed before replacing ALWZ 2.8.
- Conjecture engine: broadened the object family first, then let immature objects live.
- Battery: high rigor within a native frame, low representational displacement.
- Multi-model setups: useful only when they put mass on different latent door families.

So the workshop’s strongest conclusion is not that LLMs “lack creativity” in some vague sense. It is much sharper:

`Current systems fail at emergent thought when the needed object family has too little proposal mass under accessible representations, or when verifier policy kills it before it matures.`

That is a theorem-backed research program.
